## Executive summary

This is a fact-rich, structurally sound draft that already separates interpretation from advice and ends on a genuine open question. The highest-impact optimizations are technical: shorten the meta description, replace the internal-link placeholder with real links, and add an early definition plus a concise quotable paragraph so AI answer engines can pull from it. A declared-bias note and a short known/unknown/unknowable section will keep it fully consistent with the Layer 2 editorial posture.

## Recommended changes

1. **H1 rewrite**
   - **Location:** Front matter `title` and the `#` heading in the body.
   - **Issue:** Current H1 is accurate but not search-query-aligned.
   - **Fix:** Change to **“Why the U.S. Treasury Curve Is Steepening in July 2026 — And What Fed-ECB-BoJ Divergence Has to Do With It”** and update the schema `headline` to match.
   - **Priority:** High

2. **Meta description**
   - **Location:** Front matter `description` and schema `description`.
   - **Issue:** 219 characters; will be truncated in SERPs.
   - **Fix:** Replace with: *“The U.S. Treasury curve un-inverted in July 2026 even as jobs softened. Term premium, fiscal supply, and Fed-ECB-BoJ divergence are pulling long yields higher.”* (159 characters)
   - **Priority:** Critical

3. **URL slug**
   - **Location:** Front matter `slug`.
   - **Issue:** Slug is clean but misses key topic words.
   - **Fix:** Change to `treasury-yield-curve-steepening-fed-divergence-july-2026`.
   - **Priority:** Medium

4. **Internal links**
   - **Location:** HTML comment block immediately after the opening.
   - **Issue:** Placeholder only; no actual links.
   - **Fix:** Replace with real Aagman links (or stubs to be filled once URLs are confirmed). Suggested anchor text and targets:
     - [how the yield curve decomposes into expected rates, inflation, and term premium]
     - [the difference between a bear steepener and a bull steepener]
     - [what the Fed’s Summary of Economic Projections actually measures]
     - [how foreign demand for Treasuries shapes U.S. rates]
     - [reading the U.S. jobs report for signal versus noise]
   - **Priority:** High

5. **Heading hierarchy: H3s for mechanism and interpretation subsections**
   - **Location:** “Mechanism: how the curve actually works” and “Interpretation spectrum: four plausible readings.”
   - **Issue:** Subsections are bold paragraphs, skipping H3.
   - **Fix:** Convert the four mechanism drivers and the four interpretations (A–D) into H3s. Keep their current wording where it preserves voice.
   - **Priority:** High

6. **Question-form H3s**
   - **Location:** Signal and Mechanism sections.
   - **Issue:** H2s are not phrased as natural questions.
   - **Fix:** Add H3s such as:
     - *“Why is the curve steepening when the jobs report was weak?”*
     - *“What is term premium and why is it rising?”*
     - *“Is the steepening curve signaling recession?”*
   - **Priority:** Medium

7. **Early definition sentence**
   - **Location:** End of the “Opening” section, before “Context.”
   - **Issue:** AEO definition pattern is missing.
   - **Fix:** Add: *“Yield curve steepening happens when the gap between long- and short-term Treasury yields widens; when the long end leads the move, it is called a bear steepener.”*
   - **Priority:** High

8. **Concise quotable summary paragraph**
   - **Location:** After the opening, before the first table, or as the first paragraph under “Signal.”
   - **Issue:** No 40–60-word paragraph that answer engines can quote cleanly.
   - **Fix:** Add: *“The 10-year minus 2-year Treasury spread turned positive in early July 2026 because long yields rose faster than short yields. That is a bear steepener: the market is repricing term premium and fiscal supply, not celebrating growth. It is unusual because it happened alongside a soft jobs report and a Fed on hold.”* (~49 words)
   - **Priority:** Medium

9. **Declared structural-bias note**
   - **Location:** Early in the “Context” section or as a short editor’s note after the opening.
   - **Issue:** Aagman’s explicit long-cycle biases are not flagged.
   - **Fix:** Add one sentence such as: *“Aagman operates with a declared structural bias that the long-term interest-rate cycle has bottomed; this piece uses that bias as a lens, not as a conclusion.”*
   - **Priority:** High

10. **Known / Unknown / Unknowable section**
    - **Location:** After “Interpretation spectrum: four plausible readings,” before “Implications for decision-makers.”
    - **Issue:** Knowns, unknowns, and unknowables are implicit but not formally separated.
    - **Fix:** Add a short subsection with three bullet groups (or a table) summarizing what is known, what is not yet known, and what is structurally unknowable. Pull from section 8 of the research artifact.
    - **Priority:** Medium

11. **Visual chart additions**
    - **Location:** “Context” or “Mechanism” section.
    - **Issue:** Tables are strong, but a visual chart would improve ranking potential and shareability.
    - **Fix:** Add an annotated chart showing the U.S. Treasury yield curve on 2 July 2026 versus the prior inverted curve, with source and timeframe labeled. If feasible, add a term-premium decomposition chart.
    - **Priority:** Quick Win

## What to leave alone

- **Opening restaurant analogy.** It fits the mandatory Layer 2 opening convention and stays within the 100–150 word target.
- **Data tables and the source list.** They are already credible, well-labeled, and useful for both readers and search engines.
- **Four-interpretation structure.** It is the core intellectual device of the piece and should not be collapsed into a single narrative.
- **Cross-asset implications and the “what this does not affect” guardrails.** They keep the piece out of advice territory.
- **Open-question ending.** It preserves the required cognitive open-loop.
- **Verified numbers and dates.** Do not change any factual figure for SEO purposes.
