# Substack Overlay

Layer 2 long-form essays have room for nuance. Use it.

## Default structure

1. **Opening analogy (100–150 words)**
   - Everyday, conversational. Like explaining over coffee.
   - Makes the reader feel at home before the structural analysis begins.

2. **Context**
   - What changed?
   - Why is this being discussed now?

3. **Mechanism**
   - How does this actually work in markets?
   - What constraint, flow, or rule is involved?

4. **Signal / deviation**
   - What is unusual relative to history, structure, or expectations?

5. **Interpretation spectrum**
   - Multiple plausible readings.
   - Explicitly state uncertainty.
   - Avoid collapsing into one narrative.

6. **Historical or structural memory**
   - A past analog or recurring pattern.
   - Used to frame, not forecast.

7. **Implications for decision-makers**
   - What this affects.
   - What it does not affect.
   - Never: what to buy or sell.

8. **Open question**
   - Leave the reader with a question, not a conclusion.

## Substack-specific rules

- One idea per paragraph.
- Short sentences when discussing risk.
- No stacking of abstractions.
- Claims must be supported with charts, tables, or historical comparisons.
- Every chart labels source, timeframe, units, and adjustments (nominal vs real).
- Primary sources preferred: central banks, exchanges, sovereign data, reputable institutions.
- Charts clarify, not decorate. If a chart does not add insight, remove it.

## Pre-publish checklist

- Does this increase reader clarity?
- Does this respect uncertainty?
- Does this avoid implicit advice?
- Would this age well in 12–24 months?
- Would we stand by this during a drawdown?
- Are bias and opinion clearly labeled?

If any answer is "no" → revise.
