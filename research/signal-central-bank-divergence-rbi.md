## 1. Signal restatement

In mid-June 2026 the global monetary-policy anchor fragmented. The Federal Reserve held rates but shifted its median dot toward a possible 2026 hike; the European Central Bank hiked into a downgraded growth outlook driven by the Middle East energy shock; and the Reserve Bank of Australia paused after three rapid hikes. Against that backdrop, the Reserve Bank of India kept the repo rate at 5.25% and chose not to tighten into a slowing economy. Instead, it opened a capital-account toolbox — expanding the Fully Accessible Route for government securities, raising NRI/OCI equity limits, offering concessional forex swaps for PSU external commercial borrowings, absorbing the hedging cost on fresh FCNR(B) deposits, and pairing these with a government ordinance exempting foreign portfolio investors from tax on G-Sec interest and capital gains. The combined signal is that as the developed-world policy anchor dissolves, India is separating domestic monetary policy from external-sector management, using regulatory and capital-account instruments to absorb dollar pressure while preserving rate-setting optionality.

## 4. Mechanism

The signal works through the separation of the global policy anchor from India’s domestic policy setting.

**Developed-market divergence removes the single global anchor.** For most of the post-2008 period, major central banks moved broadly together: easing into crises, tightening into recoveries, pausing when uncertain. In June 2026 that coordination broke. The Fed is hawkishly on hold with a median dot implying a possible hike; the ECB hiked once into a growth downgrade; and the RBA paused after a rapid re-tightening. Each bank is responding to a different inflation-growth mix: the Fed to resilient U.S. demand and sticky services prices, the ECB to an energy shock that is already reversing, and the RBA to domestic demand that may be cracking. The result is that there is no longer a single global rate path for markets to price against. Capital must make relative judgments — dollar versus euro, euro versus Aussie, U.S. front end versus long end — and those judgments feed back into currency and yield differentials.

**The dollar benefits from the relative path.** Currency markets price expected future short rates, not just current levels. When the Fed’s median dot rose from 3.4% to 3.8% while the ECB and RBA offered no firm commitment to further hikes, the expected return on dollar cash rose relative to other currencies. The DXY broke above 100 and EUR/USD fell below 1.15 even though the ECB had actually raised rates. That is the key FX principle at work: currencies move on the *forward path*, not the current change.

**India absorbs the spillover through the capital account rather than the policy rate.** A stronger dollar and higher U.S. front-end yields normally tighten global financial conditions and pressure EM currencies. The textbook response is to raise domestic rates to retain capital. The RBI rejected that path because it judges the inflation impulse as supply-side — fuel pass-through, monsoon risk, global energy prices — and because growth is already slowing. Raising the repo rate would raise borrowing costs for households and firms without directly lowering oil prices. Cutting rates, by contrast, would risk de-anchoring inflation expectations. The neutral stance preserves optionality.

To manage the external account without moving the policy rate, the RBI has deployed five capital-account and regulatory levers. The FAR expansion, FPI limit relaxation and G-Sec tax exemption widen the supply of rupee assets foreigners can hold, targeting the stock and flow demand for Indian bonds. The PSU ECB and FCNR(B) swap facilities subsidise the hedging cost of foreign-currency borrowing, directly adding dollar liabilities without raising domestic rates. The export-proceeds measure accelerates the repatriation of dollars already earned. Together, these measures are designed to ease pressure on the rupee and on the RBI’s forward book without draining spot reserves.

**The rupee is managed for volatility, not a level.** Governor Malhotra reiterated that the RBI does not target a specific exchange-rate band; it intervenes to curb excessive volatility and disorderly movements. The capital-account measures therefore serve two purposes: they attract dollars, and they reduce the need for aggressive spot or forward intervention that would either deplete reserves or tighten rupee liquidity.

**Liquidity is a secondary transmission channel.** The RBI noted that banking-system liquidity had tightened as FX intervention and seasonal currency leakage offset earlier policy easing. Dollar inflows under the new measures, if they materialise, would reduce the need for forward-market intervention and add rupee liquidity to the banking system. This is how the external and domestic halves of the policy connect: capital-account tools stabilise the rupee, and a stable rupee supports domestic financial conditions even while the repo rate stays on hold.

## 5. Competing interpretations

### Interpretation A: India is preserving optionality while developed-market central banks are trapped in harder trade-offs.

**Who is making it:** RBI leadership, Indian sell-side economists and fund managers quoted after the policy, and commentators who stress India’s stronger external position relative to 2013.  
**Evidence supporting it:** The RBI held rates while cutting the growth forecast and raising the inflation forecast, explicitly framing the shock as supply-side. The capital-account measures target the external account without choking domestic demand. India’s forex reserves are $682.3 billion, the current account deficit is narrower than in past stress episodes, and gross FDI hit a record $94.5 billion in FY26. The rupee rallied post-policy and continued to strengthen as oil prices fell.  
**Evidence contradicting it:** The Q3 inflation projection of 5.9% is only 10 basis points below the 6% upper tolerance band; if food or fuel prices spike, the MPC may still have to hike. FII equity selling has continued despite the measures, and the rupee remains materially weaker than a year ago.  
**Why it might be wrong:** It assumes the supply shock fades by Q4 as the RBI projects and that the Fed does not follow through on its hawkish dots. If the dollar keeps strengthening or oil re-spikes, the capital-account toolbox may prove insufficient, and the RBI could be forced to hike from a position of having already used other ammunition.

### Interpretation B: The RBI is behind the curve; capital-account tools are no substitute for rate defence if the dollar keeps rising.

**Who is making it:** Market commentary that expected at least a hawkish tilt or 25bp hike, and analysts comparing India to Asian central banks that have already hiked to defend their currencies.  
**Evidence supporting it:** The rupee had fallen from roughly ₹90 to a record near ₹97 in about a year, and net FPI equity outflows in FY27 so far totalled $13.7 billion. A hold at 5.25% with inflation rising means the real policy rate is falling. The Fed’s dot plot and dollar strength are external forces that capital-account measures cannot fully offset.  
**Evidence contradicting it:** The rupee rallied post-policy and continued to strengthen to 94.39 by 25 June. The scale of announced inflow measures — FCNR(B) alone is estimated by some analysts at $40–50 billion potential — suggests the capital-account channel can stabilise the external account. Forex reserves and import cover are far stronger than in 2013.  
**Why it might be wrong:** If global risk appetite turns sharply negative or the Fed hikes into a disinflationary oil unwind, the capital-account measures may look prescient. The RBI’s calculus is that rate hikes would hurt growth without fixing the external problem; if the external problem resolves itself through lower oil and inflow measures, rate hikes would have been an unnecessary drag.

### Interpretation C: The developed-market divergence is a temporary energy-shock distortion; the durable story is India’s capital-account opening.

**Who is making it:** Fixed-income strategists and bond-market participants who focus on the FPI tax exemption, FAR expansion and G-Sec index inclusion.  
**Evidence supporting it:** The government’s tax exemption applies specifically to G-Secs, not corporate bonds, and is clearly designed to deepen the sovereign bond market and improve India’s position in global bond indices. FPI holdings in FAR securities rose from ₹3.23 lakh crore to ₹3.58 lakh crore within weeks. The FCNR(B) and ECB swaps also inject rupee liquidity into the banking system.  
**Evidence contradicting it:** The Governor framed the measures explicitly as strengthening the balance of payments and supporting the rupee amid external pressures. The timing — during a period of rupee weakness and FPI equity outflows — suggests currency management is at least a co-objective.  
**Why it might be wrong:** It underweights the external-account motivation. While bond-market depth is a beneficiary, the immediate trigger was the current-account and currency pressure from the West Asia conflict. If the currency motive fades, some of the measures may be allowed to expire as scheduled.

### Interpretation D: This is the early evidence of a multi-polar monetary order in which each major economy must build its own capital-account firewall.

**Who is making it:** Structural macro commentators who argue that the post-2008 convergence of central-bank behaviour was the exception, not the rule.  
**Evidence supporting it:** The Fed, ECB and RBA are now on visibly different paths. India is responding not by aligning with any of them but by using capital-account tools tailored to its own vulnerability — dollar dependence, oil imports, and FPI volatility. The measures are unilateral and regulatory rather than rate-based.  
**Evidence contradicting it:** India still operates within a dollar-centric system. The RBI’s measures are designed to attract dollars, not to reduce dollar dependence. A multi-polar order would imply deeper local-currency settlement and reduced reliance on U.S. monetary policy, neither of which is happening here.  
**Why it might be wrong:** It over-reads one policy episode into a structural regime change. The RBI is managing a temporary shock with temporary tools. A true multi-polar order would require changes to trade invoicing, reserve accumulation patterns and currency swap networks that are not yet visible.

## 6. Cross-asset implications

**FX.** The dollar is the clearest beneficiary of developed-market divergence because the Fed’s forward path shifted higher while the ECB and RBA are perceived as closer to terminal. The DXY broke above 100 and EUR/USD fell below 1.15. For India, the rupee has stabilised and strengthened to 94.39/USD by 25 June, supported by RBI inflow measures, falling crude prices and DII equity support. The near-term range is likely to be driven by the Fed’s dot plot, oil prices and FPI equity flows rather than by RBI rate changes. The RBI’s emphasis on volatility management rather than a level means the currency can still depreciate gradually if fundamentals warrant it.

**Rates.** The U.S. Treasury curve experienced a twist flattening: front-end yields rose on hawkish Fed dots while long-end yields fell on the Iran deal. The 2s30s spread compressed roughly 17 basis points. In India, the front end is anchored by the 5.25% repo hold, but the long end is sensitive to FPI demand under FAR and the government tax exemption. Corporate bond yields fell more than G-Secs immediately after the policy, reflecting relief on domestic supply pressure as PSUs gain access to cheaper ECBs. If inflation prints near the RBI’s Q3 projection, expectations of a future hike could push short-end yields higher.

**Equities.** U.S. equities fell on the hawkish surprise, with the S&P 500 down roughly 0.6% to 1.2% and the Nasdaq underperforming on rate-sensitive growth names. The rotation was defensive: Dow outperformed Nasdaq, value outperformed growth. In India, rate-sensitive sectors (banks, NBFCs, autos, real estate) face a stable funding-cost environment but no fresh rate-cut tailwind. Banks with strong NRI franchises and those able to mobilise FCNR(B) deposits may see funding advantages. The broader market has been supported by DII buying offsetting FII selling; the durability of that pattern depends on domestic flows remaining robust.

**Commodities.** Brent crude collapsed from roughly $140 in April to below $73 by late June as the Hormuz reopening accelerated. For India, each $10/bbl fall reduces the import bill and eases both the current account deficit and inflation. Gold sold off because higher real U.S. yields raise the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding bullion, and a stronger dollar weakens commodity prices globally. Spot gold fell toward $4,155–$4,220 in the days after the Fed. The rupee’s stabilisation also reduces the local-currency urgency for gold buying.

**EM credit and India credit.** A stronger dollar and higher U.S. front-end yields typically tighten global financial conditions. Dollar-denominated debt servicing becomes more expensive, and capital flows toward U.S. assets. For India, PSUs and large corporates with access to ECB markets benefit from cheaper foreign-currency borrowing costs under the RBI’s swap facilities. Smaller firms without that access remain dependent on domestic bank credit, where liquidity has tightened and transmission of past rate cuts is still incomplete. The degree of stress depends on whether the dollar move is driven by growth differentials (manageable) or a pure rates re-pricing into a slowing world (more dangerous).

**Derivatives.** India VIX below 13 suggests near-term volatility expectations have moderated, but weekly derivatives expiries and FII positioning remain live variables. The rupee forward book and RBI swaps are the key institutional instruments to watch. In the U.S., the curve-flattening trade reflects a market that is pricing a hawkish front end but not a recessionary long end.

## 7. Historical analogues or structural memory

### Analogue 1: The 2013 FCNR(B) swap during the taper tantrum.

**The episode:** In September 2013, then-RBI Governor Raghuram Rajan opened a concessional FCNR(B) swap window to attract NRI dollar deposits during a sharp rupee depreciation. Banks mobilised approximately $26–34 billion, helping stabilise the rupee after it had fallen from around ₹55 to ₹68 per dollar.

**Key similarity:** The RBI is again using a subsidised swap window for NRI deposits to attract dollar inflows without raising domestic interest rates, and it has again paired it with CRR/SLR exemptions to improve bank economics.

**Key difference:** In 2013, U.S. rates were near zero and the spread between FCNR(B) rates and U.S. alternatives was over 500 basis points; in 2026, U.S. rates are around 4.5% and the spread is roughly 150–250 basis points, making the deposit decision less of a no-brainer. India’s macro fundamentals are also stronger now: forex reserves are much larger, the current account deficit is smaller, and the banking system is healthier.

**Why it is useful for perspective, not prediction:** It shows that NRI deposit windows can materially help the external account, but the scale of inflows depends on the relative yield spread and global risk appetite, not just the RBI’s willingness to offer swaps.

### Analogue 2: The 2015 Iran nuclear deal (JCPOA) and the oil-price collapse.

**The episode:** The 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action lifted some sanctions on Iran in exchange for nuclear limits. Iranian oil returned to market and Brent crude fell sharply over the following months, though other supply factors were also at play.

**Key similarity:** A U.S.–Iran agreement is again tied to sanctions relief and a return of Iranian oil; the June 2026 MoU explicitly references reopening the Strait of Hormuz and allowing Iran to resume oil exports. The resulting oil-price fall has eased the inflation rationale for further hikes by the Fed, ECB and RBA.

**Key difference:** The 2026 agreement is an interim memorandum, not a finalized multilateral treaty; the war has damaged regional energy infrastructure; Gulf producers are ramping up supply quickly; and the 2026 deal includes far broader potential sanctions relief, including frozen assets and a $300 billion reconstruction program.

**Why it is useful for perspective, not prediction:** It shows how quickly a geopolitical risk premium can deflate when supply returns, and how central-bank hiking rationale can evaporate with it. It also shows that implementation risk — mines in Hormuz, Israeli objections, U.S. domestic politics — can keep a residual premium in prices for months.

## 8. What is known, unknown, and unknowable

### Known

- The Fed, ECB and RBA delivered a divergent policy mix in mid-June 2026: the Fed hawkishly on hold, the ECB hiking into weak growth, and the RBA pausing after three hikes.
- The RBI held the repo rate at 5.25% and retained a neutral stance on 5 June 2026, with a unanimous 6–0 vote.
- The RBI cut its FY27 GDP growth forecast to 6.6% and raised its FY27 CPI inflation forecast to 5.1% (Q3: 5.9%).
- Five specific dollar-inflow measures were announced, plus a government ordinance exempting FPIs from tax on G-Sec interest and capital gains.
- Forex reserves stood at $682.3 billion as of 29 May 2026; net FPI outflows in FY27 so far were $13.7 billion.
- The rupee strengthened to 94.39/USD by 25 June and India VIX fell below 13, as Brent crude dropped to around $73/bbl.
- Oil prices have collapsed from April peaks on the U.S.–Iran MoU.

### Unknown

- Whether the U.S.–Iran MoU will hold through its 60-day negotiating window.
- Whether the Fed will actually hike in 2026 or whether falling oil will keep it on hold.
- Whether the ECB will deliver a follow-up hike in September or pause after June.
- How much dollar inflow the FCNR(B) swap and PSU ECB facilities will actually generate. Analyst estimates range from $40 billion to $50 billion, but actual mobilisation depends on bank pricing and NRI demand.
- Whether Q3 FY27 inflation will print near the RBI’s 5.9% projection or overshoot the 6% upper tolerance band.
- Whether FPI equity selling will continue, stabilise or reverse in the second half of 2026.

### Unknowable

- The exact path of oil prices, which depend on war dynamics, mine-clearing logistics, and political decisions in Washington, Tehran and Jerusalem.
- The lagged effect of the 2025–2026 tightening cycle on household balance sheets and corporate earnings.
- Whether Chair Warsh’s framework review will alter how the Fed measures inflation or communicates policy.
- The structural level of the neutral real interest rate in a world of AI investment, defense spending, and de-globalization.
- Whether household and wage inflation expectations in India will remain anchored if headline inflation stays above 5% for several quarters.

## 9. Open questions

1. **Will the Fed follow the dot plot or will the dot plot follow oil?** If Brent stays near $70–$75, the Fed’s 3.6% PCE forecast for 2026 may prove too high, undermining the case for a hike and softening the dollar pressure on India.

2. **Can the RBI’s capital-account toolbox attract enough dollars to offset FPI equity outflows and a wider current account deficit?** The structure is in place, but the relative yield spread over U.S. alternatives is much smaller than in 2013, and the window is only four months long.

3. **If Q3 inflation prints close to or above 6%, can the RBI really avoid hiking?** The neutral stance keeps the option open, but a hike into a 6.3% Q2 growth print would test the RBI’s growth-support credentials.

4. **Is the ECB’s June hike the start of a mini-cycle or a one-off credibility move?** The answer hinges on whether services inflation continues to broaden and whether the Hormuz reopening is durable.

5. **What is the hidden cost on the RBI’s balance sheet of absorbing FCNR(B) hedging costs and running a large forward book?** The swap window transfers currency risk from banks to the central bank; if the rupee depreciates sharply over 3–5 years, the contingent liability could be material.
