# Signal Research — copper-gold-divergence

## 1. Signal restatement

Gold and copper are both trading at historically elevated levels in mid-2026, but the forces supporting each metal are structurally different. Gold is being driven by monetary and geopolitical demand: central bank accumulation, fiscal-deficit concerns, de-dollarization preferences, and safe-haven flows. Copper is being driven by physical supply tightness—mine disruptions in Chile, Indonesia and the DRC, negative treatment and refining charges, sulfuric-acid shortages, and energy-transition demand—even as broader industrial activity in China and Europe remains subdued. The signal is that the "commodities rally" narrative conflates two distinct macro stories: one about confidence in the monetary system, the other about the physical constraints on electrification and reindustrialization.

## 4. Mechanism

Gold and copper occupy different positions in the global economic and financial architecture, and that is why they can rise or fall for different reasons at the same time.

**Gold is a monetary asset.** Its price is set primarily at the margin by investment demand, central bank reserve policy, and expectations about real interest rates, fiscal sustainability, currency stability and geopolitical risk. Supply is relatively inelastic in the short run—mine production and recycling respond only slowly to price—so shifts in investment and official-sector demand can move the price sharply. In the current episode, central banks have become a persistent, largely price-insensitive buyer class, motivated by reserve diversification and concerns about sanctions and dollar-asset vulnerability. This creates a structural bid that is independent of industrial activity.

**Copper is an industrial input.** Its price is set by the physical balance of mine output, concentrate availability, smelter capacity, scrap supply and manufacturing demand. Because copper is essential to electrification—power grids, electric vehicles, renewable generation, data centers—long-run demand growth is tied to energy-transition and digital-infrastructure capital spending. But the supply side is also constrained: ore grades are declining, major projects take years to permit and build, and operations are vulnerable to accidents, weather, sulfuric-acid availability and political risk. When several large mines are disrupted simultaneously, the concentrate market tightens, TC/RCs collapse, and prices rise even if broader manufacturing demand is soft.

**The divergence arises because the marginal buyers and constraints are not the same.** Gold responds to questions about the monetary system; copper responds to questions about the physical availability of a critical industrial metal. A world of high fiscal deficits and geopolitical hedging supports gold. A world of concentrate shortages and electrification demand supports copper. The two can coexist, but they do not have to move together—and in 2026 they are sending different signals about which risks the market is pricing.

## 5. Competing interpretations

### Interpretation A: Both metals are pricing geopolitical and supply-chain fragmentation
- **Who makes it:** Strategists at BrainWorks and commodity bulls who treat copper as increasingly "strategic" rather than purely cyclical.
- **Evidence supporting it:** Copper's use in defense, grids, data centers and EVs; U.S. Section 232 tariffs; COMEX–LME spread; sulfuric-acid shortages linked to the Middle East conflict; central bank gold buying after the freezing of Russian reserves.
- **Evidence contradicting it:** Copper is still in surplus according to ICSG and Goldman Sachs; gold has corrected sharply from its January 2026 peak; industrial demand in China and Europe is subdued.
- **Why it might be wrong:** It lumps two metals with very different demand elasticities and buyer bases into one "deglobalization" trade, and it may overstate how much copper demand is truly non-cyclical in the near term.

### Interpretation B: Copper is overpricing supply tightness while gold is repricing monetary risk
- **Who makes it:** Analysts at DIY Investor / Jefferies and Goldman Sachs, who note copper was in surplus in 2025 and faces tariff-driven demand headwinds, while gold has structural monetary support.
- **Evidence supporting it:** ICSG projects a 96,000-tonne copper surplus in 2026 and a 377,000-tonne surplus in 2027; Chinese refined demand growth is modest; Goldman sees $10,000–$11,000/t copper in 2026 versus spot near $13,900/t; gold has central bank, fiscal-deficit and de-dollarization support.
- **Evidence contradicting it:** LME copper is near record highs and stocks have fallen; TC/RCs are negative; mine disruptions are real and multi-year; the Section 232 tariff could keep U.S. premiums elevated.
- **Why it might be wrong:** Surplus forecasts are sensitive to Chinese demand and scrap assumptions; the ICSG itself warns that unreported Chinese inventory can swing the apparent balance by hundreds of thousands of tonnes.

### Interpretation C: Copper is underpricing long-term structural deficit
- **Who makes it:** J.P. Morgan, Morgan Stanley, UBS and energy-transition-focused investors.
- **Evidence supporting it:** J.P. Morgan forecasts a 330,000-tonne refined copper deficit in 2026 and a $12,500/t peak in Q2 (as reported by MiningVisuals and Crux Investor); Morgan Stanley forecasts a 600,000-tonne deficit (as reported by Crux Investor); UBS sees 2.8% demand growth in 2025 and 2026; long-run electrification and AI demand are structural; mine supply growth is constrained by grades and permitting.
- **Evidence contradicting it:** Spot prices are already near record highs; ICSG and Goldman Sachs see surpluses in 2026; Chinese property and manufacturing weakness could curb industrial demand.
- **Why it might be wrong:** Bullish deficit forecasts have been pushed back before; substitution to aluminum can erode copper demand at high prices; a global growth slowdown could widen surpluses faster than models assume.

### Interpretation D: Gold's safe-haven premium is fragile and has already peaked
- **Who makes it:** Technical traders and analysts who point to gold's failure to hold above $5,000/oz after the Iran conflict escalation.
- **Evidence supporting it:** Gold fell from ~$5,500/oz in late January to ~$4,000/oz by July 2026 despite ongoing Middle East conflict; LBMA noted gold was used as a "cash machine" during the Q1 volatility spike; ETF demand may not repeat 2025's record inflows.
- **Evidence contradicting it:** Central banks keep buying; fiscal deficits remain large; real yields and currency risks are still supportive; WGC expects geopolitical risk premium to persist or expand.
- **Why it might be wrong:** Short-term liquidation is different from a structural peak; sovereign buyers are price-insensitive and provide a floor; the macro drivers (deficits, de-dollarization, geopolitics) that supported the 2025 rally are still in place.

## 6. Cross-asset implications

- **Mining equities:** Gold miners benefit from high gold prices and margin expansion but face cost inflation and energy-price volatility. Copper miners benefit from high copper prices but face operational disruption risk (Chile, Indonesia, DRC) and rising sulfuric-acid/energy costs. The divergence creates relative-value opportunities but also sector-specific risks.
- **Industrial metals:** The copper-to-aluminum price ratio is at historically high levels (Goldman Sachs projects 4.5:1 in 2026 vs. 3.8:1 recent average), which increases substitution incentives in some end-uses and may cap copper demand growth.
- **Rates and FX:** Gold is sensitive to real yields and dollar weakness. If the Fed maintains higher rates for longer or the dollar strengthens, gold may remain under pressure even as central bank buying provides a floor. Copper is typically dollar-inverse in the short term but is currently being driven more by supply-side and tariff factors.
- **Emerging markets:** Copper-producing EM economies (Chile, Peru, DRC, Zambia) see terms-of-trade support but face fiscal and operational risks. Gold-buying EM central banks (Poland, China, India, Uzbekistan, Kazakhstan) are reallocating reserves away from dollar assets.
- **Credit:** Tight concentrate markets and negative TC/RCs squeeze smelter margins, raising credit risk for leveraged copper smelters and traders. Strong metal prices support mining-sector cash flows but also invite resource-nationalist measures (taxes, royalties, export restrictions).
- **Derivatives:** The COMEX–LME spread has become a pure tariff-expectation trade, decoupled from physical fundamentals. Options markets are pricing elevated volatility in both metals.

## 7. Historical analogues or structural memory

### Analogue 1: 1970s–1980 gold bull market vs. industrial commodity cycle
- **Similarity:** Gold rose on monetary and geopolitical concerns while industrial metals were buffeted by recession, oil shocks and volatile demand.
- **Difference:** Today's copper market has a clearer long-term structural demand driver (electrification/AI) and supply constraints (grade decline, permitting) that were less prominent in the 1970s.
- **Use:** It reminds us that gold can outperform during monetary stress while copper lags, and vice versa; the two do not need to move together even in a commodity bull market.

### Analogue 2: 2003–2008 commodity supercycle
- **Similarity:** Copper rose on Chinese industrialization and supply underinvestment; gold also rose later in the cycle as inflation and dollar concerns emerged.
- **Difference:** In 2003–2008 copper and gold were more correlated because both were driven by a common macro factor (EM growth, weak dollar, inflation). In 2026 the drivers have diverged: copper is supply-tightness/energy-transition, gold is monetary/sovereign.
- **Use:** It shows that "commodities rally" framing can mask divergent drivers, and that the later stage of a cycle often sees gold decouple as monetary risks take over.

## 8. What is known, unknown, and unknowable

**Known:**
- Central bank gold buying remains structurally elevated (Q1 2026: 244t; WGC full-year forecast 700–900t).
- Gold has corrected from its January 2026 record but remains at historically high levels.
- Copper is near record prices, supported by mine disruptions, negative TC/RCs and U.S. tariff expectations.
- The ICSG, J.P. Morgan, Morgan Stanley and Goldman Sachs disagree on whether the 2026 copper market will be in surplus or deficit.
- U.S. Section 232 copper tariffs are already distorting the COMEX–LME spread and trade flows.

**Unknown:**
- Whether Chinese copper demand will accelerate, stabilize or weaken further in H2 2026.
- Whether the U.S. will impose additional tariffs on refined copper after the 30 June 2026 Commerce review.
- Whether Grasberg and Kamoa-Kakula recover on schedule or face further delays.
- Whether central bank gold buying will hold near Q1 pace or slow as prices stay high.

**Unknowable:**
- The exact size of Chinese unreported copper inventories and State Reserve Bureau stock movements.
- The duration and escalation path of the Middle East conflict and its impact on sulfur/acid and shipping routes.
- Whether a broader financial or geopolitical shock triggers liquidation in gold (as in March 2020 or March 2026) or drives further safe-haven buying.

## 9. Open questions

1. If ICSG and Goldman Sachs are right about a 2026 copper surplus, why is the spot price still near $13,900/tonne and why are TC/RCs deeply negative? Is the market pricing a future deficit or a U.S.-specific premium rather than current fundamentals?
2. Will the U.S. Commerce Department recommend tariffs on refined copper after 30 June 2026, and if so, will the market treat that as already priced in or as a new catalyst?
3. Can central bank gold demand remain price-insensitive if gold retests lower levels, or will sovereign buyers step back if the monetary-policy outlook shifts?
4. To what extent is copper demand from AI data centers and grid investment already reflected in current prices, and how much is still speculative?
5. If Chinese manufacturing and property demand do not recover, can energy-transition and defense demand alone keep copper demand growth above 2% per year?
