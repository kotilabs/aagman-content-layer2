# Research Artifact — Signal: china-deflation-trap

## 1. Signal restatement

China’s full-year 2025 consumer-price inflation was 0%, producer prices fell for a third consecutive year, and real-estate investment and home prices kept declining. Beijing has responded with a visibly larger stimulus footprint—higher fiscal deficits, local-government debt swaps, lower interest rates, and a RMB 300 billion consumer-goods trade-in program—but the bulk of the support is flowing through supply-side channels (equipment upgrades, manufacturing credit, tech/green lending) rather than direct household income support. The December 2025 CPI uptick to 0.8% year-on-year was driven by temporary food-supply shocks (fresh vegetables +18.2% YoY), while core demand, non-food prices, property prices, and producer prices remained weak. The signal is that China is running stimulus without a functioning domestic-demand engine: the policy mix can hit a 5% GDP target and keep exports strong, but it has not yet reversed the deflationary pressure coming from balance-sheet repair in the property sector and cautious household spending.

## 4. Mechanism

The signal works through a mismatch between **supply-side stimulus** and **demand-side balance-sheet repair**.

1. **Production is being supported, not consumption.** Fiscal policy in 2025 raised the deficit to around 4% of GDP, issued ultra-long special treasury bonds and local special-purpose bonds, and expanded the trade-in program. Monetary policy shifted to “moderately loose,” cutting rates and the RRR. But the largest flows are going to equipment upgrades, local-government debt refinancing, bank recapitalisation, and targeted lending to technology, green energy, and manufacturing. These are supply-side or balance-sheet-repair measures; they keep factories open and local governments solvent but do not put durable purchasing power into households’ hands.

2. **Household demand is held back by wealth loss and precautionary saving.** Urban households hold roughly 70% of their wealth in housing. With new-home prices still falling and existing-home prices falling faster, households feel poorer and are reluctant to spend or borrow. Real-estate investment, sales, and land-sales revenue keep declining, which also squeezes local-government finances and construction employment. The result is a feedback loop: falling property prices → weaker consumer confidence → lower spending → weaker corporate pricing power → lower investment and hiring.

3. **The CPI composition confirms the demand gap.** The December uptick in headline CPI came from food-supply factors (vegetable prices, partly weather-related) and pre-New-Year purchases of electronics and appliances. Core CPI, non-food prices, rents, and transport/telecommunication prices remain soft. Producer prices have now fallen for three consecutive years, pointing to industrial overcapacity and price competition rather than demand-pull inflation.

4. **The excess supply is being exported.** Weak domestic absorption means Chinese manufacturers seek foreign markets. The record 2025 trade surplus, surging steel and EV exports, and flat imports are symptoms of a domestic-demand shortfall being offset by external demand. That transmits deflationary pressure to global manufacturing margins and complicates trade relations.

In short, Beijing’s stimulus is large enough to hit a 5% GDP target and keep the financial system stable, but it is not yet large enough or sufficiently consumption-oriented to reverse the deflationary drag from the property sector and household balance-sheet repair.

## 5. Competing interpretations

### Interpretation A: A managed transition, not a trap
- **Who makes it:** Chinese official statements and some growth-focused analysts.
- **What evidence supports it:** GDP hit 5% in 2025; retail sales rose 3.7%; industrial production rose 5.9%; high-tech manufacturing and EV/steel exports are strong; unemployment is stable; PPI declines narrowed through late 2025; the NBS described the economy as “pushing forward with innovation-led and high-quality development.”
- **What evidence contradicts it:** CPI was 0%, core CPI only 0.7% for the year, property investment fell 17.2%, and new-home prices kept falling. These are not consistent with a healthy demand recovery.
- **Why it might be wrong:** It confuses output growth with price stability. Hitting a GDP target through investment and exports does not mean the deflationary pressure from household balance sheets has been resolved.

### Interpretation B: A balance-sheet recession (Koo-style)
- **Who makes it:** Richard Koo and researchers at Stanford SCCEI, among others.
- **What evidence supports it:** Households hold ~70% of wealth in housing; a 10% home-price drop cuts consumption by an estimated 1.5–2.3%; households are deleveraging while property prices fall; private investment fell 6.4% in 2025; core CPI and PPI are weak; fiscal support is not primarily aimed at household income.
- **What evidence contradicts it:** China has not experienced a systemic banking crisis; state-controlled banks can roll over bad loans; the government has more policy control than Japan did in the 1990s; exports and manufacturing are providing offset.
- **Why it might be wrong:** The model assumes private-sector balance-sheet repair is the dominant force. If Beijing eventually shifts to large-scale household transfers or if property prices stabilise sooner than expected, the analogy weakens.

### Interpretation C: Structural overcapacity and export of deflation
- **Who makes it:** Industrial analysts and some trading partners concerned about Chinese dumping.
- **What evidence supports it:** PPI has fallen for three years; steel and EV exports surged despite tariffs; imports were flat while exports grew, producing a record surplus; manufacturing investment and industrial production are strong while domestic prices are weak.
- **What evidence contradicts it:** Some of the export strength reflects front-running of tariffs and genuine competitiveness in EVs and clean-tech, not just dumping; domestic overcapacity is sector-specific (property, some industrial goods) rather than economy-wide.
- **Why it might be wrong:** It treats a demand shortfall as a supply problem. Closing the demand gap would absorb much of the perceived overcapacity without needing to export it.

### Interpretation D: A deliberate policy choice to favour supply over consumption
- **Who makes it:** Political-economy and China-watch observers.
- **What evidence supports it:** The 2025 policy mix emphasises equipment upgrades, local debt swaps, bank recapitalisation, and tech/green lending; direct household income support remains small relative to GDP; the government work report frames consumption as a “primary driver” but the spending is mostly goods subsidies and services-opening measures.
- **What evidence contradicts it:** Official rhetoric repeatedly stresses expanding domestic demand; the trade-in program was doubled; PBOC created RMB 500 billion of central-bank lending for service consumption and elderly care.
- **Why it might be wrong:** Even if the preference is real, the scale of the property wealth shock may eventually force a larger consumption-led response for social-stability reasons.

## 6. Cross-asset implications

- **Chinese equities:** CSI 300 and Hang Seng China have been sensitive to stimulus headlines but are restrained by weak earnings in property, consumer discretionary, and traditional industrials. Policy support can create tactical rallies, but a sustained re-rating likely requires evidence of property stabilisation or a consumption pivot.
- **Property and related credit:** Developers remain under pressure; real-estate investment fell 17.2% in 2025. Local-government debt swaps and bank forbearance reduce systemic refinancing risk but do not restore developer cash flows or buyer confidence. Property-related credit spreads and high-yield dollar bonds remain vulnerable.
- **Commodities:** Iron ore and steel are caught between weak property demand (historically the largest steel consumer) and strong manufacturing/EV/export demand. Copper benefits from electrification and data-center build-outs but faces tariff and global-growth headwinds. If domestic demand stays weak, Chinese industrial commodity imports may grow more slowly than headline GDP.
- **FX and rates:** The PBOC kept the RMB broadly stable and allowed 4.4% appreciation against the USD in 2025 despite domestic easing. Further rate cuts to fight deflation could widen the US–China rate differential and create capital-outflow pressure, especially if the Fed holds rates higher for longer. Conversely, a large consumption pivot could strengthen the RMB via improved growth expectations.
- **Global manufacturing and trade:** Record Chinese trade surpluses and surging steel/EV exports put pressure on manufacturing margins in ASEAN, India, Latin America, and Europe. This raises the risk of additional tariffs or anti-dumping measures, which would feed back into Chinese export growth.
- **Emerging-market FX and EM Asia:** Countries that compete with Chinese manufacturing exports face terms-of-trade and margin pressure. Countries that supply commodities to China (Australia, Brazil, Chile, Peru) face mixed signals: volumes may hold up while prices weaken.

## 7. Historical analogues or structural memory

### Japan after 1991
- **Key similarity:** A post-bubble property downturn, negative wealth effects, investment overhang, and a prolonged period of weak domestic demand. Real-estate investment turned from a growth driver to a drag, and consumer sentiment stayed depressed for years.
- **Key difference:** China’s financial system is state-dominated, allowing forbearance and controlled debt restructuring rather than a sudden banking crisis; land is state-owned; capital controls limit capital flight; and China retains strong manufacturing/export momentum and a faster-aging population.
- **Use for perspective:** The Japan analog suggests that even without a banking crisis, the real-economy drag from property wealth loss, investment overhang, and pessimistic sentiment can last many years. It is not a prediction of China’s trajectory.

### China’s 1998–2002 deflation
- **Key similarity:** The last sustained period of domestic-demand shortfall, with deflationary prices, weak consumption, and SOE restructuring. Beijing responded with proactive fiscal policy (infrastructure), sound/monetary easing, and WTO accession-led export growth.
- **Key difference:** China in the late 1990s had much lower debt, a younger population, strong export-led catch-up growth ahead, and a smaller property sector. Today the economy is larger, more indebted, demographically older, and facing a more hostile external trade environment.
- **Use for perspective:** It shows that China has exited deflation before through a combination of fiscal stimulus and external demand, but the starting conditions today are less favourable.

## 8. What is known, unknown, and unknowable

### Known
- Full-year 2025 CPI was 0% and PPI fell 2.6%.
- Property investment, sales, and prices declined through 2025.
- The policy response is heavily weighted toward supply-side measures, local-government debt management, and manufacturing/tech credit.
- Household wealth is concentrated in housing, and the estimated consumption drag from falling home prices is large relative to current stimulus.

### Unknown
- Whether Beijing will shift to a large, durable household income/consumption support package in 2026.
- How much further property prices will fall before stabilising.
- The true scale of hidden bad loans in banks, local-government financing vehicles, and developer balance sheets.
- Whether the current export surge is sustainable or partly a tariff front-run that will fade.

### Unknowable
- The political threshold at which the leadership decides consumption support outweighs supply-side industrial goals.
- Geopolitical shocks (tariffs, sanctions, Taiwan-related risk) that could abruptly change capital flows or export demand.
- The confidence turning point: when households stop expecting further home-price declines and start spending again.

## 9. Open questions

1. Will 2026 fiscal policy include direct household income transfers or a large social-safety-net expansion, or will it remain focused on equipment upgrades and local debt?
2. At what level of property-price decline does household balance-sheet repair start to ease rather than intensify?
3. Can export strength and manufacturing investment continue to offset weak domestic consumption if trade barriers rise further?
4. Does producer-price deflation need to end before consumer-price inflation can sustainably rise?
5. How will local governments fund themselves if land-sales revenue does not recover and special-bond issuance is capped?
