# Research Artifact — India Domestic-Flow Market

**Signal ID:** india-domestic-flow-market  
**Digest date:** 2026-07-03  
**Research completed:** 2026-07-08  
**Artifact version:** 1.0

---

## 1. Signal restatement

Indian equities are experiencing a structural ownership shift: foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) have been selling relentlessly through 2025 and 2026, while domestic institutional investors (DIIs) — mutual funds, insurers and pension funds — have absorbed most of that selling through steady inflows, particularly via systematic investment plans (SIPs). The result is that DII ownership of NSE-listed companies has risen to a record high while FPI ownership has fallen to a 14–17-year low, depending on the universe measured. Despite record foreign outflows, India VIX has recently traded near five-month lows, suggesting that domestic liquidity has contained panic. The open question is whether this domestic absorption is a durable re-rating of India’s investor base or a temporary counter-trend that could crack if SIP flows slow or global risk appetite deteriorates further.

## 4. Mechanism

The Indian equity market has two large, differently motivated pools of capital.

**Foreign portfolio investors** are global asset allocators. Their India exposure is a function of relative valuation, relative earnings momentum, currency expectations, global risk appetite and benchmark weights. When US rates stay high, the dollar strengthens, oil prices rise, or India underperforms China/Korea/Taiwan, FPIs can reallocate quickly. Because India’s market is premium-valued and because rupee depreciation directly reduces dollar returns, small changes in global opportunity cost can produce large outflows. FPI selling is concentrated in large-cap, liquid names — the stocks easiest to trade and most owned by foreign funds.

**Domestic institutional investors** are primarily mutual funds, life insurers (notably LIC), pension funds and banks. Their inflows are driven by household savings habits, insurance premium collections and, most visibly, monthly SIPs. SIPs are sticky by design: investors commit a fixed amount each month regardless of market level. That creates a mechanical bid into equities. When FPIs sell, domestic funds use their cash and fresh inflows to buy. This is why DII holdings have risen even as FPI holdings have fallen.

**Why India VIX has stayed subdued.** India VIX measures the implied volatility priced into Nifty 50 options over the next 30 days. It spikes when demand for protective puts surges and falls when the market believes downside is limited. Domestic absorption has prevented the kind of disorderly index declines that normally trigger panic hedging. Even during the March/April 2026 geopolitical spike, VIX reached only around 28–29 — elevated, but far below crisis levels (87 in March 2020, 92.5 in November 2008).

**The ownership crossover is the structural event.** For years, FPIs were the marginal price-setters in Indian equities. Now DIIs are larger by ownership share in several indices and by net buying flow. That changes market dynamics: corrections are shallower and shorter-lived when domestic liquidity is abundant, but it also means the market’s resilience depends on the continued health of SIP and insurance flows.

**The external sector adds a constraint.** India runs a current account deficit that must be financed by capital flows. When FPI equity outflows exceed FDI and debt inflows, the RBI must either let the rupee depreciate or use reserves. Between April and December 2025, the RBI drew down US$30.8 billion of reserves (excluding valuation effects) and allowed the rupee to weaken. That depreciation, in turn, hurts dollar-based foreign returns and can reinforce the FPI selling cycle.

## 5. Competing interpretations

### Interpretation A: Foreign selling is a structural downgrade that will keep pressure on Indian equities

**Who is making it:** Several global EM allocators, valuation-focused strategists and market commentators citing HSBC, Nomura and others.

**Supporting evidence:**
- India’s neutral weight in MSCI EM fell to a two-year low of 15.25% and India became the biggest underweight in GEM portfolios.
- 71% of large EM funds were underweight India by end-July (as cited), up from 60% a month earlier.
- India’s 2025 market return (+5%) lagged MSCI EM (+25%) and MSCI China (+35%).
- Yogesh Aggarwal of HSBC said India is “60 per cent more expensive” than China, Indonesia and Korea.
- FPIs have sold in every month of 2026 except February; the FY26 net FPI outflow was US$16.5 billion.

**Contradicting evidence:**
- DII buying has absorbed most of the selling, preventing a market crash.
- FDI rebounded and India remains the fastest-growing major economy (RBI: 7.6% in FY26).
- Light foreign positioning means there is less foreign capital left to sell; a small re-risking could trigger sharp inflows.

**Why it might be wrong:** It treats FPI outflows as a permanent vote against India rather than a relative-rotation trade. If global risk appetite stabilises and earnings improve, the same underweight positioning could reverse quickly.

### Interpretation B: Light foreign positioning and domestic stickiness are constructive if earnings deliver

**Who is making it:** HSBC and Goldman Sachs are cited as having turned overweight on India even as the broader GEM community stays cautious; Amundi also expected interest in Indian stocks to revive.

**Supporting evidence:**
- FPI ownership is at 14–17-year lows; DII ownership is at record highs.
- India VIX has fallen to five-month lows, showing domestic liquidity has contained volatility.
- GDP growth is strong (6.5–7.6% range), inflation has moderated and corporate balance sheets are healthy.
- Aless Berardi of Amundi said valuations were moving toward neutral and domestic demand staying robust, making conditions for a resurgence in foreign investment more favourable.

**Contradicting evidence:**
- Domestic flows are not infinite. SIP stoppage ratios above 100% in March–April 2026 suggest the retail base may be narrowing even if rupee contributions are stable.
- If earnings do not improve, domestic buyers may eventually lose patience.
- Rupee depreciation is a real drag on dollar returns for foreign investors.

**Why it might be wrong:** It assumes domestic flows are a permanent floor. SIPs are sticky but not immutable; a prolonged bear market or economic shock could slow them. It also assumes foreign investors will return on better earnings, which is not guaranteed if other EMs offer better risk-adjusted returns.

### Interpretation C: The ownership shift is a durable structural transition, not a cyclical blip

**Who is making it:** Domestic brokerages, the Economic Survey 2025-26, and commentators focused on India’s “SIP culture.”

**Supporting evidence:**
- The Economic Survey documented that DIIs, retail and HNI combined reached 27.8% of NSE-listed equity value by Q2 FY26, and that DII share by value surpassed FII share for the first time in Q4 FY25.
- SIP inflows have been above ₹30,000 crore for several months; equity mutual funds have seen positive inflows for 63 consecutive months.
- The value of FPI holdings has still grown at a CAGR of more than 18% since 2020 even as the ownership share has fallen, meaning the domestic share has grown faster.

**Contradicting evidence:**
- DII buying is partly a mechanical response to inflows, not necessarily a discretionary bullish call.
- Foreign ownership can fall simply because foreigners sell into domestic buying during a correction; it does not by itself prove a permanent domestic-dominated regime.
- SIP stoppage ratios above 100% are a warning that the retail broadening may be plateauing.

**Why it might be wrong:** It extrapolates a few years of SIP growth into a permanent structural change. Household financial savings can shift between asset classes, and a period of poor equity returns could redirect flows to fixed deposits, gold or real estate.

### Interpretation D: The real story is currency and global liquidity, not India-specific fundamentals

**Who is making it:** Macro strategists, RBI officials and analysts focused on the US dollar, Fed policy and oil prices.

**Supporting evidence:**
- FPI outflows accelerated in months when the US dollar strengthened, US rates stayed high and oil prices spiked.
- The rupee weakened from the mid-80s to about ₹95 per dollar, directly reducing dollar returns.
- RBI’s FY26 reserve drawdown and rupee weakness show the external constraint is binding.
- Similar FPI selling has been seen across EMs during risk-off phases.

**Contradicting evidence:**
- India-specific factors — premium valuations, slower earnings momentum, tax concerns — are repeatedly cited by foreign investors themselves.
- FPIs did not sell all EMs equally; China, Taiwan and Korea attracted flows while India saw outflows.

**Why it might be wrong:** It underweights India-specific valuation and earnings concerns. Global liquidity is a necessary condition for FPI selling, but it is not sufficient to explain why India underperformed other EMs.

## 6. Cross-asset implications

**Equities.** FPI selling has hit large-cap, liquid names hardest — IT, financials and FMCG have seen the largest FII stake cuts in dollar terms. DIIs have bought defensives (FMCG), quality private banks and domestic themes. The result is a split market: large caps are under foreign-selling pressure while mid- and small-caps have been relatively supported by domestic liquidity.

**FX.** Persistent FPI outflows and a current account deficit have weakened the rupee. RBI intervention has smoothed the path but has also drawn down reserves. A weaker rupee is negative for foreign equity returns but can eventually make Indian assets cheaper in dollar terms and improve export competitiveness.

**Rates / bonds.** While FPIs sold equities, they were buyers of Indian debt, especially government securities through the Fully Accessible Route (FAR). Net FPI debt inflows were US$2.1 billion in FY26, and June 2026 saw strong G-sec buying. This bifurcation suggests foreign investors are not negative on India per se, but are reallocating from expensive equity risk to relatively safer rupee debt.

**Credit.** Strong domestic institutions and steady insurance/pension flows support corporate bond demand. RBI relaxations on FPI corporate debt limits and the VRR-General Route merger are aimed at deepening the market, but foreign ownership of government debt remains modest.

**Commodities.** India is a large oil importer. The West Asia conflict pushed Brent crude above US$100 in May 2026 and back toward US$78–79 in July. Higher oil worsens India’s trade deficit, inflation and currency, creating a feedback loop that reinforces FPI caution.

**Derivatives.** Low India VIX through June and early July compressed option premiums, making hedging cheap. The 26% single-day VIX spike on 8 July 2026 shows how quickly geopolitical news can reprice short-term risk.

## 7. Historical analogues or structural memory

### Analogue 1: The 2013 Taper Tantrum

**Episode:** In May 2013, Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke hinted at tapering quantitative easing. US Treasury yields surged, capital fled emerging markets and the “Fragile Five” — Brazil, India, Indonesia, South Africa and Turkey — came under severe pressure.

**Key similarity:** India saw rapid capital outflows and a sharply weaker currency. The rupee fell from about ₹55 per dollar in May 2013 to almost ₹69 by August 2013. The RBI responded by tightening liquidity, raising the marginal standing facility rate by 200 bps and, eventually under Governor Raghuram Rajan, launching a foreign-currency non-resident (FCNR) deposit scheme that mobilised more than US$30 billion.

**Key difference:** India today has stronger macro fundamentals than in 2013. The current account deficit is around 1.1% of GDP versus a much wider deficit then; forex reserves are far larger (US$691 billion); and domestic institutional flows are a much bigger buffer. The ownership base has shifted toward local investors.

**Why it is useful for perspective, not prediction:** It shows how quickly a Fed-led risk-off can overwhelm emerging markets and how policy responses (rate hikes, FCNR deposits, reserve use) can stabilise but not fully prevent currency depreciation. It also shows that the worst can pass once credible policy action is taken and global conditions stabilise.

### Analogue 2: The 2020 COVID crash and V-shaped recovery

**Episode:** In March 2020, India VIX hit an all-time high near 87 as FPIs fled and markets crashed. Within months, massive global liquidity and domestic buying produced a sharp recovery.

**Key similarity:** A sudden stop in foreign flows was absorbed by domestic liquidity and policy support; the market recovered before foreign flows fully returned.

**Key difference:** The 2020 shock was exogenous and short-lived, followed by unprecedented global stimulus. The current episode is a slower-burn valuation and relative-return adjustment, not a sudden pandemic shock.

**Why it is useful for perspective, not prediction:** It demonstrates that Indian markets can recover even when foreign investors are absent, provided domestic flows and policy support remain. But it does not tell us whether the current slowdown in earnings and global risk appetite will resolve as quickly.

## 8. What is known, unknown, and unknowable

### Known
- FPIs have sold record amounts of Indian equities in 2025 and 2026; DII buying has absorbed most of it.
- FPI ownership has fallen to multi-decade lows; DII ownership has risen to record highs.
- India VIX has moderated sharply from its March/April 2026 spike and recently traded near five-month lows.
- The rupee has depreciated materially, the current account deficit is modest, and the RBI has used reserves to manage the external gap.
- SIP and equity MF flows remain positive but have shown signs of moderation, and SIP stoppage ratios crossed 100% in March–April 2026.
- India’s MSCI EM weight and active fund positioning are at multi-year lows.

### Unknown
- Whether the current FPI selling is a temporary rotation or a longer-lasting reallocation away from India.
- Whether domestic inflows, particularly SIPs, can continue at current rates if equity returns remain subdued for another 12–18 months.
- Whether corporate earnings will reaccelerate enough to justify India’s valuation premium and attract foreign capital back.
- How long the US dollar, Fed policy and geopolitical tensions will remain headwinds for EM flows.
- Whether the recent India VIX calm reflects genuine stability or complacency that could unwind rapidly.

### Unknowable
- The timing and magnitude of any reversal in FPI flows; flow dynamics are nonlinear and can turn on unpredictable global events.
- Whether a future shock (geopolitical escalation, oil spike, Fed surprise, domestic political event) could overwhelm domestic liquidity.
- The long-term equilibrium foreign-ownership share in Indian equities as domestic savings deepen.

---

## 9. Open questions

1. **Can domestic flows keep absorbing FPI selling if the current outflow pace persists for another 6–12 months?** SIP stoppage ratios above 100% suggest the retail base may be narrowing; if contributions start falling, the domestic floor weakens.

2. **What will it take for foreign investors to return?** Is it better earnings, a weaker rupee making India cheap in dollar terms, Fed rate cuts, lower oil prices, or some combination?

3. **Is India VIX’s subdued level a signal of stability or a false calm?** With 52-week highs near 28.9 and historical crisis levels far higher, a geopolitical or macro surprise could reprice volatility sharply.

4. **How will the RBI balance rupee defence with growth?** Further reserve drawdowns and possible rate hikes to attract capital could tighten domestic financial conditions.

5. **Will the ownership crossover change sector leadership permanently?** As DIIs become the dominant marginal buyer, domestic-oriented sectors and mid/small-caps may continue to outperform foreign-owned large caps.
