## 1. Signal restatement

On 5 June 2026 the Reserve Bank of India’s Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) kept the repo rate at 5.25% and retained a neutral stance, but the main policy message was external rather than monetary. Governor Sanjay Malhotra announced a package of dollar-inflow measures — expanding the Fully Accessible Route (FAR) for government securities, raising NRI/OCI equity limits, offering concessional forex swaps for PSU external commercial borrowings, and absorbing the full hedging cost on fresh 3–5 year FCNR(B) deposits — alongside a government ordinance exempting foreign portfolio investors from tax on G-Sec interest and capital gains. The RBI also cut its FY27 real GDP growth forecast to 6.6% from 6.9% and raised its FY27 CPI inflation forecast to 5.1% from 4.6%. The signal is that India is choosing to defend external stability with capital-account and regulatory tools rather than by tightening interest rates into a slowing economy, a different calculus from the Fed, ECB or several Asian central banks that have either held hawkishly or hiked.

## 4. Mechanism

The signal operates through a separation of monetary policy from external-sector management.

**Monetary policy stays on hold because the inflation shock is supply-side.** The RBI explicitly frames the rise in inflation as driven by global energy prices, fuel-price pass-through and monsoon risk, not by excess domestic demand. Core inflation excluding precious metals is projected lower, and demand pressures are described as "contained." In that context, raising the repo rate would raise borrowing costs for households and firms without directly lowering oil prices, while cutting rates into an inflation upswing would risk de-anchoring expectations. The neutral stance preserves optionality.

**External stability is addressed through the capital account and regulation.** Rather than using the interest-rate weapon to attract dollars or defend the rupee, the RBI is: (i) widening the supply of rupee assets foreigners can hold (FAR expansion, FPI limit relaxation, tax exemption); (ii) subsidising the hedging cost for foreign-currency borrowing by PSUs and NRI deposits (concessional swaps); and (iii) encouraging faster repatriation of export proceeds. The FCNR(B) swap is the most direct mechanism: banks accept USD deposits from NRIs, swap the dollars with the RBI at the spot rate and receive dollars back at the same rate at maturity, with the RBI absorbing the roughly 280–300 basis point annual hedging cost. CRR and SLR exemptions make the deposits cheaper for banks to mobilise.

**The rupee is managed for volatility, not a level.** Governor Malhotra reiterated that the RBI does not target a specific exchange-rate level or band; it intervenes to curb excessive volatility and disorderly movements. The dollar-inflow measures are intended to ease pressure on the rupee and on the RBI’s forward book without draining spot reserves.

**Liquidity is a secondary channel.** The RBI noted that banking-system liquidity had tightened as FX intervention and seasonal currency leakage offset policy easing. Dollar inflows under the new measures, if material, would reduce the need for forward-market intervention and add rupee liquidity to the banking system.

## 5. Competing interpretations

### Interpretation A: The RBI has threaded the needle — defending the rupee without choking growth.

**Who is making it:** RBI leadership, several Indian sell-side economists and fund managers quoted after the policy (e.g., Invesco Mutual Fund, HDFC Fund, SBI Chairman C.S. Setty).  
**Evidence supporting it:** The repo rate is unchanged while growth is already slowing; the RBI cut its GDP forecast. The dollar-inflow measures are targeted at the capital account, not domestic demand. Governor Malhotra stressed that India entered the turbulence with stronger fundamentals than in past crises, and that forex reserves are comfortable. FPI bond inflows of ₹35,000 crore in June suggest the measures are working.  
**Evidence contradicting it:** The Q3 inflation projection of 5.9% is only 10 basis points below the 6% upper tolerance band; if food or fuel prices spike, the MPC may still have to hike. The rupee remains materially weaker than a year ago, and FII equity selling has continued despite the measures.  
**Why it might be wrong:** It assumes the supply shock fades by Q4 as the RBI projects. If the West Asia conflict persists or the monsoon is worse than expected, the RBI could be forced into a rate hike that undermines the growth-support narrative.

### Interpretation B: The measures are necessary but insufficient; the RBI is behind the curve on the currency.

**Who is making it:** Some market commentary (e.g., Finnovate’s "Missed Credibility Signal" note) and analysts who expected at least a hawkish tilt or 25bp hike.  
**Evidence supporting it:** The rupee had fallen from roughly ₹90 to a record near ₹97 in about a year, and FPI equity outflows in FY27 so far totalled $13.7 billion. A hold at 5.25% with inflation rising means the real policy rate is falling. Several Asian central banks had already hiked to defend their currencies.  
**Evidence contradicting it:** The rupee rallied post-policy and continued to strengthen as oil prices fell. The RBI’s reserve buffer and the scale of announced inflow measures (FCNR(B) alone is estimated by some analysts at $40–50 billion potential) suggest the capital-account channel can stabilise the external account without a rate hike.  
**Why it might be wrong:** If global risk appetite turns sharply negative or the Fed follows through on its hawkish dots, the capital-account measures may not be enough to stop rupee depreciation, and the RBI may then have to tighten anyway — but from a position of having already spent some ammunition.

### Interpretation C: This is a tactical pause before a late-2026 tightening cycle.

**Who is making it:** Economists at OCBC, CareEdge Ratings, and some fixed-income analysts (e.g., IndusInd Bank’s Gaurav Kapur) who note that the real repo rate is now below the neutral rate given the revised inflation forecast.  
**Evidence supporting it:** The RBI raised its full-year inflation forecast by 50bp and its Q3 forecast to 5.9%. Governor Malhotra said the MPC will remain data-dependent and monitor whether supply-side pressures get embedded in the general price level and inflation expectations. The April 2026 projection already saw risks; the June projection sees more.  
**Evidence contradicting it:** The MPC vote was unanimous and the stance remained neutral, not hawkish. The Governor explicitly said in late June that the RBI is not considering a rate hike at present. The growth forecast was also cut, which makes a tightening cycle harder to justify.  
**Why it might be wrong:** It requires inflation to actually print near or above 6% and stay there. If oil prices remain around $70–75/bbl and the monsoon is only moderately deficient, headline inflation may peak below the upper band and the RBI can remain on hold.

### Interpretation D: The measures are primarily about liquidity and bond-market depth, not the rupee.

**Who is making it:** Fixed-income strategists and bond-market participants who note the combination of FPI tax exemption, FAR expansion and FCNR(B)/ECB swaps.  
**Evidence supporting it:** The government’s tax exemption applies specifically to G-Secs, not corporate bonds, and is clearly designed to deepen the sovereign bond market and improve India’s position in global bond indices. FPI holdings in FAR securities rose from ₹3.23 lakh crore to ₹3.58 lakh crore within weeks. The FCNR(B) and ECB swaps also inject rupee liquidity into the banking system.  
**Evidence contradicting it:** The Governor framed the measures explicitly as strengthening the balance of payments and supporting the rupee amid external pressures. The timing — during a period of rupee weakness and FPI equity outflows — suggests currency management is at least a co-objective.  
**Why it might be wrong:** It underweights the external-account motivation. While bond-market depth is a beneficiary, the immediate trigger was the current-account and currency pressure from the West Asia conflict.

## 6. Cross-asset implications

**FX.** The rupee strengthened from near-record lows to 94.39/USD by 25 June, supported by falling oil prices and the RBI’s inflow measures. The near-term range is likely to be driven by the Fed’s dot plot, oil prices and FPI equity flows rather than by RBI rate changes. The RBI’s emphasis on volatility management rather than a level means the currency can still depreciate gradually if fundamentals warrant it.

**Rates.** The front end of the Indian yield curve is anchored by the 5.25% repo hold, but the long end is sensitive to FPI demand under FAR and the government tax exemption. Corporate bond yields fell more than G-Secs immediately after the policy, reflecting relief on domestic supply pressure as PSUs gain access to cheaper ECBs. If inflation prints near the RBI’s Q3 projection, expectations of a future hike could push short-end yields higher.

**Equities.** Rate-sensitive sectors (banks, NBFCs, autos, real estate) face a stable funding-cost environment but no fresh rate-cut tailwind. Banks with strong NRI franchises and those able to mobilise FCNR(B) deposits may see funding advantages. The broader market has been supported by DII buying offsetting FII selling; the durability of that pattern depends on domestic flows remaining robust.

**Bonds / sovereign debt.** The FPI tax exemption and FAR expansion are structurally positive for G-Sec demand and could improve India’s weighting in global bond indices. The ₹35,000 crore of FPI bond buying in June is an early sign, but equity outflows show foreign risk appetite remains fragile.

**Commodities.** Lower Brent crude is the single biggest macro relief for India. Each $10/bbl fall reduces the import bill and eases both the current account deficit and inflation. Gold, which had been supported by geopolitical risk, has come under pressure as real yields and the dollar have firmed; the rupee’s stabilisation also reduces the local-currency urgency for gold buying.

**Credit.** PSUs and large corporates with access to ECB markets benefit from cheaper foreign-currency borrowing costs. Smaller firms without that access remain dependent on domestic bank credit, where liquidity has tightened and transmission of past rate cuts is still incomplete.

**Derivatives.** India VIX below 13 suggests near-term volatility expectations have moderated, but weekly derivatives expiries and FII positioning remain live variables. The rupee forward book and RBI swaps are the key institutional instruments to watch.

## 7. Historical analogues or structural memory

### Analogue 1: The 2013 FCNR(B) swap during the taper tantrum.

**The episode:** In September 2013, then-RBI Governor Raghuram Rajan opened a concessional FCNR(B) swap window to attract NRI dollar deposits during a sharp rupee depreciation. Banks mobilised approximately $26–34 billion, helping stabilise the rupee after it had fallen from around ₹55 to ₹68 per dollar.

**Key similarity:** The RBI is again using a subsidised swap window for NRI deposits to attract dollar inflows without raising domestic interest rates, and it has again paired it with CRR/SLR exemptions to improve bank economics.

**Key difference:** In 2013, US rates were near zero and the spread between FCNR(B) rates and US alternatives was over 500 basis points; in 2026, US rates are around 4.5% and the spread is roughly 150–250 basis points, making the deposit decision less of a no-brainer. India’s macro fundamentals are also stronger now: forex reserves are much larger, the current account deficit is smaller, and the banking system is healthier.

**Why it is useful for perspective, not prediction:** It shows that NRI deposit windows can materially help the external account, but the scale of inflows depends on the relative yield spread and global risk appetite, not just the RBI’s willingness to offer swaps.

### Analogue 2: The 2020–2022 pandemic-era separation of rate policy and external management.

**The episode:** During the pandemic and its aftermath, the RBI cut rates aggressively to support growth while using forward swaps, dollar sales and special deposit schemes to manage rupee volatility and accumulate reserves.

**Key similarity:** The central bank used different tools for different objectives — rate policy for domestic demand, FX and capital-account measures for the external account.

**Key difference:** In 2020–2022 the challenge was capital inflows and rupee appreciation; in 2026 it is capital outflows and rupee depreciation. The direction of pressure has reversed, even if the operating principle is similar.

**Why it is useful for perspective, not prediction:** It reminds us that modern central banks rarely have a single instrument for a single target; the art is in matching the tool to the source of the shock.

## 8. What is known, unknown, and unknowable

### Known
- The RBI held the repo rate at 5.25% and retained a neutral stance on 5 June 2026, with a unanimous 6–0 vote.
- The RBI cut its FY27 GDP growth forecast to 6.6% and raised its FY27 CPI inflation forecast to 5.1% (Q3: 5.9%).
- Five specific dollar-inflow measures were announced, plus a government ordinance exempting FPIs from tax on G-Sec interest and capital gains.
- Forex reserves stood at $682.3 billion as of 29 May 2026; net FPI outflows in FY27 so far were $13.7 billion.
- The rupee strengthened to 94.39/USD by 25 June and India VIX fell below 13, as Brent crude dropped to around $73/bbl.
- The Fed, ECB and RBA are all on divergent paths: the Fed is hawkishly on hold, the ECB hiked into weak growth, and the RBA paused after three hikes.

### Unknown
- How much dollar inflow the FCNR(B) swap and PSU ECB facilities will actually generate. Analyst estimates range from $40 billion to $50 billion, but actual mobilisation depends on bank pricing and NRI demand.
- Whether Q3 FY27 inflation will print near the RBI’s 5.9% projection or overshoot the 6% upper tolerance band.
- Whether the West Asia conflict will de-escalate further or flare again, determining the path of oil prices and freight costs.
- Whether FPI equity selling will continue, stabilise or reverse in the second half of 2026.
- The precise scale of the RBI’s forward-book intervention and the cost of the FCNR(B) swaps to its balance sheet if the rupee depreciates further.

### Unknowable
- The exact duration and intensity of the West Asia conflict and its impact on energy markets.
- Whether the current episode is a temporary supply shock that fades by Q4, as the RBI projects, or the start of a more persistent inflationary regime.
- The future path of US monetary policy under a new Fed chair and a highly uncertain political and fiscal environment.
- Whether household and wage inflation expectations in India will remain anchored if headline inflation stays above 5% for several quarters.

## 9. Open questions

1. **Will the FCNR(B) and PSU ECB swap windows attract enough dollars to offset FPI equity outflows and a wider current account deficit?** The structure is in place, but the relative yield spread over US alternatives is much smaller than in 2013, and the window is only four months long.

2. **If Q3 inflation prints close to or above 6%, can the RBI really avoid hiking?** The neutral stance keeps the option open, but a hike into a 6.3% Q2 growth print would test the RBI’s growth-support credentials.

3. **How durable is the rupee’s recovery if the Fed follows through on its hawkish 2026 dot plot?** The rupee has benefited from falling oil prices and DII support; a stronger dollar and higher US yields could reverse some of that.

4. **Will the FPI tax exemption and FAR expansion lead to sustained G-Sec index inclusion and structurally lower Indian bond yields, or is it a one-off inflow?** Early June data show ₹35,000 crore of FPI bond buying, but the test will come when global risk appetite turns.

5. **What is the hidden cost on the RBI’s balance sheet of absorbing FCNR(B) hedging costs and running a large forward book?** The swap window transfers currency risk from banks to the central bank; if the rupee depreciates sharply over 3–5 years, the contingent liability could be material.
