## Macro / Structural Lens

### Primary editorial candidates

#### 1. Central-bank policy divergence is repricing the dollar and the front end of curves
- **Why it matters now:** The Fed is signalling a possible late-2026 hike if inflation persists, while the ECB is messaging gradual easing and the BoJ is slowly normalising. The 10-year Treasury yield is back near 4.49% and the 2-year at 4.14%, steepening the curve after a long inversion. USD/JPY has risen to ~162.7 as the yen weakens despite verbal intervention warnings.
- **Primary asset classes affected:** FX (USD, JPY, EUR), US Treasuries, global rates, gold.
- **What makes it structurally interesting:** This is the first sustained curve steepening after the 2022–2024 inversion. The market is moving from pricing cuts to pricing a hawkish Fed chair reset and a possible fiscal/liquidity rebound ahead of US midterms.
- **What most commentary is missing:** The dollar strength is happening alongside softening US data (ADP/NFP slowing), which is unusual — it suggests rates are being driven by term premium and fiscal supply more than growth optimism.
- **Editorial wedge:** Market Deviations / Narrative Deconstruction

#### 2. India’s domestic flows are absorbing record foreign selling — but the valuation puzzle remains
- **Why it matters now:** FIIs/FPIs have reportedly sold ~₹208,000–260,000 crore of Indian equities year-to-date, while DIIs have infused ~₹271,000 crore. India’s neutral weight in MSCI EM has fallen to a two-year low of 15.25%. Despite this, India VIX has compressed to sub-14 levels and the market is range-bound.
- **Primary asset classes affected:** Indian equities, INR, Indian government bonds (index inclusion), EM allocators.
- **What makes it structurally interesting:** India is becoming a domestic-flow-driven market in real time. The question is whether DII inflows are a structural buffer or a temporary source of return-chasing that could reverse if SIP growth slows.
- **What most commentary is missing:** Foreign selling is often framed as a bearish signal, but positioning is now light. The real test is earnings — India’s premium valuation (23x FY27 forward vs 15x EM average) needs earnings confirmation to attract foreign capital back.
- **Editorial wedge:** Market Mechanics / Narrative Deconstruction

#### 3. The US-Iran interim deal is removing the war premium from oil — and from gold
- **Why it matters now:** On 18 June 2026 the US and Iran formally signed an interim peace agreement. Brent has fallen toward $78/b and WTI near $76/b as the Strait of Hormuz reopens gradually. Gold, which had spiked on safe-haven demand, is now back around $4,330/oz and under pressure from the Fed’s hawkish tone.
- **Primary asset classes affected:** Crude oil, gold, energy equities, inflation expectations, INR (import bill).
- **What makes it structurally interesting:** The speed of premium removal is as important as the deal itself. It tests whether the 2025–2026 commodity rallies were mostly geopolitical rent or reflected deeper supply/demand shifts.
- **What most commentary is missing:** Inventories remain tight, especially US crude stockpiles near operational minimums. A reopened Hormuz adds supply, but the underlying market may still be undersupplied if OPEC+ does not unwind cuts smoothly.
- **Editorial wedge:** Narrative Deconstruction / Market Deviations

#### 4. China’s growth-stabilisation push is easing financial conditions but not solving deflation
- **Why it matters now:** CMBI’s 2026 outlook projects China’s 10-year government bond yield rising only marginally to ~1.9%, money-market rates falling, and the PBOC continuing RRR/LPR cuts amid weak credit demand and real-estate/consumption weakness. China’s export competitiveness remains strong while domestic demand lags.
- **Primary asset classes affected:** CNY, China rates, commodities (copper, iron ore), EM FX, global manufacturing.
- **What makes it structurally interesting:** China is exporting deflation through manufacturing while easing domestically. This creates a divergence between China’s external strength and internal weakness that matters for India, Southeast Asia, and global commodity demand.
- **What most commentary is missing:** The “China stimulus” narrative often overstates domestic reflation. The more important channel for global markets is whether China’s manufacturing push keeps global goods prices contained, complicating the Fed’s inflation fight.
- **Editorial wedge:** Long-cycle / Structural
- **Sources:**

### Secondary watchlist

The link between AI data-centre build-out and base-metals demand is plausible but may be overstated relative to tariff-driven arbitrage flows. *Missing:* clean demand attribution between AI, grid investment, and speculative positioning.
2. **US tariff regime after Supreme Court review** — The Supreme Court is reviewing the IEEPA basis for broad US tariffs. A ruling could reshape trade policy, but the case is slow-moving and the market has partly priced in continued friction. *Missing:* timing and scope of the ruling.
3. **European energy and carbon costs** — TTF gas has rallied on tight storage and Middle East risk; CBAM implementation in 2026 could raise import costs for steel/fertilisers. *Missing:* actual Q1–Q2 CBAM enforcement data.

### Historical / analog triggers

- **1994–1995 Fed tightening + yen weakness:** The last time the Fed hiked while Japan stayed loose, USD/JPY rose sharply and eventually contributed to the 1997 Asian crisis. Key difference: Asian economies now have larger FX reserves and less dollar debt.
- **2013 Taper Tantrum:** A sudden repricing of US rates hurt EM flows. Relevant because India’s current account and foreign positioning are stronger, but domestic valuations are much higher.
