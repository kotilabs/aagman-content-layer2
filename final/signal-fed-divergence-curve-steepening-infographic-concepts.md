## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The Bear Steepener Decomposition
- **Central insight:** The curve is steepening because the long end is rising faster than the short end, led by a rebuilding term premium, not by growth optimism or Fed easing.
- **Data:** 10-year Treasury yield 4.49%, 2-year 4.14%, spread +35 bps; SF Fed CR 10-year term premium 1.22% (up from 1.16% at the June FOMC), 2-year term premium 0.17% (flat); 10-year yield up ~50 bps since the start of the Middle East conflict.
- **Visualization type:** Split-panel chart. Left panel: yield-curve snapshot (10y vs 2y) with an earlier inverted curve for contrast. Right panel: stacked bar or line showing the 10-year term premium rising while the 2-year term premium is flat.
- **Composition notes:** Lead with the +35 bps spread in large type. Eye then moves to the two yield dots/bars, then to the term-premium decomposition. Payoff: the steepening is a long-end phenomenon.
- **Sources:** Advisor Perspectives (2 July 2026); Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco, Treasury Yield Premiums (7 July 2026); Federal Reserve FOMC minutes (8 July 2026) for the ~50 bps move since the Middle East conflict.

### Concept 2: The Fed Holds, the Market Doesn’t Believe It
- **Central insight:** The Fed kept rates unchanged in June, but its own projections and market pricing now treat a 2026 hike as likely.
- **Data:** Fed funds target range 3.50%–3.75% (hold, 12–0 vote); June SEP median year-end 2026 projection 3.8% (up from 3.4% in March); 9 of 18 FOMC participants project ≥1 hike; CME FedWatch 96.3% probability target range above 3.50%–3.75% by 9 December 2026; AhaSignals 51 bps gap between futures-implied 3.91% and Fed published median 3.4%.
- **Visualization type:** Divergence chart. Horizontal bands for the current target range, the SEP median, the dot-plot distribution, and the futures-implied rate. A small dot-plot replica showing 9 above / 8 unchanged / 1 below.
- **Composition notes:** Open with the unanimous hold, then show the projections jumping above it. Use red/green or above/below shading to make the divergence instant. Payoff: policy is static, but expectations have moved.
- **Sources:** Federal Reserve FOMC statement and Summary of Economic Projections (17 June 2026); CME FedWatch via Webull (late June/early July 2026); AhaSignals (6 July 2026).

### Concept 3: Soft Jobs, Steep Curve
- **Central insight:** Normally a weak jobs report lowers short yields and steepens the curve through recession fears; this time the curve steepened while the long end rose, suggesting a non-standard driver.
- **Data:** June nonfarm payrolls +57,000; unemployment 4.2%; labor-force participation 61.5% (down 0.3 pp); prior payroll revisions −74,000; 10-year minus 2-year spread +35 bps; 10-year yield 4.49%.
- **Visualization type:** Tension panel. Left side: labor-market dashboard (payroll change, unemployment, participation revision). Right side: yield-curve steepness. A connecting arrow or “but” label between them.
- **Composition notes:** Start with the weak jobs numbers to set up the puzzle, then reveal the curve steepening. Use contrast to emphasize that both happened at the same time. Payoff: the bond market is pricing something beyond a normal slowdown.
- **Sources:** U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, Employment Situation — June 2026 (2 July 2026); Advisor Perspectives (2 July 2026).

### Concept 4: The Global Divergence
- **Central insight:** While the Fed is on hold, the ECB and the Bank of Japan tightened in June, widening policy gaps and pressuring the yen to a multi-decade low.
- **Data:** Fed funds 3.50%–3.75% (hold); ECB deposit facility rate 2.25% (+25 bps, first hike since September 2023); BoJ policy rate 1.0% (+25 bps, highest since September 1995); USD/JPY ~162.70 on 1 July 2026 (weakest yen in ~40 years).
- **Visualization type:** Small-multiples cards or a three-column comparison, each column showing the central bank action, the rate level, and the date. A separate FX sparkline or large figure for USD/JPY 162.70.
- **Composition notes:** Lead with the Fed hold as the baseline, then show ECB and BoJ moving in the opposite direction. End with the yen at the bottom to tie the divergence to a market price. Payoff: the U.S. is no longer the only central bank in motion.
- **Sources:** Federal Reserve FOMC statement (17 June 2026); ECB Monetary policy decisions (11 June 2026); Trading Economics, Japan Interest Rate (source: Bank of Japan, July 2026); SureShotFX (1 July 2026).

### Concept 5: Who Absorbs $671 Billion?
- **Central insight:** A heavy Treasury borrowing calendar is colliding with a buyer base that has shifted from price-insensitive official holders to price-sensitive private investors.
- **Data:** U.S. Treasury Q3 FY2026 borrowing estimate $671 billion; Q2 estimate $189 billion (revised up $79 billion); April net TIC inflow $26.1 billion, with foreign private outflows of $23.1 billion and foreign official inflows of $49.2 billion; FOMC minutes noting the ownership shift from official-sector to private investors.
- **Visualization type:** Flow diagram. Treasury issuance on the left, buyer categories on the right, with width proportional to size. A smaller inset showing the official/private composition shift.
- **Composition notes:** Open with the $671 billion Q3 number. Draw the eye across the flow to the buyer base, emphasizing the modest total inflow and the official/private split. Payoff: supply is large and the marginal buyer has changed.
- **Sources:** Quiver Quantitative / U.S. Treasury (4 May 2026); U.S. Treasury / Reuters via Yahoo Finance (18 June 2026); Federal Reserve FOMC minutes (8 July 2026).

## What to avoid

- **Do not visualize the 1994–95 or 2013 analogues as primary concepts.** They are useful interpretive context, but the artifact provides only narrative comparison, not directly comparable time-series data. Using them would require additional sourcing.
- **Do not lead with CMBI’s 2026 forecast (GDP 1.8%, unemployment 4.5%).** It is a December 2025 outlook and has been partly contradicted by newer data; it is better as background than as a headline data point.
- **Do not treat CME FedWatch probabilities as certainty.** They are market-implied, time-stamped probabilities. Use them only alongside the explicit date and label them as market pricing.
- **Do not overstate the TIC data.** April is a single month and the headline inflow includes official buyers; the private outflow is the more interesting number and should be shown in that context.
- **Do not use vague “highest since” labels without dates.** For example, the BoJ rate is the highest since September 1995 and the yen level is the weakest in roughly 40 years—include those dates explicitly.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with **Concepts 1, 2, and 3** as the strongest visual case. Together they isolate the mechanism (bear steepener), the policy-market tension (Fed hold vs hike pricing), and the macro puzzle (soft jobs vs steep curve). **Concept 4** is a viable secondary option if the piece needs global context, and **Concept 5** is best used only if the final story wants to emphasize fiscal supply. Avoid combining all five into one graphic; the signal has several moving parts and clarity is better served by focused single-insight visuals.
