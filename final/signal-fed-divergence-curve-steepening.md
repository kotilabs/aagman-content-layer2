## 1. Signal restatement

In the first week of July 2026 the U.S. Treasury yield curve is steepening even as the U.S. labor market shows unmistakable signs of cooling. The 10-year Treasury yield finished 2 July at 4.49% and the 2-year at 4.14%, leaving the 10-year minus 2-year spread at +35 basis points after a long stretch of inversion. The June employment report showed nonfarm payrolls rising only +57,000, unemployment at 4.2% after ticking down from 4.3% in May, and the labor-force participation rate falling 0.3 percentage point to 61.5%. Yet the Federal Reserve held the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% on 17 June and the new Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) shifted the median year-end 2026 rate projection to 3.8%, with nine of eighteen participants expecting at least one hike. At the same time the European Central Bank hiked all key rates by 25 basis points on 11 June and the Bank of Japan raised its policy rate to 1.0% on 16 June. The signal is the divergence: long-dated U.S. yields are being pulled higher by term-premium and fiscal-supply forces while the domestic activity data argue the other way, producing a steepening curve whose drivers are disputed.

## 4. Mechanism

The yield curve can be decomposed into an expected-policy-rate component, an inflation-expectations component, and a term premium. In the current episode the short end is broadly anchored by the Fed’s hold at 3.50%–3.75%, while the long end is being pushed up by forces that are distinct from growth optimism. If nominal yields are rising while 10-year TIPS yields and inflation breakevens from the Fed's H.15 release are stable or soft, the move is more clearly a real-yield/term-premium story; those data would reinforce the core thesis but are not included here.

**Term-premium rebuild.** The San Francisco Fed’s CR model puts the 10-year term premium at 1.22% as of 7 July 2026, up from 1.16% on the day of the June FOMC meeting and well above the zero-or-negative readings of the quantitative-easing era. The 2-year term premium has stayed flat around 0.17%. Because the long-end premium is rising while the short-end premium is stable, the curve is steepening in a "bear steepener" pattern. The FOMC minutes explicitly flag that Treasury ownership has shifted from relatively price-insensitive official-sector holders to more price-sensitive private investors, a transition that raises the compensation required to absorb duration risk. Term-premium estimates are not directly observable and can vary materially across models (e.g., the New York Fed ACM or Kim-Wright estimates), so treating any single model as precise would overstate the case.

**Fiscal supply.** The Treasury’s May 2026 quarterly refunding estimates projected $671 billion of privately-held net marketable borrowing in Q3 FY2026, on top of $189 billion in Q2. With the Federal Reserve running down its balance sheet only through principal reinvestment (and prepared to buy bills only to maintain ample reserves), a larger share of that supply must be absorbed by price-sensitive domestic and foreign private investors. April TIC data showed a net inflow of only $26.1 billion, with private foreign investors actually net sellers of short-term and banking claims; official buyers provided the offset.

**Central-bank divergence.** The ECB and BoJ are both tightening while the Fed is on hold. That divergence has widened rate differentials: the dollar has appreciated against the yen to roughly 162.70, and the U.S. 2-year yield has risen more than comparable sovereign yields in other advanced economies. The divergence matters for the Treasury curve because it affects global demand for U.S. duration and because it raises the geopolitical-risk and inflation-risk premia embedded in long-dated yields.

**Fed credibility / communication regime change.** The June statement was shorter, dropped easing-bias language, and omitted the previous employment-side qualifiers. Chair Warsh’s arrival and the planned communications task forces have introduced uncertainty about the Fed’s reaction function. AhaSignals records a 51-basis-point gap between the market-implied rate (3.91%) and the Fed’s published SEP median (3.4%), suggesting investors do not fully believe the Fed will stop at one hike if inflation stays elevated.

**Labor-market softness as a tension, not a driver.** Normally a +57,000 payroll print, falling participation, and downward revisions would push the front end lower and steepen the curve through recession fears. Here the curve is steepening because the long end is rising faster than the short end. That mismatch is the core of the signal: the bond market is pricing something other than—or in addition to—a conventional slowdown.

## 5. Competing interpretations

**A. Growth and AI optimism are keeping long yields elevated.**
- *Evidence:* FOMC minutes describe real GDP expanding at a solid pace, AI-related investment boosting data-center and high-tech equipment demand, and the S&P 500 rising nearly 6% over the intermeeting period. JPMorgan AM’s 10-year fair-value range of 4.00%–4.50% can be read as consistent with a resilient economy.
- *Counter:* The June payroll report and downward revisions undercut the "resilient labor market" story. CMBI forecasts U.S. growth slowing from 1.9% in 2025 to 1.8% in 2026 and unemployment rising to 4.5%. If the economy were genuinely accelerating, the 2-year yield would likely be rising with the 10-year; instead the spread is widening because the long end is leading.

**B. Term premium and fiscal supply are repricing the long end independently of growth.**
- *Evidence:* The SF Fed CR model shows the 10-year term premium rising to 1.22% while the 2-year term premium is flat. The FOMC minutes note the ownership shift from official to private holders. Treasury’s $671 billion Q3 borrowing estimate is large, and April TIC inflows were modest once official flows are stripped out.
- *Counter:* Long-run inflation expectations remain well anchored near 2% (per the FOMC minutes and surveys), and the Fed’s balance-sheet policy is not an active quantitative-tightening drag. If supply were the only force, one might expect more weakness in auction metrics; 5-year TIPS auctions have reportedly been well received.

**C. The Fed has lost some credibility under a new chair, and the market is pricing a hawkish reaction function.**
- *Evidence:* The June statement was shortened and dropped easing-bias language. Chair Warsh announced five internal reviews. AhaSignals shows a 51-basis-point gap between futures-implied rates and the SEP median, and CME FedWatch prices a 96.3% chance that rates will be above the current range by December 2026.
- *Counter:* The FOMC vote was unanimous, with no dissents. Long-run inflation expectations have not de-anchored. The gap could simply reflect markets pricing a risk premium around a close call, not a loss of confidence.

**D. The curve is steepening into a late-cycle slowdown, as it often does before recessions.**
- *Evidence:* The 10-year minus 2-year spread turned positive after being deeply inverted, a pattern that has historically preceded recessions with a lag. The June jobs report showed weaker hiring, falling participation, and long-term unemployment up 286,000 over the year. CMBI sees growth decelerating in the second half of 2026.
- *Counter:* Unemployment is still low, layoffs remain contained, and the Fed’s staff forecast still sees GDP growth near potential. This is not yet a classic bull steepener driven by front-end rate-cut pricing; it is a long-end-led steepener.

## 6. Cross-asset implications

**Rates and fixed income.** Higher 10-year yields and a positive term premium raise the cost of long-duration borrowing. Mortgages, corporate debt, and municipal issuance all face higher benchmark rates. The Freddie Mac 30-year fixed mortgage rate was reported at 6.43% in early July. Duration-heavy portfolios are exposed to further steepening if the long-end premium continues to rebuild.

**Equities.** Higher yields are a headwind to equity valuations, especially for long-duration growth stocks, but the market has so far focused on AI-driven earnings. The S&P 500 rose nearly 6% between the April and June FOMC meetings even as yields climbed. A sustained rise in real yields—rather than nominal yields driven by inflation expectations—would pressure multiples more directly.

**Foreign exchange.** The wide U.S.–Japan rate differential has pushed USD/JPY to roughly 162.70, a 40-year yen low, supporting carry trades and dollar strength against the yen. The ECB hike complicates the dollar–euro picture: the euro area is tightening into a growth downgrade, so EUR/USD may be more sensitive to relative growth surprises than to rate differentials alone.

**Credit.** The FOMC minutes note that gross inflows to business development companies slowed sharply in Q2 and that net inflows were likely turning negative amid redemption requests. Corporate bond issuance has remained solid, led by AI-related investment, but leveraged-loan and private-credit markets are showing signs of strain. A further rise in Treasury term premia would widen credit spreads, particularly for lower-rated borrowers.

**Commodities and inflation hedges.** Oil prices had fallen on optimism around a U.S.–Iran memorandum of understanding, but the conflict remains the key source of supply-shock inflation in the Fed’s forecast. Gold has been volatile; spot prices were around $4,165–$4,195 per ounce in early July. If fiscal-supply concerns dominate, gold and inflation-linked bonds could benefit even if nominal yields stay elevated.

## 7. Historical analogues

**1994–95 Fed tightening / Japan easing divergence.** In 1994 the Greenspan Fed raised the federal funds rate from 3% to 6% in a preemptive tightening cycle, while the Bank of Japan was cutting rates toward post-war lows. The episode is remembered as the one recent hiking cycle that did not precede a recession. It also produced a sharp rise in Treasury yields and a stronger dollar against the yen. The analogue today is imperfect: the Fed is on hold, not hiking, and Japan is now tightening. But the shared element is a large trans-Pacific rate differential driving currency and curve dynamics independent of synchronous global growth.
- Sources: Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis Review, "The 1994-95 FOMC Tightening Episode," 2025, https://fraser.stlouisfed.org/files/docs/publications/frbslreview/rev_stls_2025_v107_n06.pdf; Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco, "New Measures of Japanese Monetary Policy," 9 August 1996, https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/1996/08/new-measures-of-japanese-monetary-policy/

**2013 Taper Tantrum.** From May to early September 2013 the 10-year Treasury yield rose about 140 basis points after then-Chair Bernanke signaled a possible reduction in Fed bond purchases. The move was driven by a surge in term premiums rather than by a rise in inflation expectations or a change in the near-term policy-rate path. It demonstrated how quickly long yields can reprice when a price-insensitive buyer (the Fed) is perceived to be stepping back. The current episode differs because the Fed is not actively tapering large-scale asset purchases; the parallel is that a shift in the marginal buyer—from official to private—can move the long end sharply even when the front end is stable.
- Sources: MarketWatch, "Is this a repeat of the 2013 taper tantrum?" 14 May 2015, https://www.marketwatch.com/story/is-this-a-repeat-of-the-2013-taper-tantrum-2015-05-14; Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System, "The Treasury Tantrum of 2023," 2024, https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-treasury-tantrum-of-2023-20240903.html

## 8. Known, unknown, and unknowable

**Known.**
- The 10-year Treasury yield is near the top of JPMorgan AM’s 4.00%–4.50% fair-value range, with the 10-year minus 2-year spread positive at +35 bps.
- The Fed held rates in June but shifted its 2026 dot-plot median to imply at least one hike; 9 of 18 participants now project a hike.
- The ECB and BoJ hiked in June, widening policy divergence with the U.S.
- The June jobs report was soft: +57,000 payrolls, 4.2% unemployment, and a 0.3 pp drop in participation.
- Term-premium estimates have risen at the long end while the short-end premium has been stable.
- Treasury faces a heavy Q3 FY2026 borrowing calendar ($671 billion).

**Unknown.**
- Whether the June labor-market softness is a temporary blip or the start of a broader slowdown.
- Whether inflation will remain sticky around 3.5%–4% or fade as energy-supply shocks resolve.
- How much of the current 10-year term premium will persist if fiscal policy or foreign demand shifts.
- How Chair Warsh will communicate policy and whether the FOMC will deliver the hikes the market has priced.

**Unknowable.**
- The evolution of the Middle East conflict and any further supply disruptions.
- The pace and productivity payoff of the AI investment boom.
- The future appetite of foreign official and private buyers for U.S. Treasuries.
- The location of the neutral real interest rate in an AI- and debt-heavy economy.

## 9. Open questions

1. If the June payroll weakness is confirmed in July and August, will the FOMC’s hiking projections unwind, or will the committee treat soft labor data as secondary to sticky inflation?
2. Is the rise in the 10-year term premium a durable repricing of U.S. duration risk, or will it reverse once Treasury supply is absorbed and Middle East uncertainty fades?
3. How will foreign demand for Treasuries respond if the dollar continues to strengthen and the U.S.–Japan rate gap stays wide?
4. Will the ECB’s hike into a growth downgrade prove to be a one-off response to the energy shock or the start of a sustained tightening cycle?
5. Does the new Fed communications regime under Chair Warsh increase or decrease the risk of a 1994-style or 2013-style bond-market repricing?
