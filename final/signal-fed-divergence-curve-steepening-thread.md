1/
The U.S. Treasury curve is steepening into a jobs slowdown.

10-year yields closed at 4.49% on 2 July; 2-year yields at 4.14%. The spread turned positive at +35 basis points after a long inversion. Yet June nonfarm payrolls printed +57,000, unemployment was 4.2% after ticking down from 4.3% in May, and the participation rate fell 0.3 percentage point to 61.5%.

The bond market is pricing something stranger than a recession.

2/
The June employment report was soft across the board.

+57,000 payrolls was the weakest monthly gain in months. Prior April–May revisions took away another 74,000. Average hourly earnings rose 0.3% month-over-month and 3.5% year-over-year, enough to keep the Fed nervous but not enough to suggest demand is overheating.

A slowing labor market normally drags the front end lower. This time it did not — but May payrolls were strong (+172,000 initially, revised to +129,000), so June looks more like a noisy single print than an abrupt trend break.

3/
The Fed held the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% on 17 June.

But the June Summary of Economic Projections lifted the median year-end 2026 rate to 3.8%, up from 3.4% in March. Nine of eighteen FOMC participants now project at least one 25-basis-point hike this year; only one sees a cut.

The committee is not flinching at one bad jobs report.

4/
The SEP's inflation forecast was revised up, but that is not why the curve is steepening.

The 2026 PCE inflation forecast was lifted to 3.6% and core PCE to 3.3%, both up from 2.7% in March. Real GDP growth was trimmed to 2.2%. That is a hawkish signal, but the curve steepening is being driven by term premium, fiscal supply, and central-bank divergence — not by a de-anchoring of inflation expectations.

A new regime is being assembled while the data are still ambiguous.

5/
This is not a bull steepener pricing Fed easing.

It is a bear steepener driven by the long end — term premium, fiscal supply, and central-bank divergence are pushing 10-year yields higher while the front end stays anchored.

The curve is repricing U.S. duration risk, not U.S. recession risk.

6/
Term premium explains the shape.

The San Francisco Fed’s CR model puts the 10-year term premium at 1.22% as of 7 July, up from 1.16% at the June FOMC meeting. The 2-year term premium is flat at 0.17%.

Almost the entire steepening is coming from compensation for holding long-dated bonds.

7/
Fiscal supply is the other force.

The Treasury projects $671 billion of privately-held net marketable borrowing in Q3 FY2026, on top of $189 billion in Q2. The Fed is only reinvesting principal, so price-sensitive private investors must absorb most of the new duration.

April TIC data showed a net inflow of just $26.1 billion, with private foreign investors net sellers of short-term claims.

8/
Central banks are diverging while the Fed waits.

The ECB raised all key rates by 25 basis points on 11 June, and the Bank of Japan lifted its policy rate to 1.0% on 16 June — the highest since 1995. The widening U.S.–Japan rate gap has pushed USD/JPY to roughly 162.70, a 40-year yen low.

Global tightening is feeding U.S. currency strength and long-end volatility.

9/
Markets have moved further than the Fed.

CME FedWatch prices a 96.3% probability that the target range will be above 3.50%–3.75% by December 2026, with only an 11% chance priced for July and a 59% chance for October. AhaSignals records a 51-basis-point gap between futures-implied rates and the Fed’s published SEP median.

Futures-implied rates sit materially above the Fed’s published median — the market is pricing more tightening than the dot plot itself.

10/
The cross-asset read is already visible.

J.P. Morgan Asset Management raised its 10-year fair-value range to 4.00%–4.50%, with current yields at the upper bound. The 30-year fixed mortgage rate was reported near 6.43% in early July (Freddie Mac PMMS). BDC gross inflows slowed sharply in Q2, and leveraged-loan markets are showing strain.

Higher term premia are a tax on anything that borrows long.

11/
The tension is between two possible Julys.

If the labor-market softness is confirmed in the next two payroll reports, the FOMC’s hiking projections will look increasingly detached from the data. If inflation stays sticky near 3.5%–4%, the market’s 51-basis-point hawkish wedge will look like the more honest forecast.

Either way, the long end is no longer taking the Fed at its word.
