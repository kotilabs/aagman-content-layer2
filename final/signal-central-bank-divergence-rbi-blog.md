# The RBI’s Capital-Account Optionality: Why India Used External-Sector Tools as Global Central Banks Diverged

## When the world’s conductors stop beating the same time, India chose a different instrument

Imagine a family that has always followed the same monthly budget rule. For years, the parents and the grown children moved in rough agreement: when times were good, everyone saved a little more; when times were hard, everyone loosened. Then one month, three of the siblings announce different plans. One says she will probably spend less, even though her income is fine. Another says he will save more, even though his hours were just cut. The third says she is pausing all decisions after a spree of changes. The youngest child, watching all of this, does not change his own allowance at all. Instead, he calls relatives abroad, opens a separate account, and asks the household to redirect some money that was already coming in. He is managing the outside pressure without rewriting his internal budget.

That is more or less what happened in global central banking in mid-June 2026.

**In short:** in mid-June 2026 the Fed, ECB and RBA diverged. The RBI held the repo rate at 5.25% and opened its capital-account toolbox—FAR, NRI/OCI limits, PSU ECB swaps, FCNR(B) hedging, export-proceeds relief—so it could manage external pressure without hiking.

Central-bank divergence happens when major central banks move in conflicting directions because they face different inflation-growth mixes. India’s capital-account optionality is the choice to manage external pressure through regulatory and inflow tools while keeping the domestic policy rate unchanged, rather than hiking or cutting into a supply-side shock. Currencies price expected forward rates, not just current levels. When the [Fed’s median dot](/p/fed-june-2026-dot-plot) rose while the ECB and RBA held back, the dollar’s expected return improved. Rather than raise the repo rate into a supply-side inflation shock, the RBI used capital-account tools to attract dollars and ease pressure on the rupee.

---

## Context — the anchor dissolved

### What changed in mid-June 2026?

For most of the post-2008 era, the major central banks moved in something like rhythm. They eased into crises, tightened into recoveries, and paused when the outlook fogged. The direction was usually shared, and markets could price assets against a single global policy anchor.

In one week, that anchor fractured.

On 11 June, the European Central Bank raised all three key rates by 25 basis points — its first hike since September 2023 — even as it cut its euro-area growth forecast for 2026 to 0.8%. President Christine Lagarde called the decision unanimous and rejected the label of an "insurance hike." Five days later, the Reserve Bank of Australia left its cash rate at 4.35%, pausing after three rapid hikes in February, March and May, while keeping a conditional tightening bias. Then on 17 June, the Federal Reserve held the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% but shifted its median 2026 projection up to 3.8% from 3.4% in March. Nine of eighteen FOMC participants now saw at least one rate increase this year; six saw two or more. Only one expected a cut.

The message was not blanket disagreement. It was that each bank was responding to a different inflation-growth mix: the Fed to resilient U.S. demand and sticky services prices, the ECB to a Middle East energy shock that was already reversing, and the RBA to domestic demand that might be cracking after its recent hikes. There was no longer a single global rate path to price against.

Into this, the Reserve Bank of India held its 3–5 June MPC meeting and voted 6–0 to keep the repo rate at 5.25%, retaining its [neutral stance](/p/rbi-monetary-policy-watch). But the policy rate was only part of the story. The RBI opened what can fairly be called a capital-account toolbox.

---

## Mechanism — how the pieces connect

### How do capital-account tools defend the rupee without a rate hike?

Currency markets do not price current interest rates alone. They price the expected forward path of rates, adjusted for risk and liquidity. When the [Fed’s median dot](/p/fed-june-2026-dot-plot) rose while the ECB and RBA offered no firm commitment to further hikes, the expected return on dollar cash improved relative to alternatives. The dollar index broke above 100. EUR/USD fell below 1.15 even though the ECB had actually raised rates. That is the central FX principle: currencies move on the forward path, not the current change.

For an emerging economy with dollar-denominated energy imports and globally mobile portfolio capital, this creates a familiar pressure. A stronger dollar and higher U.S. front-end yields tighten global financial conditions. Capital drifts toward dollar assets. The domestic currency weakens. The textbook response is to raise domestic policy rates to retain capital and defend the currency.

The RBI rejected that path.

Its reasoning was structural, not doctrinaire. The MPC judged the inflation impulse as largely supply-side: fuel pass-through, monsoon risk, and the global energy shock. Headline CPI in March and April printed at 3.4% and 3.5%, while core inflation excluding precious metals ran at 2.1%–2.2%. Retail fuel prices had been raised cumulatively by 7.4% for petrol and 8.4% for diesel since May; the RBI estimated a direct inflation impact of about 36 basis points, with second-order effects still to come. Raising the repo rate would raise borrowing costs without lowering oil prices. Cutting might loosen inflation expectations. Holding at 5.25% preserved optionality in both directions.

But optionality on the domestic side still left the external side exposed. So the RBI deployed five capital-account and regulatory levers.

1. **[Fully Accessible Route](/p/india-government-bond-index-inclusion) expansion for government securities.** The RBI expanded FAR to include new 15-, 30- and 40-year issuances, and removed several short-term, concentration and individual-security limits on foreign portfolio investment under the General Route.

2. **Higher NRI and OCI equity limits.** The RBI raised NRI and OCI equity investment limits without requiring SEBI registration, and extended the same facility to all individual Persons Resident Outside India.

3. **Concessional forex swap window for PSU external commercial borrowings.** The RBI offered a swap window until 30 September 2026 to incentivise ECBs by public-sector undertakings.

4. **FCNR(B) hedging subsidy for banks.** The RBI offered to bear the full hedging cost until 30 September 2026 for authorised dealer banks raising fresh three-to-five-year FCNR(B) deposits.

5. **Export-proceeds relief.** The RBI restored the time for realisation of export proceeds to nine months.

Fresh three-to-five-year FCNR(B) deposits mobilised under the 8 June–30 September window are also exempt from CRR and SLR requirements, improving bank economics on top of the RBI's hedging subsidy.

The government added its own measure: an ordinance exempting foreign portfolio investors and the Bank for International Settlements from income tax on interest and capital gains arising from government securities, effective retrospectively from 1 April 2026.

The combined logic is to attract dollar inflows through rupee-asset demand, to subsidise foreign-currency borrowing for entities that can access it, and to accelerate the repatriation of dollars already earned. The aim is not to fix the rupee at a level. The RBI has repeatedly said it intervenes to curb excessive volatility, not to defend a band. The toolbox reduces the need for aggressive spot or forward intervention that would either drain reserves or tighten rupee liquidity.

That last point matters. Banking-system liquidity had tightened as FX intervention and seasonal currency leakage offset earlier easing. If the capital-account measures bring dollars in, they also bring rupees into the banking system and reduce the need for sterilised forward operations. The external and domestic halves of policy connect through liquidity, even when the repo rate does not move.

## Signal / deviation — what is unusual

### What made the RBI’s June 2026 response unusual?

The deviation is not that India held rates. It is that India chose to manage external pressure with the capital account rather than with the policy rate, at a moment when other central banks were either hiking or talking hawkishly.

| Central bank | Decision, mid-June 2026 | Policy rate | Forward signal | Growth-inflation context |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Federal Reserve | Hold, 17 Jun | 3.50%–3.75% | Median 2026 dot rose to 3.8%; 9 of 18 FOMC members expect ≥1 hike | Inflation projection up sharply; GDP growth trimmed |
| European Central Bank | Hike +25bp, 11 Jun | Deposit facility 2.25% | No firm commitment to follow-up; staff growth forecasts cut | Hiking into downgraded growth; energy-driven inflation |
| Reserve Bank of Australia | Hold, 16 Jun | 4.35% | Conditional tightening bias retained; pause after 3 hikes | Economy slowing as expected after rapid tightening |
| Reserve Bank of India | Hold, 5 Jun | Repo 5.25% | Neutral stance retained; capital-account tools deployed | Inflation seen as supply-side; growth forecast cut to 6.6% |

*Source: Federal Reserve, ECB, RBA, RBI, June 2026.*

In previous stress episodes, the RBI has often been forced to follow the Fed, or at least to signal that it would. This time it separated the two decisions. Domestic monetary policy would respond to domestic conditions. External-sector management would use external-sector instruments. The rupee would be managed for volatility, not for a level.

The immediate market reaction suggested the message was received. The rupee strengthened from near-record lows around 97 to 95.24 on policy day, and to 94.39 by 25 June. India VIX fell below 13. Foreign portfolio investors reportedly put ₹35,000 crore into Indian bonds by 23 June. None of this resolved the underlying tensions, but it showed that capital-account instruments can move prices when paired with tax clarity and regulatory relaxation.

## Interpretation spectrum — four plausible readings

The same set of facts supports more than one reading. The honest thing is to hold them all in tension.

*Note on bias: Aagman’s structural view is that India is in a long-term domestic-demand-driven bull market. That undercurrent makes the optionality reading intuitively appealing, but it is not evidence. Data can still contradict it.*

**Reading one: India is preserving optionality while developed-market central banks are trapped in harder trade-offs.** This is the RBI’s own framing. The inflation impulse is supply-side; growth is slowing; the repo rate is on hold; the capital-account toolbox handles the external account. India’s forex reserves stood at $682.3 billion as of 29 May, import cover was about eleven months, and gross FDI hit a record $94.5 billion in FY26. The external position is stronger than in past stress episodes, so the central bank has room to do this.

The risk to this reading is that Q3 FY27 inflation is projected at 5.9%, only ten basis points below the 6% upper tolerance band. If food or fuel prices spike, the MPC may still have to hike. And the capital-account measures are time-bound; they do not remove the structural pressure from a stronger dollar.

**Reading two: The RBI is behind the curve; capital-account tools are no substitute for rate defence if the dollar keeps rising.** The rupee had weakened from roughly ₹90 to near ₹97 in about a year. Net FPI outflows in FY27 so far were $13.7 billion, mostly from equities. A hold at 5.25% with inflation rising means the real policy rate is falling. If the Fed follows through on its dots, dollar pressure could overwhelm the inflow measures.

The counter is that the rupee rallied as oil prices collapsed. Analyst estimates for FCNR(B) mobilisation range from $40 billion to $50 billion. The toolbox may be larger than it looks, especially with a falling current-account deficit.

**Reading three: The developed-market divergence is a temporary energy-shock distortion; the durable story is India’s capital-account opening.** Fixed-income strategists tend to focus here. The FPI tax exemption applies specifically to government securities, not corporate bonds. The FAR expansion is calibrated to deepen the sovereign bond market and improve India’s position in global bond indices. Bond holdings under FAR reportedly rose from ₹3.23 lakh crore to ₹3.58 lakh crore within weeks. The capital-account measures are not merely currency defence; they are structural market-building.

The pushback is timing. The measures were announced during a period of rupee weakness and FPI equity outflows. Currency management is at least a co-objective. If the external pressure fades, some of the time-bound facilities will expire as scheduled.

**Reading four: This is early evidence of a multi-polar monetary order in which each major economy must build its own capital-account firewall.** Some structural macro commentators argue that the post-2008 convergence of central-bank behaviour was the exception, not the rule. The Fed, ECB and RBA are now on visibly different paths, and India is responding with unilateral, regulatory instruments tailored to its own vulnerabilities.

The weakness is that India still operates within a dollar-centric system. The measures attract dollars, not reduce dollar dependence. A genuine multi-polar order would require deeper local-currency settlement, changed reserve patterns, and broader currency-swap networks. Those are not yet visible.

Which reading is right? Probably parts of all four. The useful point is that the signal is ambiguous enough to support multiple narratives, which is exactly why the RBI’s optionality matters.

## Historical or structural memory — two framing devices

**The [2013 FCNR(B) swap](/p/fcnr-swap-2013-lessons).** In September 2013, then-RBI Governor Raghuram Rajan opened a concessional FCNR(B) swap window to attract NRI dollar deposits during a sharp rupee depreciation. Banks mobilised an estimated $26–34 billion — the RBI put the combined 2013 swap-window total at about $34 billion, with FCNR(B) deposits accounting for roughly $26 billion, according to later market estimates — helping stabilise the rupee after it had fallen from around ₹55 to ₹68 per dollar. The instrument is the same; the economics are not.

**2013 vs. 2026: FCNR(B) economics**

| | 2013 | 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| U.S. rates | Near zero | 3.50%–3.75% |
| Spread over dollar alternatives | >500 basis points | ~150–250 basis points |
| RBI terms | Concessional FCNR(B) swap window | Full hedging cost absorbed; CRR/SLR exempt |
| Rupee level | ~₹55 → ₹68 | ~₹90 → near ₹97 → 94.39 |
| Forex reserves / import cover | Low | $682.3 billion; ~11 months |
| Current-account deficit | Much wider | Narrower |

*Sources: RBI, Federal Reserve, market estimates. Used for perspective, not prediction.*

In 2013, U.S. rates were near zero and the spread over dollar alternatives was more than 500 basis points; in 2026, U.S. rates are around 4.5% and the spread is roughly 150–250 basis points. India’s macro fundamentals are also stronger: larger reserves, a smaller current-account deficit, and a healthier banking system. The analogue shows that NRI deposit windows can work, but the scale depends on the relative yield spread and global risk appetite, not just the RBI’s willingness to offer swaps.

**The 2015 Iran nuclear deal.** The Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action lifted some sanctions on Iran in exchange for nuclear limits. Iranian oil returned to market and Brent crude fell sharply over the following months. In June 2026, an interim U.S.–Iran memorandum reopened the Strait of Hormuz and allowed Iran to resume oil exports. Brent fell to below $73 by late June, a drop of roughly 21% over the month. The analogue shows how quickly a geopolitical risk premium can deflate, and how central-bank hiking rationale can evaporate with it. It also shows that implementation risk — mines, regional objections, domestic politics — can keep a residual premium in prices for months. History is a framing device, not a forecast.

## Implications for decision-makers

**What this affects.**

*Currency management.* The rupee is likely to be driven more by Fed dots, oil prices and FPI equity flows than by RBI rate changes in the near term. The RBI has telegraphed that it will manage volatility, not defend a level. That means gradual depreciation remains possible if fundamentals warrant it.

*Rates.* The front end of the Indian curve is anchored by the 5.25% repo hold, but the long end is sensitive to FPI demand under FAR and the G-Sec tax exemption. Corporate bond yields fell more than G-Secs immediately after the policy, reflecting the PSU ECB relief. If inflation prints near the RBI’s Q3 projection, expectations of a future hike could push short-end yields higher.

*Equities.* Rate-sensitive sectors — banks, NBFCs, autos, real estate — face a stable funding-cost environment but no fresh rate-cut tailwind. Banks with strong NRI franchises and those able to mobilise FCNR(B) deposits may see funding advantages. The broader market has been supported by domestic institutional buying offsetting foreign selling; the durability of that pattern depends on domestic flows.

*Commodities and the current account.* The collapse in [Brent crude](/p/oil-hormuz-risk-premium) is a large, positive terms-of-trade shock for India. Each $10 per barrel fall reduces the import bill and eases both the current account deficit and inflation. Gold faces headwinds from higher real U.S. yields and a stronger dollar, though sovereign default and war-cycle hedging demand is a separate floor.

*Credit.* PSUs and large corporates with access to ECB markets benefit from cheaper foreign-currency borrowing under the swap facilities. Smaller firms without that access remain dependent on domestic bank credit, where liquidity has tightened and transmission of past rate cuts is incomplete.

*RBI balance sheet.* The FCNR(B) swap is not costless. By absorbing roughly 280–300 basis points of hedging cost per annum, the RBI takes currency risk onto its own balance sheet; a material rupee depreciation over the 3–5-year deposit tenor would create a contingent liability.

**What this does not affect.**

It does not provide a reason to change a long-term asset allocation based on a single policy week. It does not tell anyone what to buy or sell. It does not resolve whether the Fed will actually hike, whether the Iran deal will hold, or whether India’s Q3 inflation will breach 6%. Those remain open.

---

## Open question

If [Brent crude](/p/oil-hormuz-risk-premium) stays near $70–$75 and the Fed’s hawkish dot plot turns out to have been written on the assumption of an energy shock that has already reversed, then the dollar pressure on India may ease without the RBI ever having to move the repo rate. The capital-account toolbox would look prescient. But if the Fed follows the dots anyway, or if Q3 inflation prints close to 6%, the RBI may find that optionality is not the same thing as escape velocity.

So the question is not whether the RBI made the right call on 5 June. The question is: **how long can a central bank manage its external account through the capital account before domestic inflation forces it to pick a side?**

That is the tension worth watching.
