# LinkedIn Carousel — Fed Divergence and Curve Steepening

*Promotes the long-form essay "The Curve Un-Inverts: Reading the Treasury Steepening of July 2026"*

---

## Slide 1

**Kicker:** The signal

**Headline:** The curve un-inverted on the same day the labor market cracked.

**Body:** For more than two years the 10-year Treasury yielded less than the 2-year — the market's way of saying rate cuts were coming. On 2 July 2026 the 10-year minus 2-year spread turned positive. The June employment report, released the same day, was the weakest in months. The two readings usually move together. This time they did not.

**Hero stat:** 10-year minus 2-year spread: +35 bps on 2 July 2026 — Advisor Perspectives / FRED

**Speaker note:** Names the shift — a curve un-inversion colliding with soft jobs data — and sets up the puzzle the essay tries to solve.

## Slide 2

**Kicker:** The jobs report

**Headline:** The economy added 57,000 jobs. The curve steepened anyway.

**Body:** June nonfarm payrolls rose only 57,000, unemployment was 4.2% after ticking down from 4.3% in May, and the labor-force participation rate fell 0.3 percentage point to 61.5%. Prior-month payrolls were revised down by a combined 74,000. Ordinarily that combination would push the front end lower on recession fears. Instead the long end rose faster than the short end.

**Hero stat:** +57,000 nonfarm payrolls; 4.2% unemployment; 61.5% participation — U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics

**Speaker note:** Establishes the domestic-activity side of the contradiction.

## Slide 3

**Kicker:** The Fed

**Headline:** The Fed held rates — but its own projections moved toward a hike.

**Body:** The FOMC kept the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% on 17 June. The new Summary of Economic Projections, however, raised the median year-end 2026 rate to 3.8% from 3.4% in March. Nine of eighteen participants now project at least one 25-basis-point hike this year.

**Hero stat:** Median year-end 2026 fed funds projection: 3.8%; 9 of 18 FOMC participants project ≥1 hike — Federal Reserve

**Speaker note:** Shows the Fed is not cutting into the weakness; if anything, it is leaning the other way.

## Slide 4

**Kicker:** The divergence

**Headline:** While the Fed held, the ECB and Bank of Japan hiked.

**Body:** The European Central Bank raised all three key rates by 25 basis points on 11 June, taking the deposit facility rate to 2.25%. The Bank of Japan lifted its short-term policy rate to 1.0% on 16 June — the highest level since 1995. Washington is stationary; Frankfurt and Tokyo are moving.

**Hero stat:** ECB deposit facility rate: 2.25%; BoJ policy rate: 1.0%, highest since 1995 — ECB, Trading Economics / Bank of Japan

**Speaker note:** Introduces the global central-bank divergence pulling on long-dated U.S. yields.

## Slide 5

**Kicker:** The thesis

**Headline:** This isn't growth optimism. It's a repricing of *duration risk*.

**Body:** A genuinely accelerating economy would lift the 2-year yield along with the 10-year. What happened is the opposite: the 10-year term premium is rising while the 2-year term premium is flat. The curve is steepening because the long end is carrying more risk compensation, not because the short end is pricing faster growth.

**Hero stat:** SF Fed 10-year term premium: 1.22%; 2-year term premium: 0.17% — Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco

**Speaker note:** The explicit pivot — rules out the easy growth narrative and names the real mechanism.

## Slide 6

**Kicker:** The mechanism

**Headline:** Treasury supply is landing on a more price-sensitive buyer.

**Body:** The Fed is allowing only principal reinvestment and buying bills only to maintain ample reserves. The Treasury projects $671 billion in privately-held net marketable borrowing in Q3 FY2026. The FOMC minutes note that ownership has shifted from relatively price-insensitive official-sector holders toward more price-sensitive private investors — a change that "could have implications for the term premium component of yields."

**Hero stat:** $671 billion in Q3 FY2026 privately-held net marketable borrowing — U.S. Treasury via Quiver Quantitative

**Speaker note:** Connects fiscal supply and the ownership shift to the term-premium rebuild.

## Slide 8

**Kicker:** Historical memory

**Headline:** The long end has a history of moving for reasons unrelated to the next GDP print.

**Body:** In 1994–95 the Greenspan Fed raised the federal funds rate from 3% to 6% while the Bank of Japan was cutting, producing a trans-Pacific rate differential that moved yields and the yen without a recession. In 2013 the taper tantrum drove the 10-year Treasury yield up about 140 basis points in four months through term premiums alone.

**Hero stat:** 1994–95: Fed funds rate 3% to 6%; 2013 taper tantrum: 10-year yield +140 bps — Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, MarketWatch

**Speaker note:** Uses history to show that long-end-led repricings are not anomalies.

**Body:** USD/JPY traded near 162.70 on 1 July, the weakest yen level in roughly four decades. A sustained rebuild in term premium does not stay inside the Treasury market.

**Speaker note:** Shows the cross-asset transmission of the curve move.
