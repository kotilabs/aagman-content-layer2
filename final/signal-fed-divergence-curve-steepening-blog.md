# Why the U.S. Treasury Curve Is Steepening in July 2026 — And What Fed-ECB-BoJ Divergence Has to Do With It

## Opening: the restaurant with two waitlists

Imagine a restaurant that takes reservations six months out. Next week's diary has emptied out, but November is fuller than ever. Ask the short-term waitlist and the place looks half-dead. Ask the long-term diary and it looks impossible to get into. Both readings are true. They just answer different questions.

The Treasury market is sending a similar signal. The front end reads a cooling job market and a Federal Reserve on hold. The long end reads something else: a repricing of U.S. duration risk, heavy government borrowing, and a world where the ECB and Bank of Japan are tightening while Washington is not. The 10-year minus 2-year spread has flipped positive after a long inversion. That is not a single verdict. It is two waitlists in the same book.

Yield curve steepening happens when the gap between long- and short-term Treasury yields widens; when the long end leads the move, it is called a bear steepener.

The 10-year minus 2-year Treasury spread turned positive in early July 2026 because long yields rose faster than short yields. That is a bear steepener: the market is repricing term premium and fiscal supply, not celebrating growth. It is unusual because it happened alongside a soft jobs report and a Fed on hold.

## Context: what changed, and why now?

For roughly two years the U.S. Treasury curve was inverted: 2-year yields sat above 10-year yields, a configuration that usually means the market expects rate cuts ahead. That began to unwind in early July 2026. On 2 July the 10-year Treasury closed at 4.49% and the 2-year at 4.14%, leaving the spread at +35 basis points — positive for the first time in a long while.[^1]

The timing is awkward. The June employment report, released on the same day, was soft. Nonfarm payrolls rose only +57,000, unemployment was 4.2% after ticking down from 4.3% in May, and the labor-force participation rate fell 0.3 percentage point to 61.5%. Prior-month payrolls were revised down by a combined 74,000.[^2] Ordinarily that combination would push the front end lower and steepen the curve through recession pricing. Instead the curve steepened because the long end rose faster than the short end. May payrolls were initially reported at +172,000 and later revised to +129,000, so the June softness followed a strong prior month rather than an abrupt trend break.

The Federal Reserve did not help settle the argument. At its 17 June meeting it held the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75%, but the Summary of Economic Projections raised the median year-end 2026 rate to 3.8% from 3.4% in March. Nine of eighteen FOMC participants now project at least one 25-basis-point hike this year.[^3] Chair Kevin Warsh, presiding over his first meeting, also announced five internal task forces to review communications, the balance sheet, data measurement, productivity and employment, and the inflation framework.[^4]

Meanwhile the rest of the world moved the other way. The European Central Bank raised all three key rates by 25 basis points on 11 June, taking the deposit facility rate to 2.25%.[^5] The Bank of Japan lifted its short-term policy rate to 1.0% on 16 June, the highest level since 1995.[^6] The Fed is on hold while two other major central banks are tightening. That divergence is part of what is pulling long-dated U.S. yields higher.

## Mechanism: how the curve actually works

### What is term premium and why is it rising?

A Treasury yield is not one thing. It can be split into three parts: expected future short-term rates, inflation expectations, and a term premium — the extra compensation investors demand for locking money up for a decade or more. In the current episode the pieces are moving in different directions. If the rise in nominal yields is accompanied by stable or soft inflation breakevens from the Fed's H.15 release, the story becomes a real-yield/term-premium repricing rather than a surge in inflation expectations.

### The short end is anchored by the Fed

The 2-year yield is a rough proxy for where the market thinks the federal funds rate will be over the next two years. With the Fed holding at 3.50%–3.75% and the June jobs report soft, the front end has not risen much. The San Francisco Fed's CR model estimates the 2-year term premium at just 0.17%, essentially unchanged from the June FOMC meeting.[^7]

### The long end is being repriced by supply and ownership

The 10-year term premium, by contrast, has risen to 1.22% from 1.16% at the time of the June FOMC meeting.[^7] That may sound small, but term premiums were zero or negative for much of the quantitative-easing era. The FOMC minutes explicitly note that Treasury ownership has shifted from relatively price-insensitive official-sector holders toward more price-sensitive private investors, and that this transition "could have implications for the term premium component of yields."[^8] Term-premium estimates are model-dependent; the San Francisco Fed CR model is one of several approaches and the exact level can vary materially across models.

### Fiscal supply is adding duration to the market

The U.S. Treasury projects $671 billion in privately-held net marketable borrowing in the third quarter of fiscal 2026, on top of a revised-up $189 billion in Q2.[^9] With the Fed allowing only principal reinvestment and standing ready to buy bills only to maintain ample reserves, a larger share of that supply must be absorbed by domestic and foreign private investors.

### Global divergence changes the marginal buyer

The ECB and BoJ hikes have widened rate differentials against the dollar in some directions and narrowed them in others. USD/JPY traded near 162.70 on 1 July, the weakest yen level in roughly four decades.[^10] A stronger dollar can reduce the foreign-exchange-hedged return for foreign buyers of U.S. Treasuries, which matters when the marginal buyer is already shifting from official to private hands.

## Signal: what is unusual relative to history and expectations

The unusual part is not that the curve steepened. Curves steepen all the time. The unusual part is that it steepened through the long end while the domestic activity data argued the other way.

### Why is the curve steepening when the jobs report was weak?

A few facts stand out:

- The 10-year yield has risen about 50 basis points since the start of the Middle East conflict and about 20 basis points since the April FOMC meeting.[^8]
- The 10-year minus 2-year spread moved from deeply inverted to +35 basis points even as payroll growth collapsed to +57,000.
- The Fed's own median projection implies at least one hike, yet the market prices even more: CME FedWatch showed a 96.3% probability that the target range would be above 3.50%–3.75% by December 2026, with only an 11% probability for July and a 59% probability for October.[^11]
- AhaSignals' Fed Rate Fragility Index records a 51-basis-point gap between the futures-implied rate (3.91%) and the Fed's published SEP median (3.4%).[^12]
- J.P. Morgan Asset Management raised its 10-year fair-value range to 4.00%–4.50%, placing the current 4.49% right at the upper edge.[^13]

The signal is a mismatch. The bond market is pricing something beyond a conventional slowdown. That something could be term premium, fiscal supply, Fed credibility, or a combination. But it is not the simple story of "bad jobs data means rate cuts."

## Interpretation spectrum: four plausible readings

No single narrative explains all of this. Here are four readings, each with evidence and each with a gap.

### A. Growth and AI optimism are keeping long yields elevated

The case: FOMC minutes describe real GDP expanding at a solid pace, AI-related investment boosting data-center and high-tech equipment demand, and the S&P 500 rising nearly 6% over the intermeeting period.[^8] JPMorgan AM's 10-year fair-value range of 4.00%–4.50% can be read as consistent with a resilient economy, though with 4.49% at the top of that range the growth-optimism reading has little room to run.

The gap: If the economy were genuinely accelerating, the 2-year yield would likely be rising with the 10-year. Instead the spread is widening because the long end is leading. The June payroll report and downward revisions also undercut the "resilient labor market" story.

### B. Term premium and fiscal supply are repricing the long end independently of growth

The case: The SF Fed CR model shows the 10-year term premium rising while the 2-year term premium is flat. The FOMC minutes flag the ownership shift from official to private holders. Treasury's $671 billion Q3 borrowing estimate is large, and April TIC inflows were modest once official flows are stripped out.[^14]

The gap: Long-run inflation expectations remain well anchored near 2%, and the Fed's balance-sheet policy is not an active quantitative-tightening drag. If supply were the only force, one might expect more weakness in auction metrics.

### C. The Fed has lost some credibility under a new chair, and the market is pricing a hawkish reaction function

The case: The June statement was shortened and dropped easing-bias language. Chair Warsh announced five internal reviews. AhaSignals shows a 51-basis-point gap between futures-implied rates and the SEP median, and CME FedWatch prices a 96.3% chance that rates will be above the current range by December 2026.[^11][^12]

The gap: The FOMC vote was unanimous, with no dissents. Long-run inflation expectations have not de-anchored. The gap could simply reflect markets pricing a risk premium around a close call, not a loss of confidence.

### Is the steepening curve signaling recession?

### D. The curve is steepening into a late-cycle slowdown, as it often does before recessions

The case: The 10-year minus 2-year spread turned positive after being deeply inverted, a pattern that has historically preceded recessions with a lag. The June jobs report showed weaker hiring, falling participation, and long-term unemployment up 286,000 over the year.[^2]

The gap: This is not yet a classic bull steepener driven by front-end rate-cut pricing. It is a long-end-led steepener. Unemployment is still low, layoffs remain contained, and the Fed's staff forecast still sees GDP growth near potential.

These readings are not mutually exclusive. The curve may be steepening for more than one reason at the same time, which is precisely why the signal is hard to trade cleanly.

## Historical memory: 1994–95 and 2013

History is useful for framing, not forecasting. Two episodes keep surfacing in discussions of the current curve.

**The 1994–95 Fed tightening cycle.** In 1994 the Greenspan Fed raised the federal funds rate from 3% to 6% in a preemptive tightening cycle, while the Bank of Japan was cutting rates toward post-war lows. The episode is remembered as the one recent hiking cycle that did not precede a recession. It also produced a sharp rise in Treasury yields and a stronger dollar against the yen. The analogue today is imperfect: the Fed is on hold, not hiking, and Japan is now tightening. But the shared element is a large trans-Pacific rate differential driving currency and curve dynamics independent of synchronous global growth.[^15]

**The 2013 Taper Tantrum.** From May to early September 2013 the 10-year Treasury yield rose about 140 basis points after then-Chair Bernanke signaled a possible reduction in Fed bond purchases. The move was driven by a surge in term premiums rather than by a rise in inflation expectations or a change in the near-term policy-rate path. It demonstrated how quickly long yields can reprice when a price-insensitive buyer is perceived to be stepping back. The current episode differs because the Fed is not actively tapering large-scale asset purchases; the parallel is that a shift in the marginal buyer — from official to private — can move the long end sharply even when the front end is stable.[^16]

Both episodes share a lesson: the long end of the curve can move for reasons that have little to do with the next GDP print.

## Implications for decision-makers: what this affects, and what it does not

What this affects:

- **Rates and fixed income.** Higher 10-year yields and a positive term premium raise the cost of long-duration borrowing. The Freddie Mac 30-year fixed mortgage rate was reported at 6.43% in the week ending 3 July 2026,[^17] a reminder that Treasury term premia feed quickly into consumer borrowing costs. Mortgages, corporate debt, and municipal issuance all face higher benchmark rates. Duration-heavy portfolios are exposed to further steepening if the long-end premium continues to rebuild.

- **Equities.** Higher yields are a headwind to equity valuations, especially for long-duration growth stocks. The S&P 500 rose nearly 6% between the April and June FOMC meetings even as yields climbed, but that resilience has been concentrated in AI-related earnings. A sustained rise in real yields — rather than nominal yields driven by inflation expectations — would pressure multiples more directly.

- **Foreign exchange.** The wide U.S.–Japan rate differential has pushed USD/JPY to roughly 162.70, a 40-year yen low, supporting carry trades and dollar strength against the yen. The ECB hike complicates the dollar–euro picture: the euro area is tightening into a growth downgrade, so EUR/USD may be more sensitive to relative growth surprises than to rate differentials alone.

- **Credit.** The FOMC minutes note that gross inflows to business development companies slowed sharply in Q2 and that net inflows were likely turning negative amid redemption requests.[^8] Corporate bond issuance has remained solid, led by AI-related investment, but leveraged-loan and private-credit markets are showing signs of strain. A further rise in Treasury term premia would widen credit spreads, particularly for lower-rated borrowers.

- **Commodities and inflation hedges.** Oil prices had fallen on optimism around a U.S.–Iran memorandum of understanding, but the conflict remains the key source of supply-shock inflation in the Fed's forecast. Gold has been volatile. If fiscal-supply concerns dominate, gold and inflation-linked bonds could benefit even if nominal yields stay elevated.

What this does not affect:

- It does not tell you whether to buy or sell Treasuries, equities, or the dollar.
- It does not prove that a recession is imminent. The steepening is long-end-led, not front-end-led.
- It does not settle whether the Fed will hike in 2026. The dot plot is a forecast, not a promise.
- It does not resolve the neutral real interest rate in an economy being reshaped by AI investment, defense spending, and deglobalization. That remains unknowable.

## Open question: which side of the curve is the message?

The Treasury curve is steepening because the long end is rising faster than the short end. That is a bear steepener in market parlance, and it usually means the market is worried about inflation, supply, or the long-run cost of capital rather than cheering growth.

But the bear steepener is happening at the same time the labor market is cooling and other central banks are tightening. The Fed's median projection says higher rates. The futures market says even higher. The term premium models say the long end is carrying more risk compensation. The Treasury's borrowing calendar says supply is heavy. And the jobs report says demand may be cracking.

So the open question is this: if the July and August employment reports confirm the June softness, will the long end of the curve keep rising because term premium and supply have become structural, or will the 10-year yield fall back into JPMorgan's fair-value range as recession pricing takes over? In other words, is the curve steepening because the economy is too strong for the Fed to stop, or because the market no longer trusts the price at which the U.S. government can borrow for a decade? The two explanations point to very different worlds. The curve, for now, is refusing to choose.
