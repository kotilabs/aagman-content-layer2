# LinkedIn Carousel — Signal: oil-gold-war-premium

## Slide 1
- **kicker:** 17 June 2026
- **headline:** The war premium is leaving the oil market faster than the war ended.
- **body:** The US and Iran signed an interim peace agreement in Versailles and Tehran. The Strait of Hormuz reopened toll-free for two months; US oil sanctions were waived immediately. Brent had traded at $120–$126/bbl in April. By late June it was below $78/bbl; by early July, near $72/bbl.
- **hero stat/quote:** Brent crude: $120–$126/bbl in April → ~$72/bbl on 6 July 2026. Source: MUFG Middle East Daily; Polyestertime.
- **speaker note:** Names the shift and establishes the magnitude of the war-premium deflation.

## Slide 2
- **kicker:** The chokepoint
- **headline:** One-fifth of global oil was trapped by a probability.
- **body:** The Strait of Hormuz normally moves about 20 mb/d of crude oil and petroleum liquids — roughly one-fifth of world supply. The EIA and IEA call it the world’s most important energy chokepoint. Futures markets repriced long before the last tanker turned around.
- **hero stat/quote:** ~20 mb/d of crude and petroleum liquids transit Hormuz pre-conflict. Source: US Energy Information Administration / International Energy Agency.
- **speaker note:** Gives the reader the scale of the tail risk the premium was pricing.

## Slide 3
- **kicker:** Inventory arithmetic
- **headline:** Demand destruction and record inventory draws happened at the same time.
- **body:** The IEA now expects global oil demand to contract by 1.1 mb/d in 2026, with second-quarter deliveries down 5 mb/d year-on-year. Supply is projected to fall 3.9 mb/d. Yet observed global stocks drew at a record 3.8 mb/d pace from February through May.
- **hero stat/quote:** 143 million barrels drawn from observed global stocks in May 2026. Source: IEA Oil Market Report, June 2026.
- **speaker note:** Shows the contradiction that makes the post-deal price level debatable.

## Slide 4
- **kicker:** The thesis
- **headline:** This isn't a peace dividend. It's a probability reset.
- **body:** The war premium was never a payment for barrels already lost. It was a *conditional probability* attached to a sustained closure of Hormuz. When the deal was signed, that probability fell. Prices moved faster than ships, mines, or inventories.
- **hero stat/quote:** Brent fell roughly $45–$55/bbl from its $120–$126/bbl April peak while Hormuz traffic was still only partly restored. Source: market reports via MUFG Middle East Daily and EIA STEO.
- **speaker note:** Names the conceptual mechanism that ties the slides together.

## Slide 5
- **kicker:** The other premium
- **headline:** Gold's war premium was already half gone before the deal.
- **body:** Gold hit roughly $5,589/oz early this year. By the time the agreement was signed it had already fallen about 18–20% as the Fed shifted hawkish. After the deal it consolidated near $4,330/oz — lower oil reducing the urgency of further rate hikes even as safe-haven demand faded, while a stronger dollar and hawkish Fed repricing capped the bounce.
- **hero stat/quote:** Gold all-time high: ~$5,589/oz in early 2026. Source: Frontier Asset Management, 8 June 2026.
- **speaker note:** Extends the argument from oil to gold via the real-rate channel.

## Slide 6
- **kicker:** Policy inertia
- **headline:** Central banks hiked, or warned of hikes, into the ceasefire.
- **body:** The ECB raised its deposit rate 25 basis points to 2.25% on 11 June, lifting its 2026 inflation forecast to 3.0%. The Fed held rates at 3.50%–3.75% on 17 June, but nine of 18 FOMC participants projected at least one 2026 hike, citing energy supply shocks.
- **hero stat/quote:** ECB deposit rate raised to 2.25% on 11 June 2026. Source: ECB / Xinhua.
- **speaker note:** Explains why lower oil is not automatically lower inflation expectations.

## Slide 7
- **kicker:** Import dependence
- **headline:** For India, the premium was an inflation forecast, not just a price.
- **body:** India imports 85–90% of its crude oil; 40–50% of those imports transit Hormuz. At its 5 June meeting the RBI raised its FY27 CPI inflation forecast to 5.1%, from 4.6%, citing higher crude prices and partial fuel pass-through.
- **hero stat/quote:** RBI FY27 CPI inflation forecast raised to 5.1% from 4.6%. Source: RBI Governor’s Statement / Chartforest, 5 June 2026.
- **speaker note:** Grounds the commodity move in a major importer’s policy response.

## Slide 8
- **kicker:** Two readings
- **headline:** Either the rally was mostly rent, or inventories are too low for $72 oil.
- **body:** The IEA projects a 5 mb/d global surplus in 2027 if Gulf production returns; the EIA sees Brent averaging $79/bbl next year. Against that, OECD petroleum inventories are projected to fall to their lowest level since 2003, and the EIA does not expect pre-conflict Hormuz traffic until early 2027.
- **hero stat/quote:** OECD petroleum inventories projected at their lowest since 2003 by end-2026. Source: US EIA Short-Term Energy Outlook, June 2026.
- **speaker note:** Poses the central empirical disagreement the carousel is walking through.

## Slide 9
- **kicker:** Fifth month
- **headline:** OPEC+ added 188,000 barrels a day as the unwind continued.
- **body:** On 3 July, the seven remaining core producers agreed to raise output by 188 kb/d for August 2026 — the fifth consecutive monthly increase and a continuation of the pre-planned unwind of April 2023 voluntary cuts, not a fresh post-deal response. The UAE had left OPEC+ on 1 May. The volume is small against the 20 mb/d Hormuz corridor, but it signals producers expect the strait to keep reopening and are willing to test demand at lower prices.
- **hero stat/quote:** OPEC+ production increase: +188 kb/d for August 2026, the fifth straight monthly increase. Source: Associated Press, 3 July 2026.
- **speaker note:** Shows the supply side continuing a pre-planned unwind rather than reacting fresh to the deal.

## Slide 10
- **kicker:** Open question
- **headline:** What reprices next — the deal, or the inventory?
- **body:** If the 60-day nuclear clock produces a durable accord and Hormuz traffic normalizes, the 2027 surplus story may stick. If mine clearance, insurance reinstatement, or Israeli action delays flows, the low-inventory bid will return before the market has rebuilt its buffers. The 1990–91 Gulf War showed that war premiums can deflate almost as quickly as they inflate once worst-case probabilities fall.
- **hero stat/quote:** EIA assumes pre-conflict Hormuz traffic will not return until early 2027. Source: US EIA STEO, June 2026.
- **speaker note:** Leaves the reader with the unresolved tension between diplomacy and physical tightness.
