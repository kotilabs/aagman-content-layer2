# India’s Domestic Flows Are Absorbing the Biggest Foreign Selloff in Years

*When the Locals Become the Market*

Picture a busy neighbourhood market that has always had two kinds of buyers. For years, a group of out-of-town traders set the tone. They arrived with large wallets, bought the best produce in bulk, and left just as quickly when they heard better prices elsewhere. Their energy made the market feel vibrant, but also volatile. Lately, something has changed. The out-of-town traders have been thinning out, selling their sacks and moving on. Yet the market has not collapsed. Instead, a steady stream of local regulars — housewives, small shopkeepers, office workers — has kept showing up every morning with fixed monthly budgets. They are not trying to time the harvest. They simply buy a little each day. That quiet persistence is now holding the market together. The Indian equity market is living through a version of this story.

India’s equity market is experiencing a historic ownership crossover: foreign portfolio investors have sold record amounts in 2026, while domestic institutional investors—mutual funds, insurers and SIP flows—have absorbed enough of that selling to become larger owners than foreigners for the first time in over a decade.

---

## Context: What Changed?

Foreign portfolio investors (FPIs) have been selling Indian equities relentlessly. In the first half of June 2026 alone, they pulled out more than ₹2.87 lakh crore — already exceeding the ₹1.66 lakh crore withdrawn in all of 2025. Calendar-year 2026 has seen net FPI selling in every month except February.

This is not a routine correction. It is the largest sustained foreign exodus from Indian equities in at least a decade and a half.

Yet the headline indices have not cracked proportionally. The Nifty 50 has fallen roughly 9–12% from its September 2024 high, but it has not experienced the panic-driven collapse one might expect from such heavy foreign selling. The reason is structural: domestic institutional investors (DIIs) — mutual funds, insurers, pension funds — have absorbed most of that selling.

The India domestic-flow market shift is the transition where domestic institutional investors, led by mutual fund SIPs and insurance flows, replace foreign portfolio investors as the marginal owners and price-setters of Indian equities.

## Mechanism: How This Actually Works

Indian equities rest on two very different pools of capital.

**Foreign portfolio investors** are global allocators. Their India exposure is a function of relative valuation, relative earnings momentum, currency expectations, global risk appetite and benchmark weights. When US rates stay high, the dollar strengthens, oil prices rise, or India underperforms China, Korea or Taiwan, FPIs can reallocate quickly. Because India trades at a premium valuation and because rupee depreciation directly reduces dollar returns, small changes in global opportunity cost can produce large outflows. FPI selling is concentrated in large-cap, liquid names — the stocks easiest to trade and most owned by foreign funds. FY26 data shows the largest dollar-value FII cuts were in IT, financials and FMCG, while domestic buyers absorbed selling in defensives, quality private banks and domestic themes.

**Domestic institutional investors** are primarily mutual funds, life insurers (notably LIC), pension funds and banks. Their inflows are driven by household savings habits, insurance premium collections and, most visibly, monthly [systematic investment plans (SIPs)](/layer1/sips-explained). SIPs are sticky by design: investors commit a fixed amount each month regardless of market level. That creates a mechanical bid into equities. When FPIs sell, domestic funds use their cash and fresh inflows to buy.

This is why DII holdings have risen even as FPI holdings have fallen.

The external sector adds a constraint. India runs a current account deficit that must be financed by capital flows. The RBI Annual Report 2025-26 explicitly described portfolio flows as 'short for financing CAD.' When FPI equity outflows exceed FDI and debt inflows, the [Reserve Bank of India](/layer2/rbi-policy-cycle) must either let the rupee depreciate or use reserves. Between April and December 2025, the RBI drew down US$30.8 billion of reserves (excluding valuation effects) and allowed the rupee to weaken from the mid-80s to about ₹95 per dollar. The West Asia conflict also pushed Brent crude above $100 a barrel in May 2026, worsening the trade balance and adding pressure on the currency. That depreciation, in turn, hurts dollar-based foreign returns and can reinforce the FPI selling cycle.

So the mechanism has two feedback loops:

1. **Domestic loop:** SIPs and insurance flows create a steady bid that cushions foreign selling.
2. **External loop:** FPI outflows pressure the rupee, which lowers dollar returns, which can encourage further FPI caution.

Which loop dominates at any moment depends on global conditions, domestic savings behaviour and RBI management.

## Why hasn’t the Nifty crashed despite record FPI selling?

Several features of this episode are historically rare.

**The scale of FPI selling.** Net FPI outflows reached US$16.5 billion in FY26, with March 2026 alone accounting for US$13.1 billion following the West Asia conflict that began on 28 February 2026. In rupee terms, the first half of June 2026 saw outflows larger than all of 2025.

**The calm in implied volatility.** [India VIX](/layer1/india-vix-explained) closed at 11.65 on 7 July 2026, its lowest level since February 2026 and near the bottom of its 52-week range of 8.72–28.91. The March/April geopolitical spike took VIX to around 28–29 — elevated, but far below crisis levels (87 in March 2020, 92.5 in November 2008). Domestic absorption has prevented the kind of disorderly index decline that normally triggers panic hedging. The calm proved fragile: on 8 July 2026, renewed US-Iran tensions pushed VIX up by about 26% to roughly 14.68–15.16 (close near 14.68, intraday high ~15.16), a reminder that low volatility can unwind quickly on geopolitical news.

**The crossover in ownership.** FPI ownership of NSE-listed companies has fallen to 14–17-year lows. DII ownership has hit record highs. In the Nifty 50, DII ownership (24.9%) now exceeds FII ownership (22.8%). This is not merely a flow phenomenon; it is a shift in who owns the market.

**The split between equity and debt.** While FPIs sold Indian equities aggressively, they bought Indian debt. Net FPI debt inflows were US$2.1 billion in FY26. Foreign investors are not negative on India per se; they are reallocating from expensive equity risk to relatively safer rupee debt.

**A warning beneath the surface.** SIP contributions remained above ₹30,000 crore in May 2026 at ₹30,954 crore. By contrast, overall equity mutual fund net inflows fell 40.4% month-on-month to ₹22,907.77 crore in May, the lowest in 12 months but still the 63rd consecutive positive month. The SIP pipe is steady even as lump-sum and direct buying weaken. Yet the SIP stoppage ratio crossed 100% in both March and April 2026 — meaning more SIP accounts were discontinued or matured than were opened. Rupee contributions look stable; account-level breadth may be narrowing.

## What are the four plausible readings of this signal?

There is no single obvious reading of this signal. Here are four plausible interpretations, each with supporting and contradicting evidence.

Aagman’s structural bias is that India is in a long-term domestic-demand-driven bull market; here that bias is an undercurrent, not a conclusion, and the data on SIP stoppage ratios and persistent FPI selling are treated as counter-evidence.

### Reading 1: Foreign selling is a structural downgrade that will keep pressure on Indian equities

This view holds that global EM allocators have reassessed India’s relative attractiveness and concluded it is too expensive.

**Supporting points:** India’s neutral weight in [MSCI EM](/layer2/emerging-market-flows) fell to a two-year low of 15.25%, and India became the biggest underweight in global EM portfolios. HSBC noted that 71% of large EM funds were underweight India (as cited, end-July 2026). India’s 2025 return of roughly +5% lagged MSCI EM (+25%) and MSCI China (+35%). Yogesh Aggarwal of HSBC said India is “60 per cent more expensive” than China, Indonesia and Korea.

**Caveat:** This reading treats FPI outflows as a permanent vote against India rather than a relative-rotation trade. Light foreign positioning also means less foreign capital left to sell. If global risk appetite stabilises, the same underweight positioning could reverse quickly.

### Reading 2: Light foreign positioning and domestic stickiness are constructive if earnings deliver

This view holds that the worst of the foreign exit is already priced in, and domestic flows provide a floor while India waits for an earnings revival.

**Supporting points:** FPI ownership is at multi-decade lows, while DII ownership is at record highs. India VIX has fallen to five-month lows, suggesting domestic liquidity has contained fear. GDP growth remains strong, inflation has moderated and corporate balance sheets are healthy. Aless Berardi of Amundi noted that valuations were moving toward neutral and domestic demand staying robust, making conditions for a resurgence in foreign investment more favourable.

**Caveat:** This reading assumes domestic flows are a permanent floor. SIPs are sticky but not immutable. If earnings do not improve or if equity returns stay subdued, domestic buyers may eventually lose patience. It also assumes foreign investors will return on better earnings, which is not guaranteed if other EMs offer better risk-adjusted returns.

### Reading 3: The ownership shift is a durable structural transition, not a cyclical blip

This view holds that India’s market is becoming genuinely domestic-dominated, driven by a deepening “SIP culture.”

**Supporting points:** The Economic Survey 2025-26 documented that DIIs, retail and HNI combined reached 27.8% of NSE-listed equity value by Q2 FY26, and that DII share by value surpassed FII share for the first time in Q4 FY25. SIP inflows have been above ₹30,000 crore for several months. The value of FPI holdings has still grown at a CAGR of more than 18% since 2020 even as ownership share has fallen — meaning the domestic share has grown faster.

**Caveat:** DII buying is partly a mechanical response to inflows, not necessarily a discretionary bullish call. Foreign ownership can fall simply because foreigners sell into domestic buying during a correction; that does not by itself prove a permanent domestic-dominated regime. SIP stoppage ratios above 100% suggest the retail broadening may be plateauing.

### Reading 4: The real story is currency and global liquidity, not India-specific fundamentals

This view holds that FPI selling is primarily a dollar, rates and oil story.

**Supporting points:** FPI outflows accelerated in months when the US dollar strengthened, US rates stayed high and oil prices spiked. The rupee weakened materially, directly reducing dollar returns. The RBI’s FY26 reserve drawdown and rupee weakness show the external constraint is binding. Similar FPI selling has been seen across EMs during risk-off phases.

**Caveat:** India-specific factors — premium valuations, slower earnings momentum, tax concerns — are repeatedly cited by foreign investors themselves. FPIs did not sell all EMs equally; China, Taiwan and Korea attracted flows while India saw outflows. Global liquidity is a necessary condition, but not sufficient, to explain India’s relative underperformance.

These four readings are not mutually exclusive. The most honest position is to hold them in tension.

## What can the 2013 Taper Tantrum and 2020 crash teach us?

History does not predict. It can, however, reduce the temptation to over-dramatise the present.

**The [2013 Taper Tantrum](/layer2/taper-tantrum-2013).** In May 2013, Federal Reserve Chairman Ben Bernanke hinted at tapering quantitative easing. US Treasury yields surged, capital fled emerging markets, and the rupee fell from about ₹55 per dollar to almost ₹69 by August 2013. The RBI responded by tightening liquidity and, eventually under Governor Raghuram Rajan, launching an FCNR deposit scheme that mobilised more than US$30 billion.

The similarity is that a Fed-led risk-off can overwhelm emerging markets quickly. The difference is that India today has stronger macro fundamentals: the current account deficit is around 1.1% of GDP, forex reserves stand at roughly US$691 billion, and domestic institutional flows are a much larger buffer.

**The 2020 COVID crash.** In March 2020, India VIX hit an all-time high near 87 as FPIs fled and markets crashed. Within months, massive global liquidity and domestic buying produced a sharp recovery.

The similarity is that Indian markets can recover even when foreign investors are absent, provided domestic flows and policy support remain. The difference is that the 2020 shock was exogenous and short-lived, followed by unprecedented global stimulus. The current episode is a slower-burn valuation and relative-return adjustment.

Both analogues suggest the same thing: the foreign-domestic balance matters, but so do the external conditions that drive it.

## Implications for Decision-Makers

This is not a recommendation section. It is a map of what this signal affects and what it does not.

**For equity market structure.** The marginal price-setter in Indian equities is changing. Large-cap, liquid names remain vulnerable to FPI selling because they are the easiest to exit — with FY26 foreign cuts concentrated in IT, financials and FMCG. Mid- and small-caps have been relatively supported by domestic liquidity, which has favoured defensives, quality private banks and domestic themes. If DII dominance persists, sector leadership may continue to tilt toward domestic-oriented themes.

**For currency and reserves.** Persistent FPI outflows and a current account deficit mean rupee pressure is likely to continue unless capital flows recover or the RBI accepts a sharper depreciation. Reserve levels are substantial but not infinite. The RBI’s choice between defending the rupee and preserving reserves will shape domestic liquidity conditions.

**For debt markets.** FPI buying of Indian government securities through the Fully Accessible Route (FAR), the RBI/SEBI framework that allows non-resident investors to buy specified government securities without restrictions — including strong G-sec buying in June 2026 via the FAR — suggests foreign capital is not fleeing India entirely. It is reallocating from equity risk to debt carry. That has implications for relative valuations between Indian equities and Indian bonds.

**For global allocators.** India is now the biggest underweight in EM portfolios by some measures. From a pure positioning standpoint, the contrarian bull case is that there is less foreign money left to sell. The bear case is that the underweight is justified by relative valuation and earnings weakness.

**What this does not tell us.** It does not tell us whether to buy or sell. It does not tell us when FPI selling will end. It does not tell us whether domestic flows will hold if equity returns remain subdued for another 12–18 months. It does not resolve whether the ownership crossover is durable or cyclical.

## Open Question

If foreign portfolio investors continue to treat India as a relative-rotation exit and domestic SIP growth begins to plateau at the same time, what becomes the market’s next source of stability — and who, if anyone, will be the marginal buyer if both flows turn negative together?
