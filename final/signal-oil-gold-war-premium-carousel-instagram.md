# Instagram Carousel — Signal: oil-gold-war-premium

**Format:** Standalone carousel (no external link, no CTA)  
**Slides:** 11

---

## Slide 1

**kicker:** The repricing
**headline:** The war premium is leaving oil and gold faster than the war left the Strait.
**body:** On 17 June the US and Iran signed an interim deal at Versailles and Tehran. It reopened the Strait of Hormuz toll-free for two months, waived sanctions on Iranian oil exports, and started a 60-day nuclear clock. Markets had spent months pricing a prolonged closure. They are now pricing the opposite — not certainty, but a lower probability of catastrophe.
**hero stat / quote:** Brent crude fell from $120–$126/bbl in April to ~$72/bbl by early July. (Source: Polyestertime / market reports, 6 July 2026)
**speaker note:** Names the shift: a diplomatic repricing of a geopolitical risk premium, not a simple price decline.

---

## Slide 2

**kicker:** The numbers
**headline:** Brent has given up roughly $50/bbl on a piece of paper signed in two capitals.
**body:** WTI is below $70 again. Gold, after touching ~$5,589/oz earlier this year, is consolidating near $4,330. The moves are out of proportion with the physical recovery because the premium was never about oil already lost — it was about oil that might be lost. Once the worst case became less likely, the premium collapsed.
**hero stat / quote:** Gold spot: ~$4,330/oz on 18 June, down from an all-time high near $5,589/oz. (Source: MUFG Middle East Daily, 18 June 2026; Frontier Asset Management, 8 June 2026)
**speaker note:** Establishes the magnitude of the unwind and hints that the mechanism is probabilistic, not physical.

---

## Slide 3

**kicker:** The channel
**headline:** Oil rallied on a chokepoint, not a shortage.
**body:** The Strait of Hormuz normally carries roughly 20 mb/d of crude and petroleum liquids — about one-fifth of global supply. When it closed, traders attached a high probability to a sustained disruption. Futures repriced immediately. Tankers did not need to stop moving for the market to behave as if they might.
**hero stat / quote:** Pre-conflict Hormuz flows: ~20 mb/d of crude oil and petroleum liquids. (Source: US Energy Information Administration / IEA, June 2026)
**speaker note:** Explains the transmission mechanism: probability-weighted tail risk, not realized supply loss.

---

## Slide 4

**kicker:** The unwind
**headline:** Physical supply is still disrupted. The premium is not.
**body:** Gulf output fell more than 11 mb/d in May because cargoes could not leave. Global inventories drew at a record pace — 143 million barrels in May alone. Yet Brent kept falling through late June because the market no longer prices the strait as permanently closed. Diplomacy moved faster than tankers.
**hero stat / quote:** Global observed oil stocks drew 143 mb in May; 3.8 mb/d on average since February. (Source: IEA Oil Market Report, June 2026)
**speaker note:** Strengthens the probability argument with the disconnect between physical tightness and falling prices.

---

## Slide 5

**kicker:** The pivot
**headline:** This isn't a peace rally. It's a probability markdown.
**body:** The interim deal is a memorandum, not a treaty. The 60-day nuclear window could still fail. Israel is not a party. A residual war premium should arguably remain. But markets do not price residual risk well — they price the base case, and the base case has shifted from "closed indefinitely" to "open enough for now."
**hero stat / quote:** Brent: ~$78/bbl the day after the deal was signed, 18 June; ~$72/bbl less than three weeks later. (Source: MUFG Middle East Daily, 18 June 2026; Polyestertime, 6 July 2026)
**speaker note:** The thesis slide: reframes the price action as a change in probability, not a verdict on peace.

---

## Slide 6

**kicker:** Gold
**headline:** Gold was already falling before the deal — because central banks were catching up.
**body:** Bullion does not yield anything, so it competes with real interest rates. As oil-driven inflation forced the ECB to hike and the Fed to turn hawkish, real yields rose and gold sold off. By the time the agreement was signed, gold had already fallen roughly 18–20% from its peak. The deal added a third force: lower oil reduced the urgency of further hikes. But 17 June was a cross-current — the peace deal lowered tail risk while a hawkish Fed surprise lifted real yields and the dollar, capping gold’s relief.
**hero stat / quote:** ECB deposit rate: raised 25 bps to 2.25% on 11 June; 9 of 18 FOMC dots saw at least one 2026 hike. (Sources: ECB / Xinhua, 11 June 2026; Fox Business, 17 June 2026)
**speaker note:** Distinguishes gold's channel from oil's — real rates and policy expectations, not just safe-haven demand.

---

## Slide 7

**kicker:** The inventory problem
**headline:** The world is short oil even if the price says otherwise.
**body:** Demand destruction is real — the IEA expects global oil demand to contract by 1.1 mb/d in 2026, with 2Q26 deliveries down 5 mb/d year-on-year. But inventories have still drawn at record speed. The EIA projects OECD petroleum inventories will fall to their lowest level since 2003. Low stocks and low prices can coexist if the market believes supply is coming back.
**hero stat / quote:** OECD petroleum inventories projected at lowest since 2003 by end-2026. (Source: US EIA Short-Term Energy Outlook, June 2026)
**speaker note:** Introduces the tension: physical tightness remains, but the market is trading the recovery narrative.

---

## Slide 8

**kicker:** The importer
**headline:** India is the most exposed large importer to Hormuz normalization.
**body:** India imports 85–90% of its crude, with 40–50% of that transiting Hormuz. Every $10/bbl rise in oil costs roughly 0.1–0.2 percentage points of GDP and 0.2 percentage points of CPI, by MUFG's estimate. The RBI has already raised its FY27 inflation forecast to 5.1%. The deal is relief, but only if it holds long enough for India to restock.
**hero stat / quote:** India's crude import dependence: 85–90% of consumption; Hormuz share of imports: 40–50%. (Source: Indian Defence Institute, 4 April 2026)
**speaker note:** Anchors the macro implications in a major importer's exposure.

---

## Slide 9

**kicker:** The unwind continues
**headline:** OPEC+ added 188,000 barrels a day — the fifth straight monthly increase.
**body:** The August hike was not a fresh post-deal response; it was the fifth consecutive monthly increase, continuing the pre-planned unwind of April 2023 voluntary cuts. With the UAE having left OPEC+ on 1 May, the decision came from the seven remaining core producers. The volume is small against the 20 mb/d Hormuz corridor, but it signals producers expect the strait to keep reopening and are willing to test demand at lower prices.
**hero stat / quote:** OPEC+ agreed to raise output by 188,000 b/d in August 2026, the fifth straight monthly increase. (Source: Associated Press, 3 July 2026)
**speaker note:** Notes the pre-planned supply unwind and sets up the question of what happens if the deal frays.

---

## Slide 10

**kicker:** The asymmetry
**headline:** The downside is priced. The upside is not.
**body:** If the 60-day talks produce a final accord, the residual premium could compress further. If they collapse, or if shipping, insurance, and mine-clearance lag the diplomacy, prices would reprice sharply higher. The EIA does not expect pre-conflict Hormuz traffic until early 2027. Markets often move on signatures; tankers move on physics.
**hero stat / quote:** EIA assumes Hormuz flows resume in 3Q26 but do not reach pre-conflict levels until early 2027. (Source: US EIA STEO, June 2026)
**speaker note:** Frames the asymmetry: the base case is priced in, but the tail risk is not fully reflected.

---

## Slide 11

**kicker:** The open question
**headline:** How much of the 2026 commodity rally was geopolitical rent, and how much was real scarcity?
**body:** That is what the next two months will decide. If demand has been destroyed and non-OPEC supply keeps rising, the IEA's projected 5 mb/d 2027 surplus looks plausible. If Hormuz normalization is slower than the deal suggests, the same tight inventories that looked bearish could turn bullish very quickly. The premium deflated in weeks. The underlying market may not.
**hero stat / quote:** IEA forecasts global oil demand to fall 1.1 mb/d y-o-y in 2026; projects a 5 mb/d surplus in 2027 if Gulf production returns. (Source: IEA Oil Market Report, June 2026)
**speaker note:** Closes with the unresolved question and no CTA, leaving the argument complete but open.
