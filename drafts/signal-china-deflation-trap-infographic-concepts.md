## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The December CPI bounce is a food mirage
- **Central insight:** The headline CPI uptick to +0.8% in December was driven by temporary food-supply shocks, not a broad demand recovery.
- **Data:** Full-year CPI 0.0% YoY; December CPI +0.8% YoY; core CPI +1.2% YoY in December and only +0.7% for the full year; fresh vegetables +18.2% YoY; pork -14.6% YoY.
- **Visualization type:** Deconstruction / stacked-impact waterfall or split bar.
- **Composition notes:** Lead with the +0.8% headline number, then visually pull it apart: show the vegetable spike pushing the bar up and pork pulling it down, leaving a small core-CPI sliver. Place full-year CPI (0.0%) and the 2% target as horizontal benchmarks so the reader sees the December number is an exception, not a trend.
- **Sources:** NBS CPI release (2026-01-10); NBS annual release (2026-01-19).

### Concept 2: Stimulus is flowing to factories and local governments, not household wallets
- **Central insight:** Beijing’s 2025 stimulus is historically large, but the biggest channels are supply-side and balance-sheet repair, while direct household support is comparatively small.
- **Data:** Government deficit RMB 5.66 trn (~4% of GDP); local special-purpose bonds RMB 4.4 trn; ultra-long special treasury bonds RMB 1.3 trn; consumer-goods trade-in RMB 300 bn; GDP +5.0% YoY but CPI 0.0% and PPI -2.6%.
- **Visualization type:** Diverging scale / Sankey-style flow diagram or side-by-side bar comparison.
- **Composition notes:** Left side: a tall stack of supply-side/balance-sheet channels (deficit, special bonds, bank/local-government support). Right side: a much smaller stack labelled direct household demand support (RMB 300 bn trade-in). Between them, show the outcome puzzle: GDP grew 5% while prices stayed flat. The eye follows from "big stimulus" to "not reaching consumption."
- **Sources:** State Council / Xinhua (2025-03-07; 2025-09-30); NBS annual release (2026-01-19).

### Concept 3: Three straight years of factory-gate deflation
- **Central insight:** Producer prices have fallen for three consecutive years, signalling industrial overcapacity and weak pricing power.
- **Data:** PPI -2.6% YoY full-year 2025; -1.9% YoY in December; this follows declines in 2023 and 2024 (third consecutive year of PPI deflation).
- **Visualization type:** Timeline / descending staircase bar chart.
- **Composition notes:** Three downward-step bars labelled 2023, 2024, 2025, each below the zero line, with 2025 at -2.6%. Add a thin horizontal line at 0% and a call-out: "3 years of falling factory-gate prices." Keep the colour in the red/deflation zone to reinforce persistence.
- **Sources:** NBS annual release (2026-01-19); State Council / Xinhua (2026-01-09).

### Concept 4: The housing wealth hole swallows the trade-in stimulus
- **Central insight:** Household wealth is concentrated in housing, and the estimated consumption drag from the broader property downturn is far larger than the biggest demand-side stimulus program.
- **Data:** Households hold ~70% of wealth in housing; NBS new-home prices in 70 cities fell 2.7% YoY in December and only 6 of 70 cities rose; independent estimates suggest observed peak-to-trough price declines of 20–40% in many markets; a 10% home-price drop cuts consumption by ~1.5–2.3%, implying a consumption loss of 2–4% of GDP; consumer-goods trade-in funding is RMB 300 bn.
- **Visualization type:** Ratio / scale comparison (icon-based or dual-axis bar).
- **Composition notes:** Start with a house icon representing 70% of household wealth. Show the NBS 70-city index down 2.7% YoY as the official benchmark, then add a separate, larger callout for the estimated 20–40% peak-to-trough decline in many markets. Translate that into the implied 2–4% of GDP consumption loss, visually towering over the RMB 300 bn trade-in box. The payoff: the headline index understates the wealth shock, and the stimulus is too small and too indirect to fill the hole.
- **Sources:** NBS; Reuters/NBS calculations via Economic Times (2026-01-19); Stanford SCCEI brief (2026-07-15); State Council / Xinhua (2025-09-30).

### Concept 5: Weak domestic demand is showing up as record exports
- **Central insight:** Weak domestic absorption is pushing excess supply abroad, creating a record trade surplus while home prices and producer prices fall at home.
- **Data:** Trade surplus ~USD 992 bn–USD 1.2 trn in 2025; exports ~USD 3.77 trn (+~5.5% YoY); imports ~USD 2.58 trn (flat); auto exports +21%; EV exports +67% to 1.65 million units; full-year CPI 0.0%; PPI -2.6%.
- **Visualization type:** Divergence / two-sided comparison or flow map.
- **Composition notes:** Split the frame. Left side: domestic weakness — falling CPI/PPI, falling property investment (-17.2%), falling home prices. Right side: external boom — record surplus, rising exports, surging EV shipments. Use arrows flowing from left to right to show excess domestic supply being absorbed overseas. The headline spans the middle: "Strong exports, flat prices."
- **Sources:** Maritime Gateway / customs data (2026-01-16); AP News (2026-01-14); IEA Global EV Outlook 2026 (2026-01-22); NBS annual release (2026-01-19).

## What to avoid

- **Avoid predicting a 2026 policy pivot.** The research flags the consumption-stimulus mismatch as a current structural fact; it does not verify that Beijing will shift to large household transfers.
- **Avoid treating the December CPI uptick as a recovery.** It is food-supply driven; the concept must deconstruct it, not celebrate it.
- **Avoid comparing China directly to Japan as if it were a forecast.** The Japan analogy is useful perspective, but the infographic should stick to verified 2025 Chinese data.
- **Avoid vague "stimulus is big" visuals without showing where the money goes.** The composition of spending is the insight, not the headline deficit alone.
- **Avoid rounding the trade surplus into a single precise number.** Use the stated range (~USD 992 bn–USD 1.2 trn) and explain it reflects customs/revision basis differences.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with Concepts 1, 2, and 4 as the strongest visual case. Concept 1 cleanly debunks the December CPI headline, Concept 2 exposes the supply-side skew of the 2025 stimulus, and Concept 4 gives the household balance-sheet mechanism scale and emotional impact. Add Concept 3 if the surface needs a timeline of PPI persistence, and Concept 5 if the global spillover angle is a priority for the audience.
