# Instagram Carousel — Fed Divergence and Curve Steepening
**Signal ID:** fed-divergence-curve-steepening  
**Surface:** Instagram carousel (STANDALONE — no external link, no CTA)

---

## Slide 1 — Hook

**kicker:** Curve / labor  
**headline:** The Treasury curve is steepening into a cooling labor market, not out of one.  
**body:** On July 2 the 10-year Treasury yield closed at 4.49% and the 2-year at 4.14%, leaving the 10-year minus 2-year spread at +35 basis points after a long stretch of inversion. The same week the June jobs report showed payrolls rising only 57,000 and unemployment at 4.2%, down from 4.3% in May. Usually a soft employment print flattens or inverts the curve as the front end prices rate cuts. This time the long end led the move.  
**hero stat / quote:** 10-year minus 2-year spread: +35 bps (Advisor Perspectives, July 2, 2026); June nonfarm payrolls: +57,000 (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, July 2, 2026).  
**speaker note:** Open by naming the anomaly—the curve is un-inverting while the labor market cools, not heats up.

## Slide 2 — Fed

**kicker:** Fed  
**headline:** The Fed held rates and raised its own forecast.  
**body:** The FOMC left the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% on June 17 by a unanimous 12–0 vote. But the June Summary of Economic Projections lifted the median year-end 2026 rate projection to 3.8%, up from 3.4% in March. Nine of eighteen participants now project at least one 25-basis-point hike before year-end.  
**hero stat / quote:** Median 2026 fed funds projection: 3.8%; 9 of 18 FOMC participants projecting ≥1 hike (Federal Reserve Summary of Economic Projections, June 17, 2026).  
**speaker note:** Establish that the Fed is on hold in action but hawkish in projection.

## Slide 3 — Divergence

**kicker:** Divergence  
**headline:** While the Fed stood still, Frankfurt and Tokyo hiked.  
**body:** The ECB raised all key rates by 25 basis points on June 11, taking the deposit facility rate to 2.25%. The Bank of Japan lifted its policy rate to 1.0% on June 16, its highest level since 1995. The result is a three-way divergence: the U.S. is on hold, Europe is tightening, and Japan is normalizing.  
**hero stat / quote:** ECB deposit facility rate: 2.25% (ECB, June 11, 2026); BoJ policy rate: 1.00% (Bank of Japan via Trading Economics, June 2026).  
**speaker note:** Show the global central-bank divergence widening rate differentials and pressuring currencies.

---

## Slide 4 — Term premium

**kicker:** Term premium  
**headline:** The long end is moving; the short end is not.  
**body:** The San Francisco Fed's CR model puts the 10-year term premium at 1.22% as of July 7, up from 1.16% at the June FOMC meeting. The 2-year term premium has stayed flat around 0.17%. That asymmetry is what steepens the curve when growth data argue the other way.  
**hero stat / quote:** SF Fed CR 10-year term premium: 1.22%; 2-year term premium: 0.17% (Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco, July 7, 2026).  
**speaker note:** Introduce the mechanism—the long-end term premium is rising while the front end stays anchored.

---

## Slide 5 — Thesis (pivot)

**kicker:** Thesis  
**headline:** This isn't a growth steepener. It's a term-premium steepener.  
**body:** A classic steepening into recovery lifts both ends, with the short end falling faster. Here the 10-year yield has risen roughly 50 basis points since the start of the Middle East conflict while front-end expectations have barely budged. The curve is repricing the *compensation* investors demand to hold long-duration U.S. debt, not the *expectation* of faster growth.  
**hero stat / quote:** 10-year Treasury yield: up ~50 bps since the start of the Middle East conflict (FOMC minutes, June 16–17, 2026).  
**speaker note:** State the thesis explicitly—the curve is steepening because of term premium, not growth optimism.

## Slide 6 — Supply

**kicker:** Supply  
**headline:** The Treasury is issuing into a changed buyer base.  
**body:** The Treasury estimates $671 billion of privately-held net marketable borrowing in Q3 FY2026, on top of $189 billion in Q2. Meanwhile the Fed is letting its balance sheet run down only through reinvestment, and the FOMC minutes note that ownership has shifted from price-insensitive official-sector holders to price-sensitive private investors.  
**hero stat / quote:** U.S. Treasury Q3 FY2026 borrowing estimate: $671 billion (Quiver Quantitative, May 4, 2026).  
**speaker note:** Bring in fiscal supply and the changing composition of Treasury demand.

## Slide 7 — Credibility

**kicker:** Credibility  
**headline:** The market is pricing a Fed it does not yet know.  
**body:** Chair Warsh presided over his first FOMC meeting in June and announced five internal reviews covering communications, the balance sheet, data sources, productivity, and the inflation framework. The June statement was shorter and dropped easing-bias language. Futures now price a 96.3% probability that the target range will be above 3.50%–3.75% by December.  
**hero stat / quote:** CME FedWatch: 96.3% probability of >3.75% by December 2026 (Webull / CME FedWatch, late June/early July 2026).  
**speaker note:** Highlight the uncertainty around the new Fed communications regime and market pricing.

## Slide 8 — Jobs

**kicker:** Jobs  
**headline:** The labor market is softening while the curve steepens.  
**body:** June nonfarm payrolls rose only 57,000, prior-month revisions were lowered by a combined 74,000, the unemployment rate was 4.2% after ticking down from 4.3% in May, and labor-force participation fell 0.3 percentage point to 61.5%. In a conventional cycle this combination would push the front end lower and steepen the curve through recession fears. It is not doing that.  
**hero stat / quote:** June unemployment rate: 4.2% (down from 4.3% in May); labor-force participation: 61.5%; prior revisions: −74,000 (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, July 2, 2026).  
**speaker note:** Sharpen the tension—the domestic data do not match the curve's message.

## Slide 9 — FX

**kicker:** FX  
**headline:** The yen is the clearest vote on the rate gap.  
**body:** USD/JPY traded near 162.70 on July 1, the weakest yen level in roughly 40 years. The divergence is not abstract: the wide U.S.–Japan rate differential is funding carry trades and forcing Tokyo to talk about intervention.  
**hero stat / quote:** USD/JPY: ~162.70 (SureShotFX, July 1, 2026).  
**speaker note:** Use the yen to make the divergence concrete.

## Slide 10 — Echo

**kicker:** Echo  
**headline:** The 2013 taper tantrum showed how the long end can move alone.  
**body:** From May to September 2013 the 10-year yield rose about 140 basis points after Bernanke signaled a reduction in Fed bond purchases. The move came from term premiums, not inflation expectations or the near-term policy path. The parallel is not exact—the Fed is not actively tapering—but the lesson is: when the marginal buyer shifts from official to private, the long end can reprice sharply with the front end anchored.  
**hero stat / quote:** 10-year Treasury yield rose ~140 bps during the 2013 taper tantrum (MarketWatch, May 14, 2015).  
**speaker note:** Provide the historical analogue for a long-end-led repricing.

## Slide 11 — Open question

**kicker:** Open question  
**headline:** If July and August payrolls stay soft, do the hiking dots survive?  
**body:** The FOMC has penciled in at least one hike, the market has priced more than one, and the curve has steepened on term-premium and supply fears. But the labor market is now arguing the opposite. The open question is whether the Fed treats the June jobs report as noise to look through or as the first print that unwinds the hawkish repricing.  
**hero stat / quote:** AhaSignals Fed Rate Fragility Index: 51-bp gap between FOMC dot-plot median and futures-implied rate (AhaSignals, July 6, 2026).  
**speaker note:** Close with the unresolved tension between labor-market softness and the hiking projections.
