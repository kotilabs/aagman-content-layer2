## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The Great Absorption
- **Central insight:** In Jan–May 2026, domestic institutions bought ~₹3.75 lakh crore of Indian equities while foreign investors sold ~₹3.18 lakh crore. Add the first five sessions of June and DII buying crosses ₹4 lakh crore — what the data show is record domestic absorption of foreign selling at this scale.
- **Data to include:** DII monthly buying for Jan–May (Jan ₹69,220 cr; Feb ₹39,702 cr; Mar ~₹1.4 lakh cr; Apr ₹43,892 cr; May ₹82,669 cr) vs FII cumulative selling (~₹3.18 lakh cr Jan–May). Add a small "+₹33,933 cr in early June" callout. Context: 2025 full-year FII outflows were ₹2.4 lakh crore; 2024 was ₹1.29 lakh crore.
- **Visualization type:** Waterfall: FII supply (~₹3.18 lakh cr, downward bar) → DII absorption (~₹3.75 lakh cr, upward bar) → residual net domestic demand (~₹0.57 lakh cr). The early-June DII datapoint appears as a separate small callout, not inside the main Jan–May bars.
- **Composition notes:** Reader sees the two opposing flows first — foreign exit and domestic entry. The full FII bar is the supply that needed to be absorbed; the residual is domestic buying in excess of foreign selling. Monthly DII bars can be shown as a stacked inset to highlight the March and May pulses. A bottom annotation notes that five-month 2026 FII selling already exceeds full-year 2025. Add a source-definition line: "Kotak Neo estimate, Jan–May 2026; NSDL/NSC and other broker estimates differ by segment and date." Add two caveat lines: (1) DII buying reflects both fresh SIP/pension/insurance flows and institutional deployment of existing liquidity; (2) the ownership flip can be read as structural, cyclical, or valuation-driven (see Concept 6). Payoff: India’s market is no longer hostage to a single marginal buyer — but the durability of that shift is contested.
- **Sources:** Kotak Neo, 9 Jun 2026; Moneycontrol, 5 May 2026.

### Concept 2: The Ownership Flip
- **Central insight:** For the first time in modern Indian market history, domestic institutions own more of Indian equities than foreign portfolio investors — the April 2026 FPI ownership reading (14.7%) is a 14-year low.
- **Data to include:** FPI ownership 14.7% (14-year low) vs DII ownership 18.9% (record high) in April 2026; confirming Nifty 500 read: DII 20.9% vs FPI 17.1% as of March 2026; FII-to-DII ownership ratio 0.8x.
- **Visualization type:** Split-bar or side-by-side comparison with a subtle historical trace (small ghost bars for prior cycle highs/lows, e.g., FPI ownership peak vs current trough).
- **Composition notes:** Two horizontal bars dominate: the DII bar longer than the FPI bar for the first time. A thin ratio line below collapses from above 1.0x to 0.8x. Color coding makes domestic and foreign ownership instantly distinguishable. Add a visible caveat that the flip can be read as structural, cyclical, or valuation-driven; direct the reader to Concept 6 for the three live interpretations. Payoff: the marginal price-setter has changed nationality — for now.
- **Sources:** Wright Research / JM Financial, 9 Jun 2026; Motilal Oswal via Multibagg.ai, 7 May 2026.

### Concept 3: The Split Market
- **Central insight:** Foreign selling weighed on the headline indices, but the domestic-flow-heavy mid- and small-cap segments barely moved — a two-speed market hidden inside one country.
- **Data to include:** BSE Sensex -13.7%; Nifty 50 -11.5%; BSE MidCap 150 -2.6%; BSE SmallCap 250 -0.5% (2026 through early June).
- **Visualization type:** Diverging slope chart or vertical lollipop chart. Four indices arranged left (large-cap) to right (small-cap), with red intensity fading toward neutral.
- **Composition notes:** Reader’s eye moves from the deeply negative Sensex/Nifty on the left to the nearly flat small-cap on the right. A short annotation explains *why*: FPI ownership is concentrated in large-cap financials/IT; domestic SIPs flow disproportionately into broader funds. Payoff: the index level is no longer the whole story.
- **Sources:** Kotak Neo, 9 Jun 2026.

### Concept 4: The Automatic Bid
- **Central insight:** In May 2026, 9.64 crore SIP accounts deployed ₹30,954 crore, keeping equity mutual-fund inflows positive for the 63rd straight month — even as equity-MF inflows fell 40% month-on-month and account-level SIP exits exceeded starts in March–April.
- **Data to include:** May 2026 SIP inflow ₹30,954 crore; contributing accounts 9.64 crore; SIP assets ₹17.12 lakh crore (~21% of industry AUM); equity MF inflows ₹22,907.77 crore (lowest in 12 months but +63 consecutive months).
- **Visualization type:** Layered timeline. Top band: monthly SIP inflows above a ₹30,000 crore threshold line; bottom band: cumulative count of positive equity-MF months climbing to 63.
- **Composition notes:** A horizontal threshold line at ₹30,000 crore with monthly bars consistently clearing it. The 63-month streak is shown as a simple counter or progress bar. Add a prominent caveat: equity MF inflows fell 40% MoM in May, and SIP stoppage ratios crossed 100% in Mar–Apr (more accounts ended than started). Rupee stickiness is not the same as account stickiness. Payoff: domestic flows are sticky, but they are not automatic or unstoppable.
- **Sources:** AMFI via The Economic Times, 10 Jun 2026; Angel One, 10 Jun 2026.

### Concept 5: RBI’s Buffer vs. the Rupee
- **Central insight:** The rupee fell ~7% to a record low on currency pressure from crude-import dollar demand plus FII repatriation; the RBI held rates and kept a $682.3 billion reserve buffer plus capital-account measures rather than hiking.
- **Data to include:** USD/INR ~₹89 at start of 2026 to ~₹96.34 record low on 19 May 2026 (~7% depreciation); RBI FX reserves $682.3 billion as of 29 May 2026 — buffer for intervention; repo rate held at 5.25% (5 Jun 2026); capital-account measures: FAR expansion, FCNR(B) hedging support, NRI/OCI equity limits, concessional forex-swap window for PSU ECBs until 30 Sep 2026.
- **Visualization type:** Two-panel small-multiple. Left panel: rupee path with reserve-buffer shading underneath; right panel: policy-rate decision shown as a held-steady marker surrounded by the capital-account toolkit.
- **Composition notes:** Reader first sees the rupee slide, then notices the reserve cushion shaded below the path, then the policy panel showing "no hike." Label the reserve line as "$682.3 bn forex reserves as of 29 May 2026 — buffer for intervention"; do not use the verb "used." A connecting arrow makes clear the RBI chose capital-account tools over rates. Payoff: the external-sector shock is being managed, not ignored.
- **Sources:** Univest, 19 May 2026; The Hindu BusinessLine, 5 Jun 2026.

### Concept 6: Three Ways to Read the Flip (caveat panel)
- **Central insight:** The same ownership flip can be read as structural domesticization, a cyclical global risk-off rotation, or a valuation reset after years of India premium — the evidence is not yet conclusive.
- **Data to include:**
  - **Structural:** DII ownership at record highs, FPI at multi-year lows; SIP inflows above ₹30k cr for three consecutive months; EPFO, NPS and insurance provide non-discretionary equity allocations.
  - **Cyclical:** FII selling is correlated with higher US yields, a stronger dollar and geopolitical risk; India fundamentals such as GDP growth and FDI remain positive.
  - **Valuation:** India’s market-cap-to-GDP ratio was elevated (~125%) entering 2026; Nifty forward multiples were near historical highs; FII selling is concentrated in the most-owned large-caps.
  - **Key unknowns from research §8:** whether the FPI exit is cyclical or structural; whether SIP inflows will hold if returns stay weak; whether the RBI’s capital-account package will attract enough dollars; whether earnings will recover to FY27 forecasts; the oil/geopolitical path.
- **Visualization type:** Three small equal panels (structural / cyclical / valuation) plus a fourth "known unknowns" box, each with an icon, a one-line label and one "for" / "against" bullet.
- **Composition notes:** Place this as a small panel adjacent to Concept 2 or at the bottom of the infographic set. It signals that the structural-domesticization story is contested and prevents the set from reading like consensus cheerleading. It should not dominate; it exists to add intellectual honesty. Reference it from Concepts 1, 2 and 4.
- **Sources:** Research artifact §5 and §8.

## What to avoid

- **FII outflow as a standalone scary number.** ₹3.18 lakh crore is dramatic, but without the DII absorption figure it tells the reader only that foreigners are selling — a story already everywhere in financial media.
- **Monthly ownership percentages without the comparison.** A single DII or FPI ownership percentage is not visually interesting until placed against the other and against history.
- **Rupee depreciation without the reserve/policy context.** A falling rupee chart invites panic; the interesting insight is the RBI’s response, so the rupee must be paired with reserves and the MPC decision.
- **Sector-level selling maps.** Financials accounted for ~51% of March FII selling, but the data is limited to one month and one source. A sector-concentration chart would be thin unless matched with a comparable DII sector-buying data set, which is available only as a broad claim (21 of 24 sectors) rather than precise monthly rupee flows.
- **FDI vs FPI full-year comparison.** While structurally interesting ($94.5 bn gross FDI vs -$16.67 bn net FPI), the FY26 fiscal year does not align cleanly with the Jan–May 2026 equity-flow window that drives the signal. It is better used as a footnote than a central visual.
- **Presenting any single FII outflow estimate as the official figure.** Kotak Neo, NSDL/NSC, Wright/JM and Angel One produce materially different numbers because of segment and date differences. Always include a source-definition line.
- **A one-sided structural-domesticization story.** The research itself labels the structural interpretation as contested; the infographic must give the reader a visual cue that cyclical and valuation readings are also live.
