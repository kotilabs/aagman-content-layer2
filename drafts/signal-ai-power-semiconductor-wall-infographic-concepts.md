## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: Where the money goes vs. where the wall is
- **Central insight:** Hyperscalers are pouring ~$700 B into AI infrastructure in 2026, but the components that actually deliver power to that compute now have longer lead times than the GPUs themselves.
- **Data to include:**
  - Top 4 US hyperscalers combined capex: **>$700 B in 2026**, up from **~$410 B in 2025**; roughly **75% on AI infrastructure** (Fortune / Accuris).
  - NVIDIA FY2026 data-center revenue: **$193.7 B** (+68% YoY) (NVIDIA).
  - PMIC lead times: **35–40 weeks**; BMC IC lead times: **21–26 weeks** (TrendForce / The Register).
  - Average power-transformer lead times: **~120 weeks in 2024**, generator step-up transformers **>160 weeks by Q1 2026** (Wood Mackenzie).
- **Visualization type:** Two-panel comparison / split funnel — left panel "Compute layer," right panel "Power layer."
- **Composition notes:** Put the $700 B headline at the top. Left panel uses blue tones and shows AI capex and NVIDIA revenue as big bars. Right panel uses orange/red tones and shows lead-time bars in weeks. The longest bar in the whole graphic should be the transformer lead time, not the revenue bar — that visual surprise is the payoff.
- **Sources:** Fortune (https://fortune.com/2026/04/30/big-tech-hyperscalers-will-spend-700-billion-on-ai-infrastructure-this-year-with-no-clear-end-in-sight-eye-on-ai/), Accuris (https://accuristech.com/blog/ai-data-center-electronic-component-supply/), NVIDIA (https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/nvidia-announces-financial-results-for-fourth-quarter-and-fiscal-2026), TrendForce/The Register (https://www.trendforce.com/presscenter/news/20260415-13013.html ; https://www.theregister.com/on-prem/2026/04/23/ai-now-gobbling-up-power-and-management-chips-for-servers/5229166), Wood Mackenzie (https://www.woodmac.com/news/opinion/supply-shortages-and-an-inflexible-market-give-rise-to-high-power-transformer-lead-times/).

### Concept 2: The shrinking supply base behind the AI power surge
- **Central insight:** AI power chips are built on mature 8-inch wafers, yet global 8-inch capacity is expected to shrink ~2.4% in 2026 just as PMIC lead times stretch to 35–40 weeks.
- **Data to include:**
  - Global 8-inch wafer foundry capacity: **-2.4% in 2026** (TrendForce via SemiMedia).
  - PMIC lead times: **35–40 weeks**; BMC IC lead times: **21–26 weeks** (TrendForce / The Register).
  - AI-server shipments: **~28% YoY growth**; general-server shipments: **~13% YoY** (downgraded from ~20%) (TrendForce).
  - Data-center electricity demand: **+17% in 2025** (IEA).
- **Visualization type:** Divergence / slope chart — demand lines rising, supply lines falling.
- **Composition notes:** Show four sloped lines from a common baseline year. AI-server growth and data-center power demand rise to the right; 8-inch capacity and general-server growth slope downward. Label the widening vertical gap in the middle "power IC squeeze." The reader sees the crossing first, then the labels explain why it matters.
- **Sources:** TrendForce via SemiMedia (https://www.semimedia.cc/20543.html), TrendForce/The Register (https://www.trendforce.com/presscenter/news/20260415-13013.html ; https://www.theregister.com/on-prem/2026/04/23/ai-now-gobbling-up-power-and-management-chips-for-servers/5229166), IEA (https://www.iea.org/news/data-centre-electricity-use-surged-in-2025-even-with-tightening-bottlenecks-driving-a-scramble-for-solutions).

### Concept 3: AI is eating the component queue
- **Central insight:** Suppliers are prioritizing higher-margin AI server orders at the same time the mature-node supply base is shrinking, so AI server shipments are growing ~28% while general-purpose server growth was cut by roughly a third.
- **Data to include:**
  - AI-server shipments: **~28% YoY**; general-server shipments: **~13% YoY** (downgraded from **~20%**) (TrendForce).
  - Global 8-inch wafer foundry capacity: **-2.4% in 2026** (TrendForce via SemiMedia).
  - Data centers projected to consume **~70% of memory chips** in 2026 (Tom's Hardware).
  - HBM consumes **23% of total DRAM wafer capacity** (Tech Insider).
  - Hyperscaler AI infrastructure capex: roughly **75% of $600 B+** (Fortune / Accuris).
- **Visualization type:** Side-by-side bar chart plus a small allocation donut.
- **Composition notes:** Left side: two bars comparing AI-server growth (28%) and general-server growth (13%), with a small downward arrow or callout noting that 8-inch capacity is shrinking ~2.4% in 2026. Right side: a donut showing 70% of memory chips going to data centers, with a callout that 23% of DRAM wafer capacity is HBM. Arrows connect the two panels to show AI demand pulling supply away from general-purpose servers. Payoff: the shortage is not just more demand — it is reallocative demand into a shrinking supply base.
- **Sources:** TrendForce (https://www.trendforce.com/presscenter/news/20260415-13013.html), TrendForce via SemiMedia (https://www.semimedia.cc/20543.html), Tom's Hardware (https://www.tomshardware.com/pc-components/ram/data-centers-will-consume-70-percent-of-memory-chips-made-in-2026-supply-shortfall-will-cause-the-chip-shortage-to-spread-to-other-segments), Tech Insider (https://tech-insider.org/memory-chip-shortage-2026-ai-consumer-electronics/), Fortune/Accuris (https://fortune.com/2026/04/30/big-tech-hyperscalers-will-spend-700-billion-on-ai-infrastructure-this-year-with-no-clear-end-in-sight-eye-on-ai/ ; https://accuristech.com/blog/ai-data-center-electronic-component-supply/).

### Concept 4: The interconnection time bomb
- **Central insight:** It takes more than seven years on average to bring an AI data center online in PJM — longer than the time needed to scale chip production — because grid interconnection and electrical equipment have become the binding constraint.
- **Data to include:**
  - PJM AI projects entering service in 2025: **>7 years average** to operation; **>3 years** to interconnection service agreement and **~4 more years** after approval (Data Center Knowledge / PJM).
  - Power-transformer lead times: **~50 weeks in 2021 → ~120 weeks in 2024**; generator step-up transformers **>160 weeks by Q1 2026** (Wood Mackenzie).
  - US data-center electrical equipment market: **$20 B in 2026 → $65 B by 2030** (Wood Mackenzie).
  - TSMC CoWoS capacity: roughly **35,000 wafers/month in late 2024 → 120,000–140,000 in 2026** (TrendForce) — optional comparison to show chip capacity can scale faster.
- **Visualization type:** Horizontal timeline / Gantt-style comparison.
- **Composition notes:** Top track: chip-scale milestones (NVIDIA revenue ramp, CoWoS expansion) measured in months to a few years. Bottom track: PJM project timeline and transformer lead times measured in years. Use a long red bar for the PJM >7-year span. Add a $20 B → $65 B callout above the equipment track. Payoff: the real wait is for power, not silicon.
- **Sources:** Data Center Knowledge/PJM (https://www.datacenterknowledge.com/energy-power-supply/why-ai-data-center-projects-face-years-of-delays-after-approval), Wood Mackenzie (https://www.woodmac.com/news/opinion/supply-shortages-and-an-inflexible-market-give-rise-to-high-power-transformer-lead-times/ ; https://www.woodmac.com/press-releases/data-center-demand-drives-us-electrical-equipment-market-to-$65b-reshaping-industry-dynamics/), TrendForce (https://www.trendforce.com/news/2026/06/15/news-tsmc-cowos-supply-demand-gap-reportedly-seen-narrowing-from-20-to-10-by-end-of-2026-as-capacity-expands/).

### Concept 5: The invisible electrical backbone
- **Central insight:** Every megawatt of AI capacity requires tons of copper, transformers, and switchgear; the US data-center electrical-equipment market is set to more than triple to $65 B by 2030.
- **Data to include:**
  - US data-center electrical equipment market: **$20 B in 2026 → $65 B by 2030** (Wood Mackenzie).
  - Padmount transformer demand in hyperscale data centers: **1,573 units in 2025 → 9,395 units by 2030** (Wood Mackenzie).
  - AI data-center copper demand: **~400,000 tonnes/year average**, peaking at **572,000 tonnes in 2028**; benchmark **~27 tonnes of copper per MW** (BloombergNEF via Carbon Credits).
  - Global AI data-center capex: **$400–450 B in 2026**, with **over half spent on chips inside devices** (Deloitte) — use to frame the non-chip remainder.
- **Visualization type:** Stacked flow / Sankey from total AI capex down to electrical equipment and raw materials.
- **Composition notes:** Start with the $400–450 B total AI data-center capex bar. Split it into "chips inside devices" (>50%) and "everything else" (land, construction, power, cooling). Zoom into the "everything else" portion to show electrical equipment growing $20 B → $65 B and copper demand peaking at 572 kt. Add padmount transformer unit growth as a small rising chart. Payoff: power infrastructure is a fast-growing, supply-constrained market of its own.
- **Sources:** Wood Mackenzie (https://www.woodmac.com/press-releases/data-center-demand-drives-us-electrical-equipment-market-to-$65b-reshaping-industry-dynamics/), BloombergNEF via Carbon Credits (https://carboncredits.com/data-centers-copper-hunger-how-ai-is-driving-a-looming-supply-crunch/), Deloitte (https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/industry/technology/technology-media-and-telecom-predictions/2026/compute-power-ai.html).

## What to avoid

- A generic "AI is booming" chart of NVIDIA revenue alone — it misses the power-semiconductor bottleneck that defines this signal.
- Merging HBM/CoWoS shortages with power-IC shortages without clearly labeling the compute layer vs. the infrastructure layer.
- Using the secondary-market figure of ~$197 B for NVIDIA FY2026 data-center revenue; the audited number is **$193.7 B**.
- Rounding lead times or capex into vague ranges (e.g., "months" instead of specific weeks, or "hundreds of billions" instead of $700 B).
- A geographic heat map — location-level power-constraint data is not in the research artifact; stay at the verified US / PJM level.
- Showing data-center power demand growth without the baseline/total-electricity context, which could misread the absolute share of global electricity.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with **Concepts 1–5**. All five are grounded in verified data from the research artifact and each makes a distinct visual argument:

1. **Concept 1** is the strongest lead — it directly contrasts money spent with lead-time reality.
2. **Concept 2** is the best supply-side divergence chart.
3. **Concept 3** works as the reallocation / cannibalization story.
4. **Concept 4** is the most compelling timeline for the grid-interconnection angle.
5. **Concept 5** best captures the commodity and electrical-equipment supercycle.

If only one or two cuts are commissioned, prioritize **Concept 1** (money vs. wall) and **Concept 4** (interconnection time bomb) because they most clearly advance the operator's stated goal: move the narrative beyond GPU design winners to infrastructure enablers.
