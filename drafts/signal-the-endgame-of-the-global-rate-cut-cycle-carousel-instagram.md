## Slide 1

**Kicker:** Base case

**Headline:** The global rate-cut cycle is largely over.

**Body:** For most of 2024 and early 2025, the question was how fast central banks would cut. Now the question is what happens if they stop cutting while rates stay well above the near-zero floors of the 2010s. This is the base-case interpretation — not a certainty.

**Hero stat / quote:** Fed funds target range held at 3.50–3.75% since December 2025; FOMC projects 3.8% at end-2026 and a longer-run rate of 3.1%.

**Source:** FXStreet / Advisor Perspectives, June 2026 FOMC dot plot

**Design note:** Clean typographic split — left side "2024–25: how fast?", right side "2026: what if they stop?" — in muted navy and cream.

**Speaker note:** Establishes the shift in the central-bank conversation and frames the rest of the deck as a base-case reading, not a settled fact.

## Slide 2

**Kicker:** Verified

**Headline:** The Fed is on hold, and its own projections moved up.

**Body:** Between March and June 2026, the FOMC raised its 2026 fed funds projection from 3.4% to 3.8%. The policy path is being revised upward, not downward.

**Hero stat / quote:** +40 bps (3.4% → 3.8%) on the 2026 projection.

**Source:** intellectia.ai, Fed Dot Plot June 2026

**Design note:** A simple two-dot line chart: March projection lower, June projection higher, with the +40 bps gap marked in a contrasting accent.

**Speaker note:** Shows that the Fed's revealed stance is hawkish relative to any lingering expectation of easier policy.

**Hero stat / quote:** 2026 PCE inflation projection: 3.6% YoY; US CPI: 4.2% YoY.

## Slide 5

**Kicker:** The balance sheet

**Headline:** The world is carrying elevated debt into a higher-rate pause.

**Body:** Governments, corporates, and households refinanced at historically low rates in 2020–21. Now new issuance and rolled-over debt reset at higher coupons. Fiscal space narrows even without a recession.

**Hero stat / quote:** Gross global debt projected at 100% of GDP by 2029; global government interest payments near 3% of global GDP.

**Source:** CRFB / IMF Fiscal Monitor, May 2026

**Design note:** A rising debt bar with a small interest-payment callout growing as a share of the bar; keep the palette sober.

**Speaker note:** Introduces the debt-sustainability channel that makes this pause structurally different from a normal mid-cycle hold.

## Slide 6

**Kicker:** The Aagman thesis

**Headline:** This isn't a pause. It's a regime.

**Body:** Rates are too low to force rapid deleveraging and too high to allow the aggressive stimulus markets grew used to in the 2010s. The policy rate now anchors a higher risk-free hurdle for valuing long-duration cash flows.

**Hero stat / quote:** "A sustained policy rate of 3.5–4.0% anchors a higher risk-free hurdle."

**Source:** Aagman mechanism note

**Design note:** Bold centered headline with the mechanism quote as a pull-quote beneath, set in a slightly lighter weight.

**Speaker note:** Names the thesis explicitly and pairs the headline with the valuation mechanism so it reads as an analytical conclusion, not a headline-only assertion.

## Slide 7

**Kicker:** Discounting

**Headline:** The policy rate anchors a higher risk-free hurdle.

**Body:** The actual risk-free discount rate for a ten-year cash flow is the Treasury yield curve, not the overnight fed funds rate. But the policy rate is the anchor that pulls it higher. A cash flow ten years out is worth less at 4% than at 1%.

**Hero stat / quote:** Fed longer-run rate: 3.1%, versus a near-zero floor through much of the 2010s.

**Source:** Advisor Perspectives, June 2026 FOMC dot plot

**Design note:** Two discount curves — one flat near 1%, one near 4% — with a present-value annotation falling on the higher curve.

**Speaker note:** Clarifies the policy-rate / Treasury-curve distinction while showing the duration math.

## Slide 8

**Kicker:** Transmission

**Headline:** Valuation discipline returns to long-duration assets.

**Body:** Assets priced for permanently low rates now face a higher bar on cash-flow growth and credit quality. Long-duration equities, parts of commercial real estate, and leveraged credit are the most exposed.

**Hero stat / quote:** "Long-duration cash flows face the most pressure."

**Source:** Aagman cross-asset framework

**Design note:** A duration timeline from short to long; risk icons cluster at the long end, with the headline at the top.

**Speaker note:** Applies the thesis to asset allocation and avoids implying every asset in each category is uniformly mispriced.

**Headline:** In real terms, policy may still be accommodative.

**Body:** With headline CPI at 4.2% and the Fed funds target range at 3.50–3.75%, the ex-post real Fed funds rate is roughly -0.5% to -0.7%. That is the strongest case against the "tight money" story.

**Hero stat / quote:** Ex-post real Fed funds rate: ~-0.5% to -0.7% versus headline CPI.

**Source:** Aagman calculation / Cambridge Currencies, May 2026

**Design note:** A simple real-rate equation: nominal rate minus inflation equals a small negative result, shown in a single line of large type.

**Speaker note:** Surfaces the main competing interpretation to preserve epistemic honesty.
