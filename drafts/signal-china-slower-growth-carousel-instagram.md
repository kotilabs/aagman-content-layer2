# Instagram Standalone Carousel — China Slower Growth

**Signal ID:** china-slower-growth  
**Format:** Instagram standalone carousel (9 slides)  
**Voice:** FT leader column × Matt Levine — dry, confident, structurally argued, lightly literary  
**Notes:** No Substack CTA. No external link. The carousel tells the full story and ends with an open question.

---

## Slide 1 — The split

**Kicker:** The split

**Headline:** China’s economy is slowing in two directions at once.

**Body:**
May retail sales contracted year-on-year for the first time in this cycle. Exports surged to a record on AI shipments and front-loading. The domestic side is shrinking; the external side is accelerating.

**Hero stat / quote:**
Retail sales -0.6% y/y in May 2026; exports +19.4% y/y to a record USD 376.78 billion. — National Bureau of Statistics of China; General Administration of Customs via Trading Economics

**Design note:**
Split-screen layout. Left: a muted red downward arrow over an empty shopping street. Right: a muted green upward arrow over a container ship. Two numbers dominate the frame. No gradients — flat, high-contrast blocks.

**Speaker note:**
Open with the central tension: this is not a single-speed slowdown. The reader needs to see the divergence immediately.

---

## Slide 2 — Households

**Kicker:** Households

**Headline:** Chinese consumers pulled back for the first time this cycle.

**Body:**
Weak income expectations, falling housing wealth, and precautionary saving pushed May retail sales into negative territory. The month-on-month figure was also negative — this is not a base-effect blip.

**Hero stat / quote:**
May retail sales -0.6% y/y; Jan–May +1.4% y/y; month-on-month -0.38%. — National Bureau of Statistics of China

**Design note:**
One large -0.6% in a restrained red. Background: a blurred, quiet retail corridor. Minimal text — let the contraction be the visual.

**Speaker note:**
Ground the slowdown in the consumer. Domestic demand carries the larger GDP weight, so this is the slide that sets the stakes.

---

## Slide 3 — Balance sheets

**Kicker:** Balance sheets

**Headline:** The property slump has become a credit contraction.

**Body:**
Property investment keeps falling at a double-digit rate. Home prices have declined for 35 consecutive months. Households are repaying debt rather than borrowing — April saw the largest monthly household-debt drop on record.

**Hero stat / quote:**
Property investment -16.2% y/y in Jan–May 2026; new home prices -3.5% y/y, the 35th consecutive annual decline. — National Bureau of Statistics of China via Trading Economics

**Design note:**
A descending staircase chart made from building silhouettes. Each step labels a data point: investment, prices, loan growth. Dark, architectural palette.

**Speaker note:**
Connect the property sector to the credit channel. The feedback loop into local government finances and bank lending is the mechanism.

---

## Slide 4 — The mask

**Kicker:** The mask

**Headline:** Factories and exports are hiding the weakness.

**Body:**
Industrial production rebounded in May. Exports hit a record, driven by semiconductors, integrated circuits, industrial robots, and rare earths. The external track is real production — but it is not broad enough to offset property and consumption.

**Hero stat / quote:**
Industrial production +4.5% y/y in May 2026; exports +19.4% y/y to a record USD 376.78 billion. — National Bureau of Statistics of China; General Administration of Customs via Trading Economics

**Design note:**
A container ship silhouette filled with microchip and robot icons. One bold line: exports up and to the right. Clean, technical blue against grey.

**Speaker note:**
Show the offset. This explains why headline GDP has not collapsed — and why the headline number is misleading.

---

## Slide 5 — Prices

**Kicker:** Prices

**Headline:** Consumer prices are too low; producer prices are rising for the wrong reason.

**Body:**
CPI and core CPI are near 1%, well below the official target. PPI is up because of energy and commodity costs, not final demand. Manufacturers are absorbing input costs through margin compression — a symptom of excess supply.

**Hero stat / quote:**
May CPI +1.2% y/y; core CPI +1.1% y/y; PPI index 103.8 (2025=100). — National Bureau of Statistics of China via Trading Economics

**Design note:**
Two diverging lines on a simple chart: a flat, pale consumer-price line and a rising, darker producer-price line. One italic label: *margin compression*.

**Speaker note:**
Use inflation to reveal the two-speed structure. The price data proves the weakness is demand-side, not supply-constrained.

---

## Slide 6 — Thesis

**Kicker:** Thesis

**Headline:** This isn’t a growth collapse. It’s a domestic-demand recession dressed up as a trade boom.

**Body:**
Q1 GDP grew 5.0% year-on-year, but April–May activity data point to a materially weaker Q2. The export boom is masking a broader contraction in domestic spending — and domestic demand is the heavier half of the economy.

**Hero stat / quote:**
“April–May data show a noticeable slowdown… if confirmed by June data, Q2 growth would fall significantly short of Q1’s 5%.” — Piraeus Group, *Global Macro Trends — June 2026*

**Design note:**
A single bold statement centered on a neutral background. Two small scales at the bottom: one large pan labelled *domestic demand*, one smaller pan labelled *exports*. The visual is the argument.

**Speaker note:**
This is the pivot slide. Name the thesis explicitly so the reader can carry it through the second half.

---

## Slide 7 — Beijing

**Kicker:** Beijing

**Headline:** The government has already downgraded its ambition.

**Body:**
The 2026 growth target was lowered to 4.5%–5.0%. The PBOC has pledged accommodative policy, but the response so far is targeted — rate and RRR cuts, not a large demand-side stimulus.

**Hero stat / quote:**
2026 GDP growth target lowered to 4.5%–5.0%; fiscal deficit target ~4% of GDP. — NPC / MarketWatch; China Daily / TheoryChina

**Design note:**
A target range that visibly steps down from the prior 5% mark. Small icons: a bank, a bond, a gear. Muted government palette — navy, grey, off-white.

**Speaker note:**
Address the policy counterweight. Beijing has room, but it is choosing restraint — for now.

---

## Slide 8 — Everywhere else

**Kicker:** Everywhere else

**Headline:** China’s shortfall is being exported through lower import demand.

**Body:**
With investment and consumption soft, China is buying less iron ore, base metals, and Asian intermediate goods. The currency is stable because the trade surplus is record-high — so the adjustment is happening through volume, not a weaker yuan.

**Hero stat / quote:**
Iron ore fell 8.18% over the past month to USD 100.33/metric ton on 26 June 2026. — Trading Economics

**Design note:**
A simplified commodity route map: ships moving from Australia/Brazil toward China, with a red volume bar shrinking. Iron ore price as the focal number.

**Speaker note:**
Show the global spillover. The signal matters because China is the second-largest economy and largest goods exporter.

---

## Slide 9 — The question

**Kicker:** The question

**Headline:** Is the export boom real AI demand, or just front-running the next tariff?

**Body:**
If the surge is durable tech demand, the external sector can keep masking domestic weakness. If it is front-loading ahead of energy-price and tariff uncertainty, the strongest offset will fade in the second half — and Q2 may look like the start of a longer downshift.

**Hero stat / quote:**
May exports +19.4% y/y to a record USD 376.78 billion. — General Administration of Customs via Trading Economics

**Design note:**
Two diverging arrows from a single export number. One arrow labelled *structural*, the other *front-loading*. Clean, open-ended — leave visual space for the reader to sit with the question.

**Speaker note:**
End with the unresolved variable. No CTA, no link — just the distinction that will decide what happens next.
