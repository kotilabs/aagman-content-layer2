**The House After the Truce**

Think of two old business partners who have spent years sharing a house. They stop speaking in the spring. They shout for a month. Then they sit down over dinner and agree to lower their voices. The shouting stops. But the locks on the doors stay. The separate bank accounts stay. The list of rooms each is forbidden to enter stays. Outsiders hear the silence and assume the partnership is repaired. Inside the house, nothing fundamental has changed. The peace is administrative, not emotional.

That is roughly where global trade sits in mid-2026.

### Context: What Changed, and What Did Not

The headlines from 2025 suggested a thaw. On May 11, 2025, the United States and China agreed to reduce reciprocal tariffs by 115 percentage points for 90 days, leaving a 10% baseline tariff on each side. The October 30, 2025 Trump–Xi meeting in Busan produced a further step: China agreed to issue general export licenses for rare earths, gallium, germanium, antimony, and graphite, and suspended additional rare-earth controls it had announced earlier that month.

These were real developments. They lowered the *headline* temperature.

But the underlying architecture of restriction remained intact. Section 301 tariffs on China are still in force. Section 232 tariffs on steel, aluminum, copper, and autos are still in force. A 10% Section 122 global import surcharge remains in effect as of July 2026, scheduled to expire on July 24, 2026. Semiconductor export controls were not removed in January 2026; they were *recast* into a case-by-case licensing regime by the U.S. Bureau of Industry and Security. New Section 301 investigations, including one into Chinese semiconductors completed in December 2025, carry a potential tariff increase in 2027.

So the question being discussed now is not whether trade friction has disappeared. It is whether the current equilibrium — calmer headlines, persistent restrictions, rerouted supply chains — is stable.

### Mechanism: How This Actually Works

The signal operates through three reinforcing channels. Each is worth separating, because they produce different effects for different actors.

**1. Tariff and regulatory stacking**

A single shipment of Chinese-origin goods can face multiple layers of duty and control: the standard MFN tariff, the Section 301 rate (up to 25% on many categories, 50% on some semiconductor items), the Section 232 sectoral tariff if it is a metal or auto, the 10% Section 122 surcharge, and fentanyl-related IEEPA duties whose legal status has shifted after court rulings.

This means the *effective* trade barrier for a given product is not the headline U.S.–China rate. It is the sum of overlapping duties, each with its own legal basis, product coverage, and enforcement mechanism. The exact effective rate is shipment-specific and hard to pin down.

The January 2026 BIS case-by-case licensing policy for advanced AI chips is a good example of the same logic in non-tariff form. The U.S. did not lift the ceiling on China-bound chip sales. It replaced a blanket presumption of denial with a controlled process: eligible chips must meet strict performance criteria, undergo U.S.-based testing, and face a 50% cap on China/Macau shipments. The policy changed shape. The restriction did not go away.

**2. Supply-chain rerouting, not reshoring**

The trade data are unambiguous. U.S. imports from China fell by $97.1 billion in January–October 2025 compared with the same period in 2024. China’s share of total U.S. imports dropped to 9.3% from 13.4% in 2024 — close to the 8.9% share it held in 2000, the year before it joined the WTO.

At the same time, imports from other economies rose sharply. U.S. goods imports from Vietnam reached $193.8 billion in 2025, up 42.0% year-on-year. Mexico became the top U.S. goods trading partner, with total bilateral goods trade of $872.8 billion. U.S. imports from India rose to $103.8 billion.

This is not reshoring. This is “China-plus-one” restructuring: final assembly or late-stage processing moves to a third country, while Chinese components, capital equipment, and intermediate goods continue to flow into those hubs. India’s trade deficit with China — $99.2 billion in fiscal 2024–25 and projected to widen to roughly $111.4 billion in 2025–26 — is a useful reality check. India is gaining U.S. market share in electronics and pharmaceuticals while still importing Chinese inputs and active pharmaceutical ingredients.

**3. Investment and capital-flow alignment**

The U.S. has used trade negotiations to extract investment and purchase commitments, not just tariff reductions. India committed to $500 billion in U.S. purchases over five years. Japan and South Korea made large investment commitments.

These deals tie market access to capital placement inside the United States. They reinforce a bloc-like architecture in which trade policy and industrial policy have become hard to separate.

Formally, an estimated 72% of world trade is still covered by MFN rules. But with WTO dispute-settlement enforcement currently paralyzed, much of that coverage lacks a practical backstop. Strategic sectors — semiconductors, critical minerals, batteries, autos, steel — are increasingly governed by bilateral deals, investment pledges, and export controls rather than multilateral rules.

### Signal / Deviation: What Is Unusual

The most striking deviation is the gap between headline and effective trade policy.

Global trade volumes are not collapsing. World merchandise trade grew 4.6% in 2025, and global trade hit a record nominal value of $35 trillion, according to UNCTAD. Yet the WTO forecasts growth to slow to just 1.9% in 2026. Services trade growth is expected to ease from 5.3% to 4.8%. So we have a world of record nominal trade alongside a sharp deceleration in real volume growth.

China’s trade data add another layer of complexity. In January–May 2026, China recorded a $451.7 billion trade surplus, with May alone posting a $105.4 billion surplus and exports up 19.4% year-on-year, according to the General Administration of Customs China. The U.S. has reduced its direct dependence on Chinese exports. China has, at least partly, compensated by exporting more to the rest of the world.

### Interpretation Spectrum: Four Plausible Readings

**Interpretation A: Managed decoupling is working.**

*Who makes it:* U.S. administration officials, the 2026 Economic Report of the President, and some reshoring advocates.

*Evidence supporting:* U.S. imports from China have fallen materially; China’s import share is back to pre-WTO levels; Mexico, Vietnam, and India have gained share; large investment commitments are flowing into U.S. strategic sectors.

*Evidence contradicting:* The U.S. trade deficit has not disappeared. It has shifted to Vietnam, Mexico, and Taiwan. Many “alternative” suppliers still rely on Chinese inputs.

*Why it might be wrong:* It conflates rerouting with genuine reshoring. The U.S. remains dependent on Asian manufacturing. It has simply changed the mailing address.

**Interpretation B: This is fragmentation, not deglobalisation — and global trade is still growing.**

*Who makes it:* The WTO, UNCTAD, and analysts who emphasize that trade volumes remain positive.

*Evidence supporting:* World merchandise trade grew 4.6% in 2025; global trade reached a record $35 trillion; AI-related goods trade has surged.

*Evidence contradicting:* 2026 growth is forecast at only 1.9%; much of 2025’s strength was front-loading ahead of tariffs; regional blocs and compliance costs are rising.

*Why it might be wrong:* It understates how much of the 2025 trade surge was a one-off pull-forward and how much persistent uncertainty is weighing on long-term investment.

**Interpretation C: China is exporting its way through containment.**

*Who makes it:* China-focused analysts pointing to China’s record trade surplus.

*Evidence supporting:* China’s January–May 2026 surplus was $451.7 billion; exports accelerated to $376.8 billion in May; China has diverted goods to ASEAN, Latin America, and other markets.

*Evidence contradicting:* U.S.-bound exports have fallen; China’s domestic demand remains weak; advanced semiconductor access is constrained.

*Why it might be wrong:* A rising surplus with the rest of the world may invite new trade barriers, and over-reliance on exports leaves China exposed to a global demand slowdown.

**Interpretation D: India is the biggest structural winner.**

*Who makes it:* India-focused investors and the Indian government.

*Evidence supporting:* India is now the U.S.’s fourth-largest trading partner; the February 2026 framework cut U.S. tariffs on Indian goods to 18%; the U.S. goods trade deficit with India reached $58.4 billion in 2025, up from lower levels, reflecting rising Indian exports.

*Evidence contradicting:* India’s deficit with China is widening; India’s exports are concentrated in services, pharmaceuticals, and gems; manufacturing competitiveness lags Vietnam in several sectors.

*Why it might be wrong:* Improved U.S. access is not the same as broad-based competitiveness. India is gaining share, but it is not yet replacing China at scale in electronics or machinery. Note that India’s reported $41.2 billion surplus with the U.S. in fiscal 2024–25 measures a different basket and period than the U.S. Census Bureau’s $58.4 billion calendar-2025 goods deficit; they should not be read as the same number.

Our view, clearly labeled as interpretation: the fragmentation thesis best fits the data. Trade volumes have held up, but the routing, composition, and governance of trade have shifted. This is a more useful frame than either “decoupling” or “deglobalisation.”

### Historical or Structural Memory

The 1930 Smoot-Hawley Tariff and the subsequent imperial preference blocs offer one structural memory: when a major economy uses tariffs as a macro and geopolitical tool, trading partners respond by negotiating bilateral deals and forming blocs. The legacy can outlast the political moment that created it.

The 1980s U.S.–Japan trade friction offers another. The U.S. used Section 301, voluntary export restraints, and technology controls to manage Japan’s rise in autos and semiconductors. Even after political agreements, sectoral restrictions persisted for years and reshaped global supply chains.

Neither episode predicts the future. Both remind us that trade restrictions tend to leave durable institutional and commercial scars even after the original dispute fades.

### Implications for Decision-Makers

This affects how investors and operators should think about exposure, not what they should buy or sell.

**For equity holders:** Margin dispersion is likely to rise. Companies with diversified supplier bases, flexible origin certification, and pricing power can absorb tariff stacking better than single-source operators. The question is not “tariffs or no tariffs” but “which firms can navigate a permanently higher compliance burden.”

**For supply-chain managers:** The “China-plus-one” strategy has become a baseline, not an edge. The deeper question is whether the second source is itself dependent on Chinese inputs. A Vietnam or India assembly line that imports Chinese components is still a China exposure, just with extra steps.

**For currency and rates watchers:** Bloc-specific trade deals alter terms of trade. India’s widening deficit with China pressures the rupee even as its U.S. surplus grows. The dollar’s reserve role remains intact for now, but persistent fragmentation is a long-run question for alternatives, not an established trend.

**For sovereign bond investors:** Slower trade growth is a growth risk, but tariff-related inflation keeps central banks cautious. The more important variable is real yields: nominal bond yields may fall if growth disappoints, but if inflation becomes stickier than expected, real returns can still compress. The bond market is pricing the *composition* of the slowdown, not just the level.

**For semiconductor and AI hardware investors:** The BIS case-by-case licensing regime and the 50% China/Macau shipment cap mean U.S. chipmakers with large China exposure face a structural revenue ceiling. At the same time, domestic and allied-country capacity gets a policy tailwind. The risk is not a sudden ban; it is a slow, managed squeeze that reallocates margins and market share over multiple years.

**For commodity markets:** Rare-earth and critical-mineral controls create supply wedges. Middle East tensions add a separate risk premium. Industrial metals and energy face upside risk from disruption; agricultural inputs are vulnerable if Hormuz-related shipping constraints persist.

**For policymakers and institutions:** The WTO’s forecast of 1.9% merchandise trade growth in 2026 is a signal that the multilateral system is still functioning as a recording device, but increasingly not as a governing one. Strategic sectors are being managed outside MFN rules.

**A tail risk worth naming:** If the U.S. tightens rules-of-origin enforcement or widens Section 301 investigations beyond China, the rerouting gains in Vietnam, India, Mexico, and Taiwan could reverse. The same administrative tools used to redirect trade away from China can be redirected at the hubs that absorbed it.

What this does **not** affect is the case for making bold directional bets on a single narrative. The data do not support a clean story of U.S. victory, Chinese collapse, Indian dominance, or global disintegration.

### Open Question

The July 24, 2026 expiration of the 10% Section 122 surcharge is the nearest observable decision point. If it expires and is not replaced, the effective tariff stack eases. If it is extended or converted into something more permanent, the current equilibrium hardens into something closer to a new baseline.

The deeper question is this: if the world can have record nominal trade, falling direct U.S.–China shipments, and a historic Chinese surplus all at the same time, what exactly are we measuring when we say “global trade”?

We will be watching whether the rerouting we see in the data reflects genuine diversification or simply a more elaborate version of the same dependence.
