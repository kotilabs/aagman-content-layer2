# Economic Fragmentation and Persistent Trade Frictions: The Supply Chain Is Rerouting, Not Reshoring

## Opening

Imagine a busy highway that is suddenly closed at one interchange. The traffic does not disappear. It finds the next exit, threads through side streets, and rejoins the main road a few miles later. The cars still arrive, but the route is longer, the timing less predictable, and the neighbourhoods along the way are changed by the detour. This is roughly what happened to global trade after the 2025 tariff peaks. The U.S. and China struck truces, shook hands in Geneva and Busan, and lowered some headline rates. Yet the barriers on technology, capital, and strategic goods never came down. Traffic simply found new roads.

## Context: what changed, and why it matters now

In May 2025 the U.S. and China agreed to reduce reciprocal tariffs by 115 percentage points for 90 days, leaving a 10% tariff on each side. In October/November 2025 a Trump-Xi meeting in Busan produced a suspension of some Chinese rare-earth export controls. By early 2026 the Bureau of Industry and Security had replaced presumptive denial of advanced semiconductor exports to China with a case-by-case licensing regime.

These looked like de-escalations. They were not dismantlings.

Section 301 tariffs on China stayed in force. New Section 301 investigations were launched in 2026. A 10% Section 122 global surcharge remained on the books as of July 2026, due to expire on July 24, 2026. The result is a trading system in which the *headline* rate fell from crisis peaks but the *effective* barrier remained high and opaque.

The numbers show the rerouting clearly. U.S. goods imports from China fell by $97.1 billion in January–October 2025 compared with the same period in 2024. China's share of total U.S. imports dropped to 9.3% from 13.4% in 2024, close to the 8.9% level of 2000, the year before it joined the WTO. Meanwhile U.S. imports from Vietnam rose 42.0% to $193.8 billion in 2025, imports from India reached $103.8 billion, and Mexico became the top U.S. goods trading partner with $872.8 billion in total trade.

Trade volumes have not collapsed. Global merchandise trade grew 4.6% in 2025 and hit a record nominal value of $35 trillion, according to UNCTAD. The story is not deglobalisation in the crude sense. It is fragmentation: the same goods, travelling different roads.

## Mechanism: how tariff stacking reroutes trade

The signal operates through three reinforcing channels.

**Tariff and regulatory stacking.** Chinese goods still face Section 301 duties, Section 232 sectoral tariffs on steel, aluminium, copper, and autos, the 10% Section 122 global surcharge, and fentanyl-related IEEPA duties whose legal status has shifted with court rulings. Semiconductor exports are governed by the January 2026 BIS case-by-case licensing regime, and a Section 301 semiconductor investigation carries a potential tariff increase in 2027. The effective rate depends on HS code, origin certification, and which duties stack. No shipper can know it with certainty.

**Supply-chain rerouting, not reshoring.** Lower U.S. imports from China and higher imports from Vietnam, India, Mexico, Taiwan, and Switzerland describe a substitution effect. Much of this is "China-plus-one" restructuring: final assembly moves to a third country while Chinese components, capital equipment, or intermediate goods continue to flow into those hubs. India's $99.2 billion trade deficit with China in fiscal year 2024–25, projected to widen to about $111.4 billion in 2025–26, is the cleanest illustration of the limits of decoupling.

**Investment and capital-flow realignment.** The U.S. has used trade negotiations to extract capital placement. India committed to purchase $500 billion in U.S. exports over five years. Taiwan pledged $250 billion in semiconductor, energy, and AI investment plus another $250 billion in government financing. Japan and South Korea made large commitments. These are not traditional tariff deals; they tie market access to capital placement inside the U.S.

The WTO estimates that MFN rules still cover an estimated 72% of world trade. But strategic sectors—semiconductors, critical minerals, batteries, autos, steel, aluminium—are increasingly governed by bilateral deals, investment pledges, and export controls.

## Signal / deviation: what is unusual

The unusual feature is the divergence between trade *volume* and trade *composition*. World merchandise trade grew 4.6% in 2025 and is forecast to slow only to 1.9% in 2026. Global trade value hit a record. Yet the routing is being politically determined to a degree not seen since the managed trade of the 1980s. The system is growing and fragmenting at the same time.

A second deviation is the persistence of barriers after the political crisis passed. The May and October/November 2025 truces were supposed to stabilise the relationship. Instead they froze a subset of tariffs while investigations, controls, and surcharges continued to multiply.

## Interpretation spectrum

**Managed decoupling is working.** U.S. officials and the 2026 Economic Report of the President can point to falling Chinese import share and rising investment in strategic sectors. The interpretation treats rerouting as reshoring. The risk is that it confuses a changed route with a changed dependency: the U.S. remains reliant on Asian manufacturing, just through different customs declarations.

**Fragmentation, not deglobalisation—and trade is still growing.** The WTO and UNCTAD read the data this way. Record trade value and positive volume growth support it. The weakness is that 2026 growth is forecast at only 1.9%, and much of the 2025 strength may have been front-loading ahead of tariffs.

**China is exporting its way through containment.** China's trade surplus reached $451.7 billion in January–May 2026, with May alone posting a $105.4 billion surplus and exports up 19.4% year-on-year. The interpretation notes that China is diverting goods to ASEAN, Latin America, and other markets. The risk is that a rising surplus with the rest of the world invites new barriers.

**India is the biggest structural winner.** India's advocates cite its rise to fourth-largest U.S. trading partner, the February 2026 framework that cut U.S. tariffs on Indian goods to 18%, and the $500 billion purchase commitment. The counter is that India's deficit with China is widening and its manufacturing competitiveness still lags Vietnam in several sectors.

## Historical or structural memory

The 1980s U.S.-Japan trade and technology friction is the nearest analogue. Washington used Section 301, voluntary export restraints, and technology controls to manage Japan's rise in autos and semiconductors. Sectoral restrictions persisted for years after political agreements, reshaping supply chains without restoring U.S. dominance in those industries. The lesson is not that the same outcome will repeat; it is that managed friction tends to outlast the political moment that creates it.

## Implications for decision-makers

Trade-sensitive equities, particularly industrials, consumer discretionary, and tech hardware, face rising margin dispersion. Companies with diversified supplier bases and clear origin documentation are better placed than single-source operators. Shipping and logistics face longer routes and bloc-specific compliance, which lifts tonne-mile demand but also volatility and insurance costs. Commodities are exposed to rare-earth and critical-mineral controls plus Middle East energy risk. Currencies such as USD/CNY and USD/INR are sensitive to trade balances and capital-flow commitments. Sovereign bonds may benefit from slower trade growth but face tariff-related inflation pressure.

What this does *not* imply is a simple directional trade. The system is fragmenting, not collapsing. Some firms gain from rerouting; others lose from compliance costs.

## Open question

The Section 122 10% global surcharge is scheduled to expire on July 24, 2026. If it is allowed to lapse, some of the pressure on routed trade eases. If it is extended or replaced, the detour becomes the permanent road. The deeper question is how much of Vietnam's 42% export surge to the U.S. is genuine diversification and how much is rerouting of Chinese inputs through a different customs gate. Until that is known, we are measuring the map, not the territory.
