1/
The U.S. and China shook hands in Geneva and Busan. Trade barriers did not come down—they were recast.

2/
May 2025: both sides cut reciprocal tariffs by 115 percentage points, leaving 10% in place.
October/November 2025: China suspended some rare-earth export controls.
January 2026: the U.S. moved advanced AI chip exports to China from "presumptive denial" to case-by-case review.

3/
These were pauses, not dismantlings.
Section 301 tariffs on China remain. New Section 301 investigations launched in 2026. A 10% Section 122 global surcharge is still collected as of July 2026.

4/
U.S. imports from China fell by $97.1 billion in January–October 2025. China's share of U.S. imports dropped to 9.3% from 13.4% in 2024—near its 2000 level.

5/
The traffic found new roads.
U.S. imports from Vietnam rose 42.0% to $193.8 billion in 2025. Imports from India reached $103.8 billion. Mexico became the top U.S. trading partner at $872.8 billion in total goods trade.

6/
This isn't reshoring. It's rerouting.
Much of the shift is "China-plus-one": final assembly moves to Vietnam, India, or Mexico while Chinese components and capital equipment keep flowing into those hubs.

7/
India's trade deficit with China hit $99.2 billion in FY24–25 and is projected to widen to about $111.4 billion in FY25–26. Decoupling has limits.

8/
The effective tariff on a Chinese shipment is now a stack: Section 301 + Section 232 + Section 122 + fentanyl-related duties. The exact rate depends on HS code, origin, and which layers apply. No shipper knows it with certainty.

9/
Trade volumes have not collapsed. World merchandise trade grew 4.6% in 2025. Global trade value hit a record $35 trillion. The WTO still sees 72% of world trade covered by MFN rules.

10/
This isn't deglobalisation. It's fragmentation: the same goods travelling different roads, with strategic sectors governed by bilateral deals and investment pledges rather than uniform rules.

11/
The open variable: the 10% Section 122 surcharge expires July 24, 2026. If it lapses, pressure eases. If it is extended, the detour becomes the permanent road—and Vietnam's 42% surge starts to look less like diversification and more like a customs rerouting.
