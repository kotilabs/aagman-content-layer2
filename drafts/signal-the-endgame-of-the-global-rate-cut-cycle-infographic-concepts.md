## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The dot plot moved the wrong way
- **Central insight:** The Fed did not mark down its rate outlook at the June 2026 meeting; it raised the 2026 projection by 40 bps and lifted the longer-run neutral estimate to 3.1%, implying the post-cut landing zone is far above the 2010s floor.
- **Data:** Fed funds target range 3.50–3.75% since December 2025; March 2026 median projection 3.4% → June 2026 median projection 3.8%; longer-run median 3.1%; 2026 PCE inflation projection 3.6% YoY.
- **Visualization type:** Annotated projection timeline / dot-strip chart.
- **Composition notes:** Reader sees the current target band on the left, a descending staircase of median dots from 3.8% (end-2026) to 3.6% (2027) to 3.4% (2028) and then 3.1% longer-run in the centre, and a +40 bps revision arrow with the 3.6% inflation figure on the right. A faint baseline near 0% shows the 2010s floor for contrast.
- **Sources:** Advisor Perspectives (https://www.advisorperspectives.com/articles/2026/07/13/fed-funds-rate-projections-june-2026-fomc-dot-plot); intellectia.ai (https://intellectia.ai/blog/fed-dot-plot-june-2026); FXStreet (https://www.fxstreet.com/news/fed-preview-fomc-to-keep-rates-on-hold-at-350-375-in-june-202607160054).

### Concept 2: Inflation is still above target
- **Central insight:** Headline and core inflation remain above the Fed’s 2% target, and the FOMC’s own 2026 PCE projection has risen to 3.6%, which is why the central bank is holding rates higher for longer.
- **Data:** US CPI 4.2% YoY (May 2026); US core CPI 2.9% YoY (May 2026); 2026 PCE inflation projection 3.6% YoY; 2% target line.
- **Visualization type:** Horizontal bullet / bar chart against a target line.
- **Composition notes:** A single horizontal scale from 0% to 5% with a vertical 2% target marker. Three bars extend rightward: core CPI at 2.9%, PCE projection at 3.6%, and headline CPI at 4.2%. Each bar is labelled with its value and source. The payoff is visual: none of the bars reach the target line.
- **Sources:** Cambridge Currencies (https://www.cambridgecurrencies.com/en/news/us-inflation-data-may-2026); intellectia.ai (https://intellectia.ai/blog/fed-dot-plot-june-2026).

### Concept 3: The end of synchronized easing
- **Central insight:** The three major advanced-economy central banks are no longer moving in the same direction: the Fed is on hold, the ECB hiked for the first time since September 2023, and the Bank of Japan reached 1.00%.
- **Data:** Fed funds target range 3.50–3.75%, on hold since December 2025; ECB deposit facility rate was 2.0% in June 2025, raised in June 2026 (first hike since September 2023)—the research artifact establishes a single June 2026 hike but does not provide the new deposit facility rate level; BoJ policy rate 1.00% as of 16 June 2026.
- **Visualization type:** Three-column central-bank dashboard with directional arrows.
- **Composition notes:** Three vertical panels for the Fed, ECB, and BoJ. Each panel shows the key rate or stance, the date of the latest decision, and an arrow indicating direction: horizontal for hold, upward for hike. The headline across the top reads that the post-inflation easing cycle is over. Small annotations give the policy context (first ECB hike since 2023, BoJ above zero).
- **Sources:** FXStreet / Advisor Perspectives (Fed); Statista (https://www.statista.com/statistics/421102/ecb-interest-rate-on-deposit-facility/); Finance Calendar (https://financecalendar.com/event/bank-of-japan-interest-rate-decision-june-2026/).

### Concept 4: Debt stock meets higher rates
- **Central insight:** With gross global debt projected to reach 100% of GDP by 2029 and government interest payments near 3% of global GDP, the end of rate cuts turns a monetary stance question into a fiscal sustainability constraint.
- **Data:** Gross global debt projected 100% of GDP by 2029; global government interest payments near 3% of global GDP; Fed funds target range 3.50–3.75% as the refinancing benchmark.
- **Visualization type:** Split-panel comparison (debt stock + interest burden).
- **Composition notes:** Left half shows a single large figure: 100% of GDP by 2029, with a rising debt-to-GDP trajectory implied by the arrow. Right half shows a horizontal bar labelled "global government interest payments" at roughly 3% of global GDP. A thin line connects the two panels to the current Fed funds rate band, signalling the channel through which higher rates feed debt service.
- **Sources:** Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget summarising IMF Fiscal Monitor May 2026 (https://www.crfb.org/blogs/debt-around-world); FXStreet / Advisor Perspectives (Fed rate).

### Concept 5: The constrained policy space
- **Central insight:** Central banks are caught between sticky inflation, below-trend growth, and an elevated global debt stock, leaving them limited room to ease or tighten aggressively.
- **Data:** US CPI 4.2% YoY (US country-specific measure); IMF global headline inflation 4.7% in 2026 (global measure, not comparable to US CPI); IMF global growth 3.0% in 2026; gross global debt projected 100% of GDP by 2029; Fed funds target 3.50–3.75%.
- **Visualization type:** Triangular tension diagram or three-gauge dashboard.
- **Composition notes:** Centre shows the Fed funds target band as the fulcrum. Three surrounding blocks pull in different directions: inflation block shows 4.2% US CPI and 4.7% global headline inflation as separate, non-comparable readings that both argue against cuts; growth (3.0% global) argues against hikes; debt (100% of GDP by 2029) argues against holding rates high indefinitely. Arrows show the conflicting pressures. The payoff is that the next policy move is structurally unclear.
- **Sources:** Cambridge Currencies (https://www.cambridgecurrencies.com/en/news/us-inflation-data-may-2026); Xinhua / Anadolu / IDN Financials reporting IMF July 2026 WEO; CRFB / IMF Fiscal Monitor May 2026 (https://www.crfb.org/blogs/debt-around-world); FXStreet / Advisor Perspectives (Fed rate).

## What to avoid

- Predicting specific Treasury yields, credit spreads, or asset prices: the artifact does not contain those figures.
- Treating the ECB’s June 2026 hike as a confirmed tightening cycle: the research only establishes a single hike after a long pause; the new rate level is not provided.
- Conflating gross global debt at 100% of GDP with an imminent crisis: the number is structurally important, but the artifact notes that major economies still borrow in their own currencies and have deep domestic investor bases.
- Using the BoJ’s 1.00% rate to draw conclusions about yen carry trades or JPY flows without supporting data.
- Framing the Fed dot plot as a commitment: it is a median projection that can shift between meetings, as the March-to-June revision demonstrates.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with Concepts 1, 3, and 4 as the strongest visual cases; add Concept 2 if a tighter inflation-focused graphic is useful, and Concept 5 only if the broader macro-tension framing is needed. All five are supported by verifiable numbers from the research artifact.
