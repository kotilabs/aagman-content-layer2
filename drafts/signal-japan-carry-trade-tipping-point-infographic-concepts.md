## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The carry differential is closing from both ends
- **Central insight:** The yen carry trade’s profit margin is shrinking because the BoJ is hiking while the Fed is on hold — the Fed–BoJ spread is projected to compress from **+2.62% today to +1.38% in 12 months**.
- **Data:**
  - BoJ policy rate **1.0%** as of 16 June 2026.
  - Fed effective funds rate **3.63%** as of 21 July 2026.
  - Current Fed–BoJ spread **+2.62%**; projected 12-month spread **+1.38%**.
- **Visualization type:** Slope / connected-dot chart or dual-axis rate-and-spread timeline.
- **Composition notes:** The reader sees two converging lines first — the BoJ line rising from near zero and the effective Fed line flat-to-falling. A bold band between them narrows visibly toward the right, ending at the projected **1.38%** gap. The payoff: carry math is weakening even without a market crisis.
- **Caveat / competing channel:** Energy prices and geopolitics can override or delay the rate-differential story. Japan imports nearly all its energy, and Middle East conflict-driven oil prices have widened the trade deficit — a channel that works independently of Fed–BoJ spread compression.
- **Sources:**
  - Bank of Japan, 16 June 2026 — https://www.boj.or.jp/en/mopo/mpmdeci/mpr_2026/k260616a.pdf
  - FRED, Federal Funds Effective Rate (DFF) — https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFF
  - CentralBank.watch, 23 July 2026 — https://centralbank.watch/compare/currency-pair-deep-dives/usd-jpy/

### Concept 2: The feedback loop — why the trigger is positioning, not just rates
- **Central insight:** With leveraged funds already **net short 90,461 yen futures contracts**, any yen appreciation forces some shorts to cover by buying yen — and that buying pushes the yen higher, hitting more positions in a self-reinforcing loop.
- **Data:**
  - CFTC leveraged-fund net short **90,461** JPY futures contracts as of 14 July 2026.
  - USD/JPY near **163.15** on 22 July 2026, a 40-year low for the yen.
  - MOF spent **¥11.7349 trillion** on yen-buying intervention from 28 April to 27 May 2026, yet USD/JPY kept rising.
- **Visualization type:** Circular flow diagram / loop chart with a positioning gauge.
- **Composition notes:** Start with the crowded-short gauge at the top (**90,461 net shorts**). Arrow 1: yen appreciates. Arrow 2: yen-funded investors face mark-to-market losses. Arrow 3: forced buying of yen to close positions. Arrow 4: more yen demand → further appreciation. An intervention box sits off to the side with a small label: “¥11.7tn did not reverse the trend.” The payoff: the tipping point is a positioning level, not a magic interest-rate number.
- **Sources:**
  - CFTC Commitments of Traders, positions as of 14 July 2026 — https://www.cftc.gov/dea/futures/financial_lf.htm
  - Caixin, 22 July 2026 — https://international.caixin.com/2026-07-22/102466972.html
  - NS Journal, 22 July 2026 — https://www.nsjournal.jp/%E5%A4%96%E5%9B%BD%E7%82%BA%E6%9B%BF%E5%B8%82%E6%B3%81%E3%83%BB7%E6%9C%8822%E6%97%A5%E3%80%80%E3%83%89%E3%83%AB%E5%86%86%E3%83%AC%E3%83%B3%E3%82%B8163-22-162-67%E3%80%80%E4%B8%AD%E5%BF%83%E7%9B%B8%E5%A0%B4/
  - Ministry of Finance Japan, 29 May 2026 — https://www.mof.go.jp/english/policy/international_policy/reference/feio/monthly/20260529e.html

### Concept 3: The iceberg — how big the carry trade really is
- **Central insight:** The speculative yen carry trade is already large, but the structural yen-funding market behind it is enormous: official intervention, the estimated carry stock, and yen-side FX derivatives sit at vastly different scales.
- **Data:**
  - MOF intervention: **¥11.7349 trillion** (28 April – 27 May 2026).
  - BIS estimated carry-trade stock: roughly **¥40 trillion (~$250 billion)** going into August 2024.
  - Yen-side FX swaps / forwards / currency swaps: **$14.2 trillion (¥1,994 trillion)** at end-2023.
- **Visualization type:** Log-scale bar chart or nested proportional-area chart (blocks).
- **Composition notes:** Three blocks side by side, each labelled with its value: smallest = MOF intervention (**¥11.7tn**); middle = estimated August 2024 carry stock (**¥40tn / ~$250bn**); largest = yen-side FX derivatives (**$14.2tn / ¥1,994tn**). Area makes the orders-of-magnitude gap immediate. Annotation: “Intervention is a drop in the derivatives ocean.” Payoff: even a small deleveraging of the large underlying stock can move global markets.
- **Sources:**
  - Ministry of Finance Japan, 29 May 2026 — https://www.mof.go.jp/english/policy/international_policy/reference/feio/monthly/20260529e.html
  - BIS Bulletin No. 90, 27 August 2024 — https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull90.htm
  - BIS Quarterly Review, September 2024 — https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2409y.htm

### Concept 4: August 2024 — a dress rehearsal
- **Central insight:** The last time the BoJ took a small hawkish step, the unwind was fast and global: in a single day the Nikkei plunged **12.4%**, VIX spiked above **65**, Bitcoin dropped **24%**, and the S&P 500 fell **3%**.
- **Data:**
  - On 5 August 2024: Nikkei 225 **−12.4%**; VIX intraday peak **>65**; Bitcoin **−24%**; S&P 500 **−3%**.
  - Trigger context: a small BoJ rate hike in July 2024 and the subsequent carry-trade unwind (BIS Bulletin No. 90).
- **Visualization type:** Grouped bar / impact panel or four dashboard tiles.
- **Composition notes:** Four tiles arranged in a grid, each with the asset icon, the drop, and a before/after marker. A headline banner reads: “One BoJ move. One day. Global deleveraging.” The payoff: this is not a theoretical risk — it already happened.
- **Sources:**
  - l0g.fr, 13 July 2026 — https://l0g.fr/en/analysis/the-yen-carry-trade/
  - BIS Bulletin No. 90, 27 August 2024 — https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull90.htm

### Concept 5: The BoJ’s domestic tightrope
- **Central insight:** The BoJ has cover to keep hiking — wages rose **5.26%** in the 2026 Shuntō and the 10-year JGB yield is above **2.75%** — but Japan’s debt is **248.7%** of GDP and real wages fell **1.3%** in 2025, limiting how far it can go.
- **Data:**
  - 2026 Shuntō wage increase: **+5.26%**.
  - Real wage growth in 2025: **−1.3%** y/y.
  - 10-year JGB auction yield: **2.755%** on 2 July 2026.
  - General government gross debt: **248.7%** of GDP in 2025.
  - BoJ CPI projection for fiscal 2026: **2.5–3.0%**.
- **Visualization type:** Balance-scale / seesaw diagram.
- **Composition notes:** Left pan carries the hawkish arguments: wage growth (**+5.26%**), JGB yield (**2.755%**), and the BoJ’s fiscal 2026 CPI projection (**2.5–3.0%**). Right pan carries the constraints: debt (**248.7%** of GDP) and real wages (**−1.3%**). The fulcrum is the **1.0%** policy rate. The payoff: normalization is possible, but bounded.
- **Sources:**
  - ISVD, 5 May 2026 — https://isvd.or.jp/en/columns/2026-05-01-wage-hike-real-wage-paradox-2026
  - Ministry of Finance Japan, 2 July 2026 — https://www.mof.go.jp/english/policy/jgbs/auction/calendar/eresul/eresul20260702.htm
  - Trading Economics / Ministry of Finance — https://tradingeconomics.com/japan/government-debt-to-gdp
  - Bank of Japan, April 2026 Outlook — https://www.boj.or.jp/en/mopo/outlook/highlight/ten202604.htm

## What to avoid

- **USD/JPY at 163 as a standalone chart.** The level is striking, but without the spread, positioning, and feedback loop it becomes a price-meme, not an insight.
- **Debt-to-GDP alone at 248.7%.** The number is dramatic, but it needs the rate / yield context to explain why it constrains the BoJ; otherwise it reads like a generic Japan-is-in-debt headline.
- **The Nikkei record high (62,009.59) as a bullish signal.** It is a weak carry-trade datapoint because the record reflects tech/export momentum, not the unwind risk.
- **Headline CPI 1.5% by itself.** Core inflation is below target and not visually surprising; it only works as part of the domestic-tightrope story.
- **Any claim about the *current* outstanding carry-trade size.** The research flags this as unknown; use only the dated BIS estimate (¥40tn / ~$250bn going into August 2024) and label it as such.
- **Intervention as proof that authorities will win.** The MOF spent ¥11.7tn and the yen kept weakening — so the visual story must be “intervention failed to reverse the trend,” not “authorities are in control.”

## Final recommendation

Proceed with **Concepts 1–4** as the strongest visual case: they move from the macro differential (1), to the positioning feedback loop (2), to the scale of the risk (3), to the historical proof-of-concept (4). Use **Concept 5** only if the domestic-policy angle is the lead story or as a second, separate infographic; it is interesting but slightly less directly tied to the carry-trade tipping point.
