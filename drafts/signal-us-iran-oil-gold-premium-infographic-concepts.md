## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The 60-Day Peace Trade
- **Central insight:** The market repriced oil and gold as if a 60-day interim MOU were a final peace treaty.
- **Data to include:**
  - 18 Jun 2026: 14-point US–Iran MOU signed; 60-day negotiation window begins.
  - Brent: $72.95/bbl on 26 Jun, down ~20% over the month (Trading Economics).
  - Gold: $4,087.01/oz on 26 Jun, down ~8% over the month and ~26% below the Jan 2026 all-time high of $5,608.35/oz (Trading Economics).
  - 30 days: US blockade-removal deadline under the MOU (Yonhap).
- **Visualization type:** Timeline with dual price rails.
- **Composition notes:** Reader sees a horizontal timeline anchored on 18 Jun (MOU signing). Above the line, a falling Brent curve; below, a falling gold curve. Callouts flag the 60-day window and the 30-day blockade clause. The payoff is the gap between what was signed (interim) and what was priced (final).
- **Sources:** Trading Economics (Brent, Gold); Yonhap (MOU text).

### Concept 2: Hormuz — The Arithmetic of a Chokepoint
- **Central insight:** Gulf bypass pipelines can replace only roughly one-third to two-fifths of Hormuz capacity, which is why reopening news moved prices so fast.
- **Data to include:**
  - Normal Hormuz flow: 17–21 mb/d of crude and condensate (The Middle East Insider / EIA).
  - Conflict-peak flow: ~2.1 mb/d (The Middle East Insider, Apr 2026).
  - Post-MOU 24-hour flow: >20 million barrels (Angel One / US Energy Secretary Chris Wright, 26 Jun 2026).
  - Combined Gulf bypass capacity: 6.5–7 mb/d (The Middle East Insider).
  - Full-closure shortfall: ~12–13 mb/d versus normal flows (The Middle East Insider).
- **Visualization type:** Geographic chokepoint diagram paired with a capacity bar comparison.
- **Composition notes:** A narrow strait icon flanked by three flow bars: pre-war baseline, April collapse, and post-MOU recovery. Below, a second set shows bypass capacity dwarfed by normal Hormuz flow. Reader first grasps the bottleneck, then the replacement gap, then why the reopening narrative dominates pricing.
- **Sources:** The Middle East Insider; Angel One / US Energy Secretary Chris Wright.

### Concept 3: Oil's Fastest Correction vs. a Bullish Signal Nobody Noticed
- **Central insight:** Brent fell ~20% in a month even as US crude inventories hit their lowest level since 1984.
- **Data to include:**
  - Brent: $72.95/bbl on 26 Jun, down ~20% month-on-month (Trading Economics).
  - US crude oil inventories: lowest since 1984 in the week reported around 26 Jun 2026 (Angel One / EIA).
  - WTI: $70.07/bbl on 26 Jun, down 0.38% on the day (Angel One).
- **Visualization type:** Divergence chart.
- **Composition notes:** Two vertical scales — Brent price falling on the left, inventory level falling on the right. A shaded band notes that falling inventories are usually read as a bullish crude signal. The reader's eye follows the divergence, ending on the caption: "Positioning ignored a 38-year inventory low." The payoff is the power of narrative over a fundamental signal.
- **Sources:** Trading Economics; Angel One / EIA.

### Concept 4: India's Oil Math
- **Central insight:** India's inflation and rates outlook is now partly a bet on a 60-day diplomatic window.
- **Data to include:**
  - India imports ~85% of its crude oil consumption (Axis Direct).
  - Every $10/bbl rise in crude = ~0.35–0.5% of GDP in current account deficit (Axis Direct).
  - Every $10/bbl rise in crude = ~$18 billion added to the import bill (Kotak IE via HDFC AMC).
  - RBI repo rate: 5.25% on hold (The Hindu BusinessLine, 5 Jun 2026).
  - RBI FY27 CPI inflation forecast: 5.1% (The Hindu BusinessLine).
  - India's 10-year G-Sec yield: 6.84% on 23 Jun 2026 (Univest).
- **Visualization type:** Flow chart / impact cascade.
- **Composition notes:** Reader starts at "85% import dependence," follows arrows to CAD and import-bill sensitivities, then to RBI forecasts and the bond yield. A ghosted $90/bbl price box on the side is labeled "illustrative stress-test: what if the MOU frays?" The payoff is the transmission from a Gulf strait to domestic rates.
- **Sources:** Axis Direct; HDFC AMC (Kotak IE estimate); The Hindu BusinessLine; Univest.

### Concept 5: Gold's Two Sell-Offs
- **Central insight:** Gold fell on both peace (war premium removal) and the Fed (higher real-rate expectations), making the correction faster than either force alone.
- **Data to include:**
  - Gold all-time high: $5,608.35/oz in Jan 2026 (Trading Economics).
  - Gold on 26 Jun 2026: $4,087.01/oz, down ~26% from the high (Trading Economics).
  - Gold 1-month change: ~−8% (26 May – 26 Jun) (Trading Economics).
  - Gold on 12 Jun 2026: just below $4,200/oz, with TD Securities strategist Ryan McKay noting Fed-hike probabilities limited upside (Yahoo Finance / Bloomberg).
  - Gold on 29 Jun 2026: $4,016.62/oz, down 10.45% month-on-month (Trading Economics).
- **Visualization type:** Dual-axis timeline / layered area chart.
- **Composition notes:** A single falling gold curve from January peak to late June. Two labeled bands pull it downward: "Hormuz peace premium removed" (geopolitical) and "Hawkish Fed repricing" (rates). Reader sees the magnitude of the peak-to-trough move first, then the two drivers. The payoff is that gold is caught in a squeeze from both directions.
- **Sources:** Trading Economics; Yahoo Finance / Bloomberg.

## What to avoid

- **Avoid treating the MOU as a treaty.** The 60-day window and "no charge for 60 days only" language must be visible; otherwise the infographic implies permanence.
- **Avoid showing the $300 billion reconstruction plan without context.** It is a commitment to work toward a plan, not money already deployed.
- **Avoid implying the 20% oil drop is purely supply-driven.** No new barrels came online; this is a positioning and narrative repricing.
- **Avoid using the India CAD/import-bill sensitivities as precise government forecasts.** They are broker estimates; label them as such.
- **Avoid a single "everything is fine now" framing.** The data supports asymmetry (premium could rebuild quickly), not resolution.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with **Concepts 1, 2, 4, and 5**. Concept 3 is interesting but depends on a single directional inventory claim without an exact barrel figure; include it only if a hard inventory number can be added. The strongest visual case is the gap between interim diplomacy and market pricing (Concept 1) and the Hormuz capacity arithmetic (Concept 2).
