## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The Two-Speed Economy
- **Central insight:** In May 2026, China's domestic demand indicators contracted while its export engine hit a record, revealing an economy split in two.
- **Data to include:**
  - Retail sales: -0.6% y/y (May 2026), first y/y contraction in the current release; Jan–May +1.4% y/y
  - Fixed-asset investment: -4.1% y/y (Jan–May 2026), deepening from -1.6% (Jan–Apr)
  - Property investment: -16.2% y/y (Jan–May 2026), accelerating from -13.7% (Jan–Apr)
  - Exports: +19.4% y/y to a record USD 376.78 billion (May 2026), up from +14.1% in April
  - Industrial production: +4.5% y/y (May 2026), up from +4.1% in April
  - CPI: +1.2% y/y; core CPI: +1.1% y/y (May 2026) — consumer demand is soft
  - PPI: 103.8 index points (2025=100), up from 103.2 in April — upstream cost pressure, not consumer demand
  - Urban surveyed unemployment: 5.1% in May 2026, down from 5.2% in April — a stable labour-market counterpoint
- **Visualization type:** Divergence / split comparison (two columns or diverging arrows), with an inset price split
- **Rough composition notes:** Reader sees a bold headline "Two Economies, One Country" with a central dividing line. Left side: downward arrows in red/orange for retail sales, FAI, and property investment. Right side: upward arrows in blue/green for exports and industrial production. The largest figure — USD 376.78 billion record exports — anchors the right; the first retail-sales contraction (-0.6%) anchors the left. A small inset panel shows CPI (+1.2%) and core CPI (+1.1%) running well below PPI (103.8), making the demand split concrete on the price level. A discreet footnote notes the unemployment rate ticked down to 5.1%, flagging it as a counter-indicator that the labour market is still stable. Eye moves across the divide, then down to a footnote explaining that exports are partly front-loading and not broad enough to offset domestic weakness.
- **Sources:** NBS retail sales; NBS via Trading Economics for FAI, property investment, IP, CPI, PPI; General Administration of Customs via Trading Economics for exports; Xinhua/gov.cn for unemployment

### Concept 2: 35 Months of Falling House Prices
- **Central insight:** China's national housing market has been in annual decline for 35 consecutive months, with stabilization visible only in a handful of first-tier cities.
- **Data to include:**
  - New home prices across 70 cities: -3.5% y/y, -0.2% m/m (May 2026); 35th consecutive y/y decline
  - First-tier cities: +0.2% m/m
  - Second-tier cities: -0.1% m/m
  - Third-tier cities: -0.4% m/m
  - 16 of 70 cities reported m/m increases
  - Property investment: -16.2% y/y (Jan–May 2026)
- **Visualization type:** Timeline with tiered geographic comparison
- **Rough composition notes:** A horizontal timeline dominates the top, showing 35 monthly bars below the zero line, with the May 2026 -3.5% bar highlighted. Below the timeline, four small city-tier boxes show first-tier (+0.2%) in a different color from the falling second- and third-tier cities. A small callout notes that only 16 of 70 cities saw any monthly increase. The eye travels from the long timeline to the tier breakdown, ending on the property-investment figure that shows the sector is still dragging the wider economy.
- **Sources:** NBS via Trading Economics for 70-city prices; Anbound/Xinhua for tier breakdown; NBS via Trading Economics for property investment

### Concept 3: When Credit Stops Circulating
- **Central insight:** China's households are paying down debt at a record pace and real-economy loan growth is at a two-decade low, even as the central bank promises easier policy.
- **Data to include:**
  - Outstanding RMB loans to the real economy: +5.6% y/y (April 2026), slowest in roughly two decades
  - Household debt contracted by CNY 786.9 billion in April 2026, largest monthly drop on record
  - Total social financing stock: +7.8% y/y (end-April 2026)
  - New TSF flow: CNY 2.03 trillion (May 2026), up from CNY 620 billion in April
  - PBOC pledged "appropriately accommodative" monetary policy and flexible RRR/interest-rate cuts
- **Visualization type:** Ratio / flow chart (stock vs flow, supply vs demand)
- **Rough composition notes:** Two connected panels. Left panel shows a narrowing pipe: liquidity supply from the PBOC (TSF stock +7.8%, new TSF flow rebounding) flowing into a much smaller pipe labeled real-economy loan growth (+5.6%, two-decade low). Right panel shows household debt plunging by CNY 786.9 billion. The headline reads "Liquidity In, Credit Demand Out." The payoff is the visual gap between what the central bank is supplying and what households and businesses are willing to borrow.
- **Sources:** PBOC via CGTN and DBS for loan growth; China Banking News for household debt contraction; PBOC via Trading Economics for TSF flow; PBOC for policy pledge

### Concept 4: Slower Growth by Design
- **Central insight:** Beijing lowered its 2026 growth target before the May deterioration and paired it with a modest, targeted fiscal package rather than a broad stimulus.
- **Data to include:**
  - 2026 GDP target: 4.5%–5.0%, down from roughly 5% in 2025
  - Q1 2026 real GDP: +5.0% y/y
  - 2026 budget deficit target: ~4% of GDP (CNY 5.89 trillion)
  - Planned bond issuance and deficit financing: CNY 1.3 trillion ultra-long special treasury bonds + CNY 300 billion bank recapitalisation bonds + CNY 4.4 trillion local government special-purpose bonds + CNY 5.89 trillion budget deficit
  - May retail sales: -0.6% y/y
- **Visualization type:** Comparison / target-versus-outcome
- **Rough composition notes:** A horizontal band shows the 2026 target range (4.5%–5.0%) with Q1's +5.0% sitting at the upper edge and May's retail contraction as a warning that Q2 may fall short. Below, four stacked bars break down the planned government debt and deficit financing: CNY 1.3trn ultra-long special treasury bonds, CNY 0.3trn bank recapitalisation bonds, CNY 4.4trn local government special-purpose bonds, and CNY 5.89trn budget deficit. The four bars total roughly CNY 11.89 trillion. The composition contrasts the lowered ambition (top) with the scale but targeted nature of the planned response (bottom). The reader sees that Beijing prepared for slower growth rather than being surprised by it.
- **Sources:** NPC / MarketWatch / New Straits Times for GDP target; NBS for Q1 GDP; China Daily / TheoryChina for fiscal targets; NBS for retail sales

### Concept 5: China's Slowdown Spills Outward
- **Central insight:** Weak Chinese domestic demand is already dragging China-sensitive commodities and contributing to the weakest global trade outlook in years.
- **Data to include:**
  - Iron ore: USD 100.33/metric ton on 26 June 2026, down 8.18% over the past month
  - China's 2025 merchandise trade surplus: record ~USD 1.2 trillion
  - WTO 2026 global merchandise trade volume growth forecast: 0.5%, cut from 1.8% (August 2025 forecast)
  - May retail sales: -0.6% y/y
  - Fixed-asset investment: -4.1% y/y (Jan–May 2026)
- **Visualization type:** Flow chart / cascade
- **Rough composition notes:** Reader starts at the top with a map pin on China and two weakening domestic-demand figures (retail sales -0.6%, FAI -4.1%). Arrows flow outward to two channels: a commodity channel showing iron ore down 8.18% to USD 100.33/MT, and a trade channel showing the WTO global trade forecast cut from 1.8% to 0.5%. A smaller counter-flow notes China's record trade surplus (~USD 1.2 trillion), explaining why the spillover is showing up in lower import demand and commodity prices rather than a weaker currency. The payoff is that this is not just a China story.
- **Sources:** Trading Economics for iron ore; HKMA for 2025 trade surplus; WTO for global trade forecast; NBS for retail sales and FAI

## What to avoid

- **Shanghai Composite and USD/CNY as headline visuals:** Both are available (4,027 and 6.80 respectively), but they do not yet tell a clean, divergent story. The yuan has been stable despite weak data, and equities are reacting to multiple factors. Dropped because they add noise rather than insight.
- "**China stimulus is coming**" framing: The fiscal package is already known and sized. The more interesting point is that it is modest relative to the weakness, not that it is large. Avoid implying a pending bazooka.
- **First-tier city stabilization without context:** First-tier home prices rose 0.2% m/m, but this is a narrow slice. Any concept using it must immediately contrast with national declines and third-tier weakness; otherwise it misleads.
- **Front-loading as a certainty:** Export strength is partly attributed to front-loading ahead of tariff and energy-price uncertainty. Concepts should present this as a known risk, not assume the surge will reverse.
- **Japan balance-sheet comparison as a central visual:** The Japan analogue is useful for perspective, but the research artifact treats it as structural memory, not a verified fact. It is better left to accompanying text than to an infographic anchored on data.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with **Concepts 1, 2, 3, and 5**. These four cover the core signal (two-speed divergence), the domestic mechanism (property and credit), and the global transmission (commodities and trade). Concept 4 is also viable but slightly more policy-focused and less visually distinctive; include it if the publication schedule has room for a fifth piece, otherwise lead with Concept 1 as the flagship.
