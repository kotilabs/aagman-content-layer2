## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The Divergence — Two Markets, One Economy
- **Central insight:** The rupee and the Nifty are sending opposite signals about the same macro environment.
- **Data:** USD/INR 95.4346 (FBIL, 6 Jul 2026); Nifty 50 24,207.45 (NSE, 13 Jul 2026); India VIX 12.25 (NSE, 10 Jul 2026).
- **Visualization type:** Divergence / dual-axis chart.
- **Composition notes:** Left half tracks USD/INR from Sep 2024 to Jul 2026 with a clear upward trend. Right half tracks Nifty 50 over the same period, range-bound. A central column labels the tension: "Currency warns / Equities absorb." Eye follows the widening gap, then lands on the question in the headline.
- **Sources:** FBIL, NSE India, Univest.

### Concept 2: Flow Composition — Who Is Buying and Selling
- **Central insight:** Foreign equity selling has been almost fully offset by domestic institutional buying.
- **Data:** FPI equity outflows Mar–Jun 2026 ~USD 27.7 billion; DII net buying Jan–early Jun 2026 ~₹4.16 lakh crore; SIP Jun 2026 ₹31,781 crore.
- **Visualization type:** Sankey / flow diagram.
- **Composition notes:** Two vertical bars at the top — FPIs (red outflow) and DIIs (green inflow). Arrows flow into an "Indian equities" pool, with net absorption shown as the residual. A smaller side flow shows debt inflows exceeding equity outflows by USD 500 million in June. Payoff: the market is not bleeding capital; it is rotating it.
- **Sources:** Finnovate / NSDL, NiftyTrader / NSE-BSE, AMFI.

### Concept 3: 2013 vs 2026 — Why the Structure Differs
- **Central insight:** India’s external vulnerability today is far lower than during the Taper Tantrum.
- **Data:** 2013 CAD ~4.7% of GDP vs Q1 FY26 ~0.2%; 2013 inflation ~10% vs Jun 2026 CPI 4.38%; FX reserves now above USD 700 billion.
- **Visualization type:** Side-by-side comparison table with small bar charts.
- **Composition notes:** Two columns, 2013 and 2026. Bars for CAD, inflation, reserves. 2013 column is red-tinged; 2026 is amber. A one-line caption explains: "Exposed, but not fragile in the same way." Payoff: history lowers urgency.
- **Sources:** PRS / RBI, Goodreturns / MoSPI, PeakMocks / RBI.

### Concept 4: The Daily Mechanics — 1 July 2026 in Miniature
- **Central insight:** A single session can illustrate the structural absorption story.
- **Data:** FPI sell ₹1,140 crore; DII buy ₹3,159 crore; ratio ~2.8x.
- **Visualization type:** Simple two-bar comparison with ratio callout.
- **Composition notes:** Two vertical bars, DII bar nearly three times the FPI bar. A large "~3x" sits between them. Body text: "Domestic absorption, one session at a time." Clean, high-contrast, shareable as a single screenshot.
- **Sources:** RupeeRadar / NSE.

### Concept 5: The RBI’s Buffer — Reserve Drawdown
- **Central insight:** The RBI is actively managing the currency, but reserves have fallen from their peak.
- **Data:** FX reserves USD 728.494 billion (week ended 27 Feb 2026); USD 703.31 billion (week ended 17 Apr 2026).
- **Visualization type:** Area chart with peak-to-trough annotation.
- **Composition notes:** Line/area chart of weekly FX reserves, peak marked, latest level marked, difference of ~USD 25 billion highlighted. Caption: "Reserves are large, but no longer accumulating." Payoff: the central bank has ammunition, and it is using it.
- **Sources:** PeakMocks / RBI, multibagg.ai / RBI.

## What to avoid

- **A simple "rupee falling" chart** — already widely covered; does not capture the divergence that makes this signal interesting.
- **A Nifty-alone chart** — misses the cross-asset tension entirely.
- **A generic FPI vs DII time series without the ratio** — less structurally informative than the absorption comparison.
- **Any visualization implying causation** — the relationship between rupee weakness and equity stability is debated; keep it as divergence, not cause-effect.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with Concepts 1, 2, and 3. Concept 1 captures the headline tension, Concept 2 explains the mechanism, and Concept 3 provides the historical context. Concepts 4 and 5 are strong optional additions if the design budget allows; Concept 4 is highly shareable, and Concept 5 adds the policy dimension.
