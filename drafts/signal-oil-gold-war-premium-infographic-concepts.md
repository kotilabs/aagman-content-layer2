## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The war-premium collapse
- **Central insight:** The US-Iran interim deal repriced oil and gold faster than physical supply recovered, revealing how much of the 2026 commodity rally was geopolitical rent rather than scarcity.
- **Data:** Brent ~$120–$126/bbl (April 2026) → ~$78/bbl (18 June) → ~$72/bbl (6 July); Gold ~$5,589/oz (early 2026 peak) → ~$4,330/oz (18 June). Dates: 17 June 2026 interim deal signing.
- **Visualization type:** Dual-axis timeline / price waterfall.
- **Composition notes:** Lead with two descending lines on a shared timeline (Feb–Jul 2026). Mark the 17 June deal with a vertical anchor. Left axis = Brent ($/bbl); right axis = Gold ($/oz). Use different line weights or colors. The eye follows the steep drops; the payoff is the gap between the April peak and the July price, annotated with percentage change ranges.
- **Sources:** MUFG Middle East Daily (18 June 2026); Frontier Asset Management (8 June 2026); Polyestertime (6 July 2026)

### Concept 2: The Hormuz chokepoint
- **Central insight:** Roughly 20% of global seaborne oil and LNG normally moves through one narrow strait, which is why its closure—and reopening—moved prices worldwide.
- **Data:** ~20 mb/d of crude oil and petroleum liquids pre-conflict; India receives 40–50% of its crude imports and 85–90% of total consumption through this corridor.
- **Visualization type:** Geographic flow diagram / sankey-style map.
- **Composition notes:** Center the Strait of Hormuz on a simplified Middle East/South Asia map. Arrows in = flows from Gulf producers; arrows out = a verified share to India (40–50% of crude imports), with remaining arrows labeled "other importers" / "rest of world." Use width to encode the aggregate ~20 mb/d volume, not unverified country splits. Annotate the 20 mb/d figure prominently. A secondary callout box shows India's exposure. Payoff: the reader sees why a local diplomatic event became a global macro shock.
- **Sources:** US EIA / IEA; Indian Defence Institute (4 April 2026)

### Concept 3: Demand destruction vs. supply shock
- **Central insight:** Oil demand is contracting (-1.1 mb/d in 2026), supply is falling faster (-3.9 mb/d), and inventories are still drawing at record pace—so the price collapse is about repriced risk, not healed physical markets.
- **Data:** Demand growth -1.1 mb/d y-o-y; supply -3.9 mb/d y-o-y to 102.4 mb/d; observed stocks -143 mb in May 2026, -3.8 mb/d since February; OECD inventories lowest since 2003 by end-2026.
- **Visualization type:** Divergence bar chart + inventory draw-down.
- **Composition notes:** Three panels or a stacked layout. Top: paired horizontal bars for demand change (-1.1 mb/d) and supply change (-3.9 mb/d). Middle: a falling inventory cylinder/bar showing the 143 mb May draw. Bottom: OECD inventory level vs. the 2003 low watermark. Color-code supply in red, demand in amber, inventories in grey. Payoff: the contradiction that prices fell while physical buffers shrank.
- **Sources:** IEA Oil Market Report, June 2026; US EIA STEO, June 2026

### Concept 4: Policy divergence and the importer inflation shock
- **Central insight:** The same war-driven energy shock pushed the ECB to hike, the Fed to lean hawkish, and India's inflation forecast up sharply—showing how a Gulf chokepoint reverberates into central-bank decisions.
- **Data:** ECB deposit rate 2.25% (+25 bps, 11 June 2026); Fed funds rate 3.50%–3.75% (held, 17 June), 9 of 18 FOMC dots project a 2026 hike; India CPI FY27 forecast 5.1% (up from 4.6%), with March/April actuals at 3.4%/3.5%.
- **Visualization type:** Small-multiples comparison / policy dashboard.
- **Composition notes:** Three vertical cards side by side: ECB (hike), Fed (hawkish hold), RBI/India (inflation escalation). Each card shows the key number, the date, and one-line context. A thin connecting line or shared header reads: "Same shock, different policy responses." Add a small footnote on the Fed card: Warsh did not submit a projection, so the 9-of-18 hike split is among the remaining participants. Payoff: the reader sees that lower oil prices are not yet translating into easier policy because inflation expectations have already shifted.
- **Sources:** Xinhua / ECB (11 June 2026); Fox Business / FOMC (17 June 2026); RBI / Chartforest (5 June 2026)

## What to avoid

- **"Low inventories mean prices must rise."** The data shows the opposite: OECD inventories are projected to their lowest since 2003 while Brent fell ~40–50%. Avoid implying a mechanical inventory-price relationship.
- **A simple "peace deal = cheap oil" narrative.** The deal is interim, with a 60-day nuclear clock and no treaty status. The residual risk premium is real and should not be erased visually.
- **India-inflation-as-pure-oil-shock.** RBI cited crude prices, input costs, and partial fuel pass-through together. Do not reduce India's CPI jump to oil alone.
- **Precise percentage drops where sources give ranges.** Brent peak is reported as ~$120–$126/bbl; present the range rather than a single eye-catching percentage.
- **Geographic overreach.** The Hormuz flow diagram should stick to verified pre-conflict volumes (~20 mb/d) and verified importer exposures (India 40–50% of crude imports). Do not invent country-specific shares.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with Concepts 1 and 3 as the strongest pair: the price-collapse timeline is the most immediate reader hook, and the demand-destruction/supply-shock divergence explains the underlying tension. If a single hero graphic is needed, use Concept 1. If space allows, add Concept 2 (geographic chokepoint) to ground the macro story in physical infrastructure. Concept 4 works as a secondary follow-up for a policy-focused audience.
