# The Rupee–Equity Divergence in India

## The currency is clearing external pressure while domestic flows hold the index steady. Both readings are true.

Think of a family-run restaurant that has survived a decade by building local loyalty. On a Friday evening, the dining room is full. Regulars keep returning. The owner has enough neighbourhood support that the place can absorb a few cancelled tourist buses without emptying out. But in the back office, the produce supplier has raised prices, the gas bill is climbing, and the bank line of credit costs more than it used to. The dining room says the business is healthy. The ledger says the margin is thinner. Both readings are true. The question is which one tells you what happens next.

That is roughly where India finds itself in July 2026.

### Context

The Indian rupee has weakened past 95 to the US dollar, a level that would have been considered extreme even two years ago. The Nifty 50, meanwhile, has stayed in a tight range around 24,200. Foreign portfolio investors have sold roughly $64 billion of Indian equities since the September 2024 peak, including $5.2 billion in June alone. Early July data show a partial reversal: FPIs were net buyers through the first half of the month. In a narrower window, between January and early June 2026, domestic institutional investors absorbed most of the foreign selling in that same period, purchasing around ₹4.16 lakh crore of equities. Systematic investment plan contributions crossed ₹31,781 crore in June, extending the streak above ₹30,000 crore to four months.

This is the divergence everyone is trying to read. Currency markets and equity markets are sending different signals about the same economy. In normal times, sustained foreign selling and a weakening currency tend to coincide with equity stress. That relationship has broken, or at least bent, in 2026. The question is whether one market is leading and the other is lagging, or whether the structure of Indian capital flows has changed enough that the old relationship no longer applies.

What follows is an attempt to separate the known from the unknown, the mechanism from the narrative, and the plausible from the merely convenient.

### Mechanism

The divergence is best understood as two separate flow systems operating on different time horizons and with different constraints.

**The currency side is clearing external pressure.** The rupee is weakening because the demand for dollars has risen on multiple fronts. India’s merchandise trade deficit widened to $30.43 billion in June 2026, up from $19.12 billion a year earlier. Brent crude has traded above $76 per barrel and, as of July 13, briefly touched $79.21, raising the oil import bill. The US dollar index has also strengthened, partly on safe-haven demand after reports of US strikes on Iran in early July. When the dollar rises globally and oil rises simultaneously, the rupee faces a terms-of-trade and funding squeeze regardless of domestic conditions.

The current account deficit for fiscal year 2025-26 was $25.2 billion; while still moderate relative to GDP, the direction is deteriorating. The Reserve Bank of India has intervened, drawing foreign exchange reserves down from a February 2026 peak of $728.5 billion to roughly $703.3 billion by mid-April. It has also used targeted measures, such as FCNR(B) concessional hedging and expanded Fully Accessible Route bond access, to attract capital without burning reserves.

Foreign portfolio selling contributes to this dollar demand, but it is not the primary driver. When FPIs exit Indian equities, they convert rupees back into dollars. A sustained wave of such selling creates persistent pressure on the currency, even if the outflows are orderly. The scale matters: $64 billion in cumulative foreign equity selling since September 2024 is a large stock-flow drag. Still, the trade deficit and oil bill are the larger mechanical forces on the rupee near term.

**The equity side is clearing domestic absorption.** Mutual funds, insurance companies, and pension flows bought roughly ₹4.16 lakh crore of equities between January and early June 2026. SIP flows are a structural feature of this bid: they arrive regardless of whether the market is up or down in a given month. The Employees’ Provident Fund Organisation and other pension-like vehicles also deploy capital on a recurring basis. This creates a price-insensitive buyer on the other side of foreign selling.

The result is a market that has been range-bound rather than collapsing. The Nifty 50 closed at 24,211.00 on 13 July 2026. For the week ended 10 July 2026, the index closed at 24,206.90, down roughly 0.26% from the previous week. India VIX, a measure of implied equity volatility, was at 12.25 on 10 July 2026. That is not the level of an options market pricing acute stress.

**The asset-class split matters.** FPIs are not leaving India wholesale. In June 2026, debt inflows exceeded equity outflows by roughly $500 million. Foreign capital is rotating from Indian equities into Indian government bonds, not fleeing the country. This is a sectoral and asset-class reallocation, not a balance-of-payments crisis. It helps explain why the rupee can weaken while the equity index holds its ground.

**The composition of the index matters too.** The Nifty 50 is heavy in domestic financials, information technology services, and consumer-facing companies. These sectors are less directly exposed to imported energy costs and currency translation than the rupee itself, or than a commodity-heavy index would be. IT services even gain competitiveness from a weaker rupee. This is part of why the headline index can look calm while the external ledger deteriorates.

The RBI, meanwhile, is navigating a familiar trilemma. It wants to support growth, keep inflation near target, and prevent disorderly rupee weakness. It has held the repo rate at 5.25% with a neutral stance and lowered its GDP growth projection for 2026-27 to 6.6%. Inflation, at 4.38% in June, has crossed back above the 4% target for the first time in eighteen months. That limits the central bank’s room to ease policy even if growth slows. It is possible that the RBI is allowing a gradual, controlled depreciation rather than defending a specific level, recognising that some rupee weakness supports export competitiveness and eases the external adjustment.

### Signal / Deviation

The unusual feature is not that the rupee is weak or that equities are stable. It is that both are happening at the same time, with such persistence.

Historically, foreign equity outflows of the current scale have been associated with Nifty drawdowns and elevated volatility. The 2022 Russia-Ukraine episode saw FPIs sell a then-record ~$16.5 billion of Indian equities; the Nifty corrected sharply before recovering. The 2013 taper tantrum produced a much more severe simultaneous shock to the currency, bonds, and equities. In 2026, foreign selling has been larger and more prolonged, yet the index has remained range-bound and volatility has stayed compressed.

The deviation is that domestic absorption has become large enough to override the foreign-sell signal in equity prices. Whether this represents a structural change in India’s market microstructure or a temporary cushion that will eventually compress is the central question.

### Interpretation Spectrum

There are at least four plausible readings of this divergence. None can be ruled out with certainty.

**Reading A: Domestic absorption may be maturing.** The SIP and DII backstop could be large enough to stabilise Indian equities during foreign outflows. The market may be shifting from foreign-flow dependence to domestic-flow dependence. If this is true, the sensitivity of Indian equities to global risk appetite has structurally declined. Evidence in favour: ₹4.16 lakh crore of DII buying in five months, SIPs above ₹30,000 crore for four consecutive months, and the Nifty holding its range despite persistent foreign selling. Evidence against: domestic flows have never been tested through a prolonged bear market or a sharp drawdown; their price-insensitivity may be a feature of stable-to-rising markets rather than a law of nature. SIP flows held up during the 2020 and 2022 corrections, but those were short-lived disruptions.

**Reading B: FX is pricing a risk that equities are ignoring.** The rupee may be the pressure-release valve for an external sector that is deteriorating. Equity indices could be stable only because price-insensitive domestic flows are masking weak foreign conviction. If those domestic flows slow, or if oil prices spike further, equities could catch down to the currency signal. Evidence in favour: USD/INR above 95, trade deficit widening to $30.4 billion, CPI back above target, and reserves drawing down. Evidence against: the current account deficit is still modest as a share of GDP, the RBI has ample reserves, and FPI selling has been partly offset by bond inflows. A counter-reading here is that the RBI is allowing gradual depreciation as a managed adjustment, not fighting a disorderly collapse.

**Reading C: Different markets, different horizons.** Both markets can be rational. The FX market is repricing near-term funding and oil risk. Equity investors are looking through to FY2026-27 earnings and domestic growth. The divergence may resolve not through a crash but through time: either the external shock fades and the rupee recovers, or earnings disappoint and equities reprice. Evidence in favour: the RBI still projects 6.6% GDP growth, and the equity market is pricing a domestic earnings story that the currency market does not dispute. The index composition also insulates the headline Nifty from the direct channels hurting the rupee. Evidence against: if imported inflation persists, the RBI may be forced to tighten or hold rates higher for longer, which would hurt both the growth narrative and equity valuations.

**Reading D: Passive rebalancing and sector rotation.** Part of the FPI selling may be mechanical. India’s weight in MSCI emerging market benchmarks may have fallen as North Asian tech has rallied, forcing passive outflows. At the same time, domestic institutions are deploying SIP and EPFO money into domestic benchmarks. This is less about macro conviction and more about flow plumbing. The divergence could persist until the passive rebalancing ends or until India’s EM weight stabilises. Evidence in favour: reported sectoral data show FPI selling concentrated in BFSI, oil & gas, and autos, with BFSI turning net buyer in June after the rate outlook shifted. Evidence against: passive rebalancing alone cannot explain the scale of the rupee move or the persistence of the divergence.

**Aagman’s structural bias, declared:** We are inclined to view India as being in a long-term capital-flow and demand-driven bull market. That bias makes Reading A more interesting to us than Reading B. But it is a bias, not a conclusion. The data required to confirm a structural shift in domestic absorption do not yet exist. If the data change, the reading must change.

### Historical or Structural Memory

In 2013, a hint of Fed tapering triggered a sudden stop of capital flows into emerging markets. India was labelled one of the “Fragile Five”: the current account deficit was near 4.7% of GDP, inflation was close to 10%, and the rupee depreciated by roughly a fifth. The RBI was forced to raise rates and impose gold-import curbs.

In 2022, the Russia-Ukraine war drove FPIs to sell a then-record ~$16.5 billion of Indian equities, Brent crude crossed $120 per barrel, and the rupee fell to a then-record low near 83.2. Equities corrected but recovered within months as domestic flows remained strong and the commodity shock eased.

Both episodes share the structure of an external shock meeting a domestic market. The difference today is that the current account deficit is far smaller, foreign exchange reserves are near $675 billion, inflation is anchored near target, and domestic flows are larger than in either prior episode. The system is exposed, but it is not fragile in the same way.

### Implications for Decision-Makers

This divergence does not resolve into a simple trade. It does, however, change what careful observers should watch.

**What this affects:**

- **Risk budgeting.** The low level of India VIX may understate the embedded risk if the rupee is correctly pricing external stress. A portfolio that treats the equity index alone as the risk signal may be missing the currency signal.
- **Sector selection.** A weaker rupee raises input costs for oil-importing and commodity-consuming sectors, while exporters and IT services gain competitiveness. Airlines, paints, and downstream oil marketing companies face margin pressure. This is not a recommendation; it is a transmission channel to monitor.
- **Rate expectations.** CPI is back above 4% and the RBI is on hold. If oil or currency pass-through intensifies, the market may price a higher-for-longer rate path. FPI debt inflows via the Fully Accessible Route could partly offset the pressure on yields.
- **Liquidity assumptions.** The domestic bid has provided a floor, but it is not infinite. If SIP growth slows or if retail participation turns, the cushion could compress quickly.

**What this does not affect:**

- **Long-cycle India conviction.** The structural story of domestic demand, formalisation, and capital deepening does not depend on the July 2026 rupee level.
- **The need to distinguish signal from noise.** The divergence is a signal that capital structure is changing. It is not, by itself, a signal that the market will crash or soar.

**What is known, unknown, and unknowable:**

- **Known:** FPIs have sold heavily in 2026; DIIs have absorbed most of it in the same window. The rupee is at multi-year weak levels and the trade deficit is widening. SIP flows remain robust, equity mutual fund inflows recovered in June, and India VIX is low. The RBI is on hold at 5.25%.
- **Unknown:** How long the current FPI selling wave will last; whether DII/SIP inflows can sustain their pace if returns flatten; the trajectory of oil prices and Middle East tensions; the strength of the monsoon and its impact on food inflation.
- **Unknowable:** Sudden geopolitical escalations or peace deals; shifts in global central bank communication that change dollar liquidity; unanticipated domestic policy responses.

### Open Question

The central tension is this: if the domestic bid has become a structural feature of the Indian market, then the rupee-equity divergence is not a warning but a portrait of a maturing capital market. If the domestic bid is mostly a function of a stable-to-rising market environment, then the currency market is showing what the equity market will eventually have to absorb.

The question to leave with is not what to buy or sell. It is whether India’s domestic absorption is a genuine change in market structure, or merely a very large cushion that has not yet been stress-tested.

When the test comes, both the dining room and the ledger will tell the same story. Until then, the wise observer keeps an eye on each.
