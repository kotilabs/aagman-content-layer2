# The Rupee-Equity Divergence in India: Which Market Is Telling the Truth?

Imagine two doctors examining the same patient. One takes the blood pressure and finds it dangerously high. The other checks the temperature and finds it almost normal. Neither is wrong, but they are reading different systems. That is roughly where Indian markets sit in mid-July 2026. The rupee has weakened past 95 to the dollar, foreign portfolio investors have sold Indian equities at a record clip, and the merchandise trade deficit has widened sharply. Yet the Nifty 50 is range-bound around 24,200, equity mutual fund inflows rose 26.5% month-on-month in June, and the India VIX is below 13. One market is flashing a warning. The other is acting as if the patient is stable. The divergence is the story.

## Context: What changed?

The Indian rupee touched a multi-year weak level in early July 2026. The FBIL USD/INR reference rate was 95.4346 on 6 July 2026, up from 95.2408 two days earlier. In interbank trade on 8 July, the rupee opened at 95.15 and slipped to 95.16, down 20 paise, as the dollar index firmed and Brent crude moved higher on reports of US strikes on Iran.

At the same time, foreign portfolio investors have been net sellers of Indian equities through most of 2026. For the four months March–June 2026, FPIs sold a cumulative ~USD 27.7 billion of Indian equities, the most concentrated four-month outflow period on record. Total equity selling since the September 2024 peak reached approximately USD 64 billion by end-June. By the first fortnight of June, cumulative FPI outflows in calendar year 2026 had reached ₹2.87 lakh crore, exceeding the ₹1.66 lakh crore sold in all of 2025.

The twist is that Indian equities have not collapsed. On 13 July 2026, the Nifty 50 closed at 24,207.45. For the week ended 10 July, it closed at 24,211.65, down just 0.24%, with a weekly range of 23,805.20 to 24,530.90. India VIX closed at 12.25 on 10 July, having opened the month at 13.60.

## Mechanism: How the divergence works

Two offsetting forces explain the split screen.

**Rupee weakness.** The currency is under pressure from a widening merchandise trade deficit, elevated crude prices, geopolitical risk premium, and sustained FPI equity outflows that create dollar demand. India’s merchandise trade deficit widened to USD 30.43 billion in June 2026 from USD 19.12 billion in June 2025. The current account deficit for FY 2025-26 was USD 25.2 billion; the merchandise trade deficit in Q4 FY 2025-26 was USD 83.4 billion. Brent crude rose to USD 79.21 per barrel on 13 July, up 4.21% from the previous day and 14.45% higher year-on-year. The RBI has intervened and drawn down foreign exchange reserves from an all-time high of USD 728.494 billion in late February 2026 to USD 703.31 billion by mid-April.

**Equity resilience.** Domestic institutional investors have absorbed the foreign selling. DIIs net purchased approximately ₹4.16 lakh crore of Indian equities between January and early June 2026, while FPIs sold roughly ₹2.7 lakh crore in the same period. March saw the largest monthly DII buying at ₹1.36 lakh crore. Equity mutual fund net inflows rose 26.5% month-on-month to ₹28,973.41 crore in June 2026, and SIP contributions reached ₹31,781 crore, extending a five-month streak above ₹30,000 crore.

**Flow composition matters.** FPIs are not leaving India wholesale. In June, FPI debt inflows exceeded equity outflows by USD 500 million. This is an asset-class and sectoral rotation, not a balance-of-payments panic.

## Signal / deviation: What is unusual?

The divergence is unusual because the currency and equity markets are reading the same macro environment differently. Historically, sustained FPI selling and rupee weakness have coincided with equity stress. This time, domestic flows have provided a floor.

On 1 July 2026, FPIs were net sellers of ₹1,140 crore in the cash market while DIIs were net buyers of ₹3,159 crore — nearly three times the FPI selling. That ratio is the mechanical explanation for the Nifty’s range-bound behaviour. It is also the central question: has India’s equity market become structurally less sensitive to foreign flows, or is it simply masking them?

## Interpretation spectrum: Four readings

**Reading A — Domestic absorption has matured.** The DII/SIP backstop is now large enough to stabilise Indian equities during foreign outflows. India’s market structure has shifted from foreign-flow dependence to domestic-flow dependence. Evidence: ₹4.16 lakh crore of DII buying, SIPs above ₹30,000 crore for five months, and the Nifty holding its range.

**Reading B — FX is pricing a risk equities are ignoring.** The rupee is the pressure-release valve for an external sector that is deteriorating: wider trade deficit, oil risk, and FPI equity outflows. Equity indices are stable only because price-insensitive domestic flows are masking weak foreign conviction. Evidence: USD/INR above 95, trade deficit widening to USD 30.4 billion, CPI back above target, and reserves drawing down.

**Reading C — Different markets, different horizons.** The FX market is repricing near-term funding and oil risk, while equity investors are looking through to FY27 earnings and domestic growth. The divergence resolves not necessarily through a crash but through time. Evidence: RBI still projects 6.6% GDP growth, and FPI selling has been concentrated in specific sectors.

**Reading D — Passive rebalancing and sector rotation.** Part of the FPI selling is mechanical: India’s weight in MSCI EM benchmarks has fallen, forcing passive outflows. Domestic institutions are deploying SIP and EPFO money into domestic benchmarks. This is flow plumbing as much as macro conviction.

*Opinion / bias:* Aagman’s structural bias is that India is in a long-term bull market driven by internal demand and capital flows. That bias inclines us to watch whether domestic absorption is durable, not to assume it is infinite.

## Historical or structural memory

The 2013 Taper Tantrum is the obvious analogue. Then, a hint of Fed tapering triggered a sudden stop of capital flows into emerging markets. India was labelled one of the “Fragile Five”: the current account deficit was near 4.7% of GDP, inflation was close to 10%, and the rupee depreciated by roughly a fifth.

The similarity is foreign selling pressure and rupee weakness triggered by an external shock. The difference is that today’s current account deficit is far smaller, FX reserves are above USD 700 billion, inflation is anchored near target, and domestic flows are absorbing foreign selling rather than amplifying panic. The system is exposed but not fragile in the same way.

The 2022 Russia–Ukraine war is a closer comparison. FPIs sold a record USD 28.4 billion of Indian equities, Brent crude crossed USD 120 per barrel, and the rupee fell to a then-record low near 83.2. Equities corrected but recovered within months as domestic flows remained strong and the commodity shock eased. The difference now is that the rupee has moved well beyond the 2022 low and the FPI selling has been more prolonged.

## Implications for decision-makers

**USD/INR:** Depreciation pressure is likely to persist while oil and geopolitical risks remain elevated. The RBI has reserves to smooth volatility but may allow gradual weakness rather than burn reserves aggressively.

**Indian equities:** The domestic bid provides a floor, but foreign selling caps upside and narrows leadership. A sustained break below the recent range would test whether DII flows are truly price-insensitive.

**Rates:** CPI is back above the 4% target and the RBI is on hold at 5.25% with a neutral stance. If oil or currency pass-through intensifies, the market may price a higher-for-longer rate path. FPI debt inflows via the Fully Accessible Route could partly offset pressure on yields.

**Credit / corporate:** A weaker rupee raises input costs for oil-importing and commodity-consuming sectors; exporters and IT services gain competitiveness. Airlines, paints, and downstream oil marketing companies face margin pressure.

**Commodities:** Brent crude is the swing variable. Any escalation in the Middle East could push oil higher, widening the trade deficit and weakening the rupee further; de-escalation would relieve both.

**Gold / reserves:** The RBI has been repatriating gold, reflecting a broader concern about sovereign asset safety. Reserve accumulation has stalled, and the drawdown since February signals active FX management.

No buy, sell, or hold recommendation is implied. These are observations about market structure, not portfolio advice.

## What is known, unknown, and unknowable

**Known**
- FPIs have sold heavily in 2026; DIIs have absorbed most of it.
- The rupee is at multi-year weak levels and the trade deficit is widening.
- SIP flows remain robust, equity mutual fund inflows recovered in June, and India VIX is low.
- The RBI is on hold at 5.25% and has deployed targeted capital-inflow measures.

**Unknown**
- How long the current FPI selling wave will last and what triggers a reversal.
- Whether DII/SIP inflows can sustain their current pace if equity returns stay flat or turn negative.
- The trajectory of oil prices and the duration of Middle East tensions.
- The strength of the monsoon and its impact on food inflation and rural demand.

**Unknowable**
- Sudden geopolitical escalations or peace deals that reprice oil and risk appetite overnight.
- Shifts in global central bank communication that change dollar liquidity conditions.
- Unanticipated domestic policy responses or political economy shocks.

## Open question

If the domestic bid is price-insensitive, it can absorb foreign selling indefinitely — until it isn’t. The unresolved tension is whether the rupee is warning about an external risk that equities will eventually have to price, or whether the currency is simply clearing a terms-of-trade shock while domestic flows keep stocks stable. The market structure has changed enough to make the old playbook incomplete. It has not changed enough to make the currency irrelevant.
