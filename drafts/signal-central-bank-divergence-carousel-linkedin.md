# LinkedIn Promotional Carousel — Central-Bank Divergence

**Signal ID:** central-bank-divergence  
**Platform:** LinkedIn  
**Slides:** 9  
**Date:** 2026-06-27

---

## Slide 1 — Hook

**Kicker:** One week in June

**Headline:** The Fed held, the ECB hiked, and the RBA paused — and the global policy anchor disappeared in the same breath.

**Body:** For years, the major central banks moved in rough concert: hold together, ease together, tighten together. In mid-June 2026, the dollar, euro and Australian dollar blocs delivered a split verdict. Even after the hike, the ECB's deposit rate at 2.25% remained the lowest of the three. That split is the signal.

**Hero stat/quote:** Federal Reserve held the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% on 17 June; the ECB raised all three key rates by 25bp on 11 June; the RBA left its cash rate at 4.35% on 16 June. — Federal Reserve, ECB, RBA

**Design note:** Three central bank buildings (or flags) side by side, arrows pointing in different directions. Neutral palette, one accent color per bank.

**Speaker note:** Names the shift immediately: the era of coordinated central-bank policy is over, at least for this window.

---

## Slide 2 — The Fed

**Kicker:** The hold that wasn't

**Headline:** The Fed kept rates unchanged but its own projections now imply a 2026 hike.

**Body:** The median year-end fed funds projection rose from 3.4% to 3.8% in one meeting. Nine of eighteen FOMC participants now project at least one hike; in March, zero did. One participant projected a cut, underscoring the committee divide. The statement dropped its easing-bias language.

**Hero stat/quote:** Median 2026 federal funds rate projection: 3.8% in June, up from 3.4% in March. — Federal Reserve, Summary of Economic Projections, 17 June 2026

**Design note:** Dot plot before/after — sparse March cluster lower, June cluster shifted right. Use actual Fed projection visual language.

**Speaker note:** Establishes that the Fed's action was not the rate decision but the forward-path re-pricing.

---

## Slide 3 — The ECB

**Kicker:** Hiking into weakness

**Headline:** The ECB raised rates for the first time since 2023 while cutting its own growth forecast.

**Body:** Lagarde called the decision "simply a good decision from the point of view of monetary policy" and rejected the label "insurance hike." The deposit facility rate went to 2.25%, but the 2026 GDP projection was cut to 0.8%. The bank is tightening into a downgrade.

**Hero stat/quote:** ECB deposit facility rate: 2.25% (+25bp), effective 17 June 2026; ECB 2026 GDP growth projection cut to 0.8%. — European Central Bank, 11 June 2026

**Design note:** A single scale with growth forecast on one side and policy rate on the other, moving in opposite directions.

**Speaker note:** Shows the second bank tightening into weakness — a credibility move, not a growth endorsement.

---

## Slide 4 — The RBA

**Kicker:** Pause, not stop

**Headline:** The RBA paused after three consecutive 2026 hikes, but kept its finger on the trigger.

**Body:** Australian inflation is still above target, yet unemployment rose to 4.5% in April and Q1 GDP slowed to 0.3% q/q. The bank's statement retained the phrase "including increasing the cash rate target further if required." The pause is conditional.

**Hero stat/quote:** RBA cash rate target: 4.35% (hold) on 16 June 2026, after 25bp hikes in February, March and May. — Reserve Bank of Australia

**Design note:** A trail of three upward steps ending in a flat plateau, with a small upward arrow as a question mark.

**Speaker note:** Completes the three-bank picture: the RBA is observing, not declaring victory.

---

## Slide 5 — Pivot / Thesis

**Kicker:** The point

**Headline:** This isn't synchronized policy. It's a world without a single global policy anchor.

**Body:** When central banks move together, relative-rate trades are quiet. When they diverge, every currency, bond and commodity has to reprice against a moving benchmark. The anchor is gone; the trades are not.

**Hero stat/quote:** By late June: DXY broke above 100; EUR/USD fell below 1.15; AUD/USD softened toward 0.69. — MarketPulse / RBA homepage

**Design note:** A ship's anchor sinking below the frame while exchange-rate tickers drift in different directions. One line of text only.

**Speaker note:** The pivot slide. Names the thesis explicitly and transitions from individual banks to market-level consequences.

---

## Slide 6 — FX Mechanism

**Kicker:** What moves currencies

**Headline:** Currencies price the expected path of short rates, not just the current rate change.

**Body:** The ECB actually hiked; the euro still fell. Why? The hike was priced and Lagarde offered no follow-up commitment. The Fed held; the dollar still rose. Why? The dot plot shifted. Markets trade the forward path, not the headline.

**Hero stat/quote:** By late June: DXY broke above 100; EUR/USD fell below 1.15; AUD/USD softened toward 0.69. — MarketPulse / RBA homepage

**Design note:** Three currency tickers or small trend lines — DXY rising above 100, EUR/USD falling below 1.15, AUD/USD drifting toward 0.69 — labeled as late-June levels. Clean, sparse.

**Speaker note:** Explains the FX reaction through mechanism, not assertion.

---

## Slide 7 — Rates and Gold

**Kicker:** Twist flattening

**Headline:** The Treasury curve flattened for a hawkish reason, not a recessionary one.

**Body:** The front end repriced on Fed dots; the long end was comforted by falling oil and a fading supply shock. Gold sold off because higher real U.S. yields raise the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding bullion — even as a Middle East war raged.

**Hero stat/quote:** 2-year U.S. Treasury yield rose ~10bp after the Fed; 30-year yield fell ~7bp; gold spot fell toward $4,155–$4,220/oz. — Mariemont Capital / IndexBox / Yahoo Finance

**Design note:** 2s30s spread compressing, with a small gold bar drifting downward. Two-panel layout.

**Speaker note:** Distinguishes a policy-driven flattening from a growth-collapse flattening.

---

## Slide 8 — The Oil Complication

**Kicker:** The unwind

**Headline:** Brent crude has collapsed from roughly $140 in April to below $73 — before the central banks have finished responding.

**Body:** The U.S.–Iran memorandum signed on 18 June reopened the Strait of Hormuz narrative and drained the geopolitical premium. If oil stays near $70–$75, the Fed's 3.6% PCE inflation forecast for 2026 may already be stale. The MoU starts a 60-day negotiating window; mines in Hormuz and Israeli objections could keep a residual premium.

**Hero stat/quote:** Brent crude oil fell to approximately $72.95/bbl on 26 June 2026, down roughly 21% over the month. — Trading Economics, 27 June 2026

**Design note:** A steep downward oil price line, with small central bank logos chasing it from above. Sparse.

**Speaker note:** Introduces the central unknowable: policy is tightening just as the inflation impulse may be fading.

---

## Slide 9 — Open Question + CTA

**Kicker:** The essay asks

**Headline:** Will the Fed follow its own dot plot, or will the dot plot follow oil?

**Body:** If the Iran deal holds and energy prices keep falling, the case for a 2026 Fed hike weakens quickly. If the deal frays, the ECB and RBA may be forced back into the hiking room. The divergence is durable; the direction is not.

**Hero stat/quote:** "If Brent stays near $70–$75, the Fed's 3.6% PCE forecast for 2026 may prove too high, undermining the case for a hike." — Aagman Layer 2 Research

**Design note:** A question mark formed from a yield curve and an oil derrick. Below it, one CTA line: "Read the full essay on Substack — link in comments."

**Speaker note:** Closes with the unresolved tension and a single, specific CTA.
