# The Endgame of the Global Rate-Cut Cycle: Why Central Banks Are Out of Easy Choices

Think of the global economy as a household that refinanced its mortgage in 2020 at an almost insultingly low teaser rate. For a few years the monthly payment was small enough to finance renovations, keep the heat high, and ignore the cracks in the foundation. Then the teaser expired. The new rate is not necessarily unaffordable, but it is high enough that the renovation budget has shrunk, the thermostat has come down, and every crack is suddenly worth inspecting. The house is the same house. The income is roughly the same income. But the cost of carrying the structure has changed, and that changes every decision that follows. This is where the major central banks have left the world after the post-inflation easing cycle: not in crisis, but in a different room of the same house, with less spare cash and more things to fix.

The major advanced-economy central banks have largely stopped cutting rates, leaving the world in a constrained equilibrium where rates are high enough to discipline valuations but not high enough to deflate the debt built over the preceding decade.

## Context

The conversation has shifted. For most of 2024 and early 2025, the question was how fast central banks would cut. Now the question is what happens if they stop cutting while rates are still well above the floors of the 2010s.

**Fact:** The Federal Reserve has held the fed funds target range at 3.50–3.75% since December 2025. At its June 2026 meeting, the Federal Open Market Committee left the range unchanged.

**Fact:** The June 2026 FOMC Summary of Economic Projections shows the median fed funds rate at 3.8% at end-2026, 3.6% in 2027, 3.4% in 2028, and a longer-run rate of 3.1%.

**Fact:** The Fed raised its 2026 projection by 40 basis points between March and June 2026, from 3.4% to 3.8%. The 2026 [PCE inflation](/layer1/inflation-primer) projection jumped to 3.6% year-over-year.

**Fact:** The European Central Bank raised its deposit facility rate in June 2026 from 2.0%; the research artifact does not specify the new level. This was the ECB's first hike since September 2023. The Bank of Japan raised its policy rate to 1.00% at its June 16, 2026 meeting, continuing a gradual exit from negative rates and yield-curve control.

The advanced-economy easing cycle is largely over. The new focus is the *terminal* rate: high enough to enforce valuation discipline, but not high enough to deflate the debt accumulated over the preceding decade.

## How does the end of rate cuts affect asset prices?

This signal operates through three connected channels.

**1. The discount-rate channel.** A sustained policy rate of 3.5–4.0% anchors the risk-free hurdle used to value equities, real estate, and long-duration credit. The actual risk-free discount rate for a ten-year cash flow is the Treasury yield curve, not the overnight fed funds rate, but the policy rate is the anchor that pulls it higher. Asset prices must now clear a higher bar on cash-flow growth and credit quality than during the 2010s, when rates were compressed by quantitative easing and low neutral-rate estimates. A cash flow ten years out is worth less today when discounted at 4% than when discounted at 1%. This is arithmetic, not opinion.

**2. The debt-sustainability channel.** Governments, corporates, and households refinanced at historically low rates during 2020–2021. With policy rates now "[higher for longer](/layer2/fed-terminal-rate-debate)," new issuance and rolled-over debt reset at higher coupons. The IMF projects gross global debt reaching 100% of GDP by 2029 and government interest payments near 3% of global GDP. Fiscal space narrows even without a recession. This is the [debt sustainability](/layer2/fiscal-dominance-signal) problem that makes the end of cuts structurally different from a normal pause.

**3. The policy-flexibility channel.** Central banks are no longer cutting to support demand. If a shock hits, they have less room to ease without reigniting inflation — currently above target in the US and globally — or triggering currency depreciation and capital outflows, especially in economies with large external debt and low-rate [carry trades](/layer1/yen-funding-costs).

The result is a constrained equilibrium. Rates are too low to force rapid deleveraging, but too high to allow the kind of aggressive stimulus that markets became accustomed to in the 2010s.

## What makes this rate regime unusual?

The unusual element is not that rates are high by long-run historical standards. They are not. The deviation is the *combination* of high rates, high debt, and sticky inflation in a world that still expects central banks to act as a backstop — and that deviation is relative to the post-GFC zero-bound regime, not the 1970s–1990s.

The Fed's longer-run neutral estimate has shifted materially higher versus the 2010s. The ECB is hiking, not easing. The BoJ is normalising. These are not the actions of central banks preparing to rescue risk assets at the first sign of trouble.

One caveat to the aggregate framing: "gross global debt" blends advanced economies and emerging markets. The US, euro area, and Japan have very different debt dynamics, currency denominations, and domestic investor bases. The aggregate number matters for the global picture, but it does not mean every economy is equally constrained.

## Interpretation Spectrum

There are at least three plausible readings of the same data. None can be dismissed.

**Interpretation A: "Higher-for-longer is the new neutral."**
Fed officials, via the dot plot, and the IMF, via WEO projections, lean this way. The evidence: the FOMC raised its 2026 projection to 3.8%, the longer-run estimate is 3.1%, core CPI is near 3%, and global growth is holding up at 3.0% without major financial stress. The risk to this view is that it assumes the neutral rate has permanently risen. A recession or financial accident could quickly reprice the terminal rate lower.

**Interpretation B: "Central banks are one shock away from restarting cuts."**
Bond bulls and risk-asset allocators often make this case. Growth is below trend; inflation has fallen from 2022 peaks; the ECB's June 2026 move was its first hike since 2023, suggesting caution. The evidence against: the Fed dot plot moved *upward*, not down; the BoJ is hiking, not cutting; and the IMF projects sticky inflation.

There is also a direct counter to the "higher-for-longer" framing itself. With headline CPI at 4.2% and the Fed funds target range at 3.50–3.75%, the ex-post real Fed funds rate is roughly -0.5% to -0.7%. In real terms, monetary policy may still be accommodative even if nominal rates feel elevated compared with the 2010s.

**Interpretation C: "Fiscal dominance will force rates higher regardless."**
Fiscal hawks and debt-sustainability analysts argue that global debt approaching 100% of GDP and rising interest payments will push rates up even if growth slows. The counter: there is no current sovereign funding crisis in major economies, and demand for safe assets remains strong. The US, euro area, and Japan still borrow in their own currencies with deep domestic investor bases.

**Opinion / bias:** Aagman's declared structural bias is that the long-term interest rate cycle has bottomed and structural pressure is upward. That bias informs what we watch. It does not dictate a conclusion. If data contradicts the bias, data wins.

## What we know, what we don't, and what we can't know

A useful way to keep the uncertainty in view:

**Known**
- The Fed, ECB, and BoJ are holding policy rates well above the 2010s floor.
- The FOMC projects the fed funds rate staying above 3.5% through 2026 and a longer-run neutral near 3.1%.
- Global debt is elevated and interest payments are rising as a share of GDP.

**Unknown**
- Whether the current inflation stickiness is temporary or persistent.
- How quickly growth will slow as prior rate hikes continue to transmit through the economy.
- Whether fiscal authorities will tighten policy in response to higher debt service, or let deficits widen.

**Unknowable**
- The precise location of the neutral real interest rate, which is unobservable and inferred only with lag.
- The timing and nature of the next shock that could force central banks off their current path.
- The political economy of debt sustainability in an era of ageing populations and climate-transition spending needs.

This is the standard Layer 2 posture: separate the facts from the guesses, and the guesses from the things that cannot be known in advance.

## Historical or Structural Memory

Two episodes are useful for perspective, not prediction.

**The [mid-1990s Fed pause](/layer1/historical-regime-archive).** After cutting in 1995–96, the Fed held rates around 5.25% for an extended period while the economy grew and inflation stayed contained. This shows that "higher for longer" can coexist with growth. The difference: debt levels in the mid-1990s were far lower than today, so fiscal constraints were not binding.

**The 2018 hiking cycle and 2019 reversal.** The Fed hiked to 2.25–2.50% by late 2018, then cut three times in 2019 as growth and market stress mounted. This demonstrates that terminal-rate projections can shift quickly. The difference: inflation was below target in 2019, giving the Fed cover to cut. In 2026, inflation is above target, making a 2019-style pivot harder.

History here should make the present feel less urgent, not more dramatic. The pattern is familiar even if the debt stock is not. (This framing is anchored in the research artifact's historical-analogues section.)

## What does the end of the cutting cycle mean for decision-makers?

This affects:

- **Capital allocation.** The cost of carry has reset. Long-duration cash flows, leveraged structures, and assets priced for permanently low rates face the most pressure.
- **Refinancing calendars.** Corporate and sovereign issuers must plan for coupons materially above those locked in during 2020–2021.
- **Policy expectations.** The bar for aggressive central bank easing is higher when inflation is above target. Fiscal policy may need to do more of the work in the next slowdown.
- **Risk management.** Volatility around central bank meetings is likely to persist because the terminal-rate distribution is wide and the data are noisy.

This does **not** affect:

- The need for a long-term asset allocation plan. One rate regime does not invalidate diversification.
- The validity of every growth story. Higher rates are a headwind, not a death sentence, for businesses that generate cash.
- The case for emergency rate cuts as a base case. The Fed is on hold, not in panic mode.

No stock tips, price targets, or return promises follow from this. The implication is structural: the world is carrying more debt at higher rates than it did a decade ago, and that changes how policy and markets behave.

## Open Question

If the next downturn arrives while inflation is still above target and debt service is already crowding out other spending, which constraint binds first: the central bank's inflation mandate, or the government's fiscal arithmetic? The answer will determine whether the next easing cycle looks like the ones we remember, or like something we have not yet seen.
