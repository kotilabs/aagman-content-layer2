### The Endgame of the Global Rate-Cut Cycle

Imagine a homeowner who has spent a decade taking long baths because water was cheap. The boiler worked, the bills were small, and no one questioned the habit. Then the utility company raised rates. Now every bath costs meaningfully more. He can still bathe, but he can no longer pretend the cost does not matter. He cannot go back to the old routine without wasting money, and he cannot stop bathing entirely without real discomfort. So he sits in a constrained middle: warm enough to function, expensive enough to change behavior.

That is roughly where the major advanced-economy central banks are today. The post-inflation cutting cycle is largely over, for now. The question is no longer whether the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, or the Bank of Japan will ease again. The question is whether they can afford to stay where they are — and what that does to every asset price built on the assumption that rates could always fall back to zero.

---

### Context: The Cut Cycle Is Largely Over

The facts are straightforward. The US federal funds rate target range has been 3.50–3.75% since December 2025, and the Federal Open Market Committee held it unchanged at its June 2026 meeting. The ECB raised its deposit facility rate in June 2026 — the first increase since September 2023. The exact post-hike level was not specified in the available data. The Bank of Japan raised its policy rate to 1.00% on June 16, 2026. These are not emergency levels. They are also not the near-zero floors that defined the 2010s and the pandemic years.

What changed the conversation is the June 2026 FOMC Summary of Economic Projections. The median fed funds rate projection for end-2026 rose to 3.8%, up from 3.4% in March. The longer-run estimate moved to 3.1%. The 2026 PCE inflation projection jumped to 3.6% year-over-year. Inflation data for May 2026 showed US CPI at 4.2% and core CPI at 2.9%.

The dot plot also implies a gradual descent: 3.8% at end-2026, 3.6% in 2027, 3.4% in 2028, and 3.1% longer-run. So the regime is not a permanent plateau at 3.75%. It is a higher-for-longer path toward a higher neutral rate than the one markets grew used to.

The global context reinforces the point. The IMF’s July 2026 World Economic Outlook projects global growth of 3.0% in 2026 and 3.4% in 2027, with global headline inflation at 4.7% and 3.9% over the same horizon. Meanwhile, the IMF’s May 2026 Fiscal Monitor projects gross global debt reaching 100% of GDP by 2029 and government interest payments near 3% of global GDP.

So the signal is not a single policy move. It is a regime change on the current base case: the easy part of the post-inflation adjustment is finished, and the hard part — living with the consequences — has begun.

### Mechanism: Three Channels

Why does this matter so much? Because the level of the central bank’s policy rate is not just a number on a screen. It is the anchor that propagates through three connected mechanisms.

**The discount-rate channel.**

Every long-duration asset — equities, real estate, long-maturity credit — is priced as a stream of future cash flows discounted back to today. The risk-free rate is the denominator in that calculation. When the risk-free rate was near zero, distant cash flows looked valuable. A growth stock with most of its expected profits in 2035, a thirty-year bond, or a leveraged commercial property deal could all be justified at high valuations.

With the Fed funds rate at 3.50–3.75% and the longer-run neutral rate estimated at 3.1%, the denominator is no longer negligible. Future cash flows must be larger, more certain, or closer in time to justify current prices. This is why valuation discipline returns. It is not a moral story about greed. It is arithmetic.

**The debt-sustainability channel.**

Governments, corporates, and households issued a great deal of debt in 2020 and 2021 at rates that now look like a historical anomaly. As that debt matures and rolls over, it resets at higher coupons. The IMF’s projection of gross global debt at 100% of GDP by 2029, with government interest payments near 3% of global GDP, captures the mechanical consequence.

This matters because debt service is not abstract. It competes with other spending. For a sovereign, higher interest payments mean less fiscal room for defense, pensions, or infrastructure before deficits widen further. For a company, it means cash flows that once went to investment or dividends now go to creditors. For a household, it means less disposable income.

The constraint is not necessarily a crisis. It is a narrowing of margin.

**The policy-flexibility channel.**

Central banks entered 2025 with the ability to cut aggressively because inflation had fallen. They have now largely used that option. If a shock arrives — a credit event, a geopolitical disruption, a sharper growth slowdown — their room to respond is limited.

Cutting rates from 3.75% is possible, but it is not the same as cutting from 0.50%. Every cut now risks reigniting inflation, which is already above target, or weakening the currency and triggering capital outflows, especially in economies that borrow in dollars. The central bank is no longer the obvious first responder. It is one actor in a crowded room.

The result is a constrained equilibrium. Rates are too low to force rapid deleveraging and too high to allow the aggressive stimulus that markets became accustomed to in the 2010s.

### Signal / Deviation: The Constrained Equilibrium

What is unusual is not that rates are high by historical standards. They are not. The 1990s and early 2000s saw higher nominal rates. What is unusual is the combination of variables: nominal rates well above the 2010s floor, elevated debt, above-target inflation, and central banks that are trying to hold rather than ease.

On the real-rate measure, the picture is more nuanced than the nominal rate alone suggests. Against May 2026 headline CPI of 4.2%, the ex-post real fed funds rate is roughly -0.5% to -0.7%. Against core CPI of 2.9%, it is roughly +0.6% to +0.85%. On one measure, policy is still accommodative; on the other, it is mildly tight. This ambiguity is itself part of the signal.

### Interpretation Spectrum

There are four plausible readings of this environment. None is certain.

**Interpretation A: “Higher-for-longer is the new neutral.”**

This is the Fed’s implied story. The dot plot shows rates staying above 3.5% through 2026 and a longer-run neutral of 3.1%. Core inflation is near 3%, growth is near 3%, and financial markets have not broken. The argument is that the neutral rate has risen because of productivity gains, fiscal expansion, and a structural shift in capital demand.

The weakness of this view is that it assumes the neutral rate is stable and knowable. It is neither. It is inferred only with lag. If growth slows more than expected, the same Fed that projects 3.8% could be cutting within a year. The source of the 3.6% PCE overshoot — demand, supply, tariffs, or one-off factors — also matters. If it is supply-side, the Fed may have more room to cut than the projection suggests.

**Interpretation B: “Central banks are one shock away from restarting cuts.”**

This view starts with the observation that growth is below trend, inflation has fallen substantially from 2022 peaks, and the ECB’s June move was cautious after a long pause. Bond bulls and risk-asset allocators argue that the cutting cycle is merely paused, not over.

The weakness here is that it underweights the Fed’s upward revision. The dot plot moved higher, not lower. The Bank of Japan is hiking. The IMF projects sticky inflation. A shock might force cuts, but the starting point for a pivot is less forgiving than in 2019.

**Interpretation C: “Fiscal dominance will force rates higher regardless.”**

This is the debt-sustainability argument. With global debt near 100% of GDP and interest payments rising, governments will have to issue more debt to service existing debt. At some point, markets demand higher yields, and central banks lose control of the term structure.

The weakness is that this conflates high debt with imminent crisis. The US, euro area, and Japan still borrow in their own currencies and have deep domestic investor bases. Fiscal stress is real, but it does not automatically translate into a bond-market revolt.

**Interpretation D: “Real rates are still accommodative, so the tightening story is overstated.”**

This is the strongest counterargument. A 3.50–3.75% nominal rate looks high relative to the 2010s floor, but it is below the 4.2% headline inflation rate. If the relevant measure for the economy is headline, then policy is still loose and the “endgame” framing is premature. Only on core does it look mildly restrictive.

The weakness is that central banks do not target headline in real time, and the inflation composition matters. But the point stands: nominal rates alone do not prove the era of easy money is over.

Our read — clearly labeled as interpretation, not fact — is that the burden of proof has shifted. For a decade, the default assumption was that central banks would ease if anything went wrong. Now, on the current base case, the default assumption should be that they have less room to do so, unless conditions clearly deteriorate.

### Known, Unknown, and Unknowable

**Known**
- The Fed, ECB, and BoJ are holding policy rates well above the 2010s floor.
- The FOMC projects the fed funds rate declining to 3.8% in 2026, 3.6% in 2027, 3.4% in 2028, and a longer-run neutral near 3.1%.
- Global debt is elevated and interest payments are rising as a share of GDP.

**Unknown**
- Whether the current inflation stickiness is temporary or persistent.
- How quickly growth will slow as prior rate hikes continue to transmit through the economy.
- Whether fiscal authorities will tighten policy in response to higher debt service, or let deficits widen.
- Whether the ECB’s June 2026 hike is a one-off inflation response or the start of a new tightening cycle.

**Unknowable**
- The precise location of the neutral real interest rate, which is unobservable and inferred only with lag.
- The timing and nature of the next shock that could force central banks off their current path.
- The political economy of debt sustainability in an era of ageing populations and climate-transition spending needs.

### Structural Memory: Two Pauses

History is useful here only as perspective, not as prediction.

The mid-1990s offer one reference. After cutting in 1995–96, the Fed held rates around 5.25% for an extended period while the economy grew and inflation stayed contained. “Higher for longer” can coexist with growth. The key difference is that debt levels in the mid-1990s were far lower, so debt service was not a binding constraint.

The 2018–2019 cycle offers another. The Fed hiked to 2.25–2.50% by late 2018, then cut three times in 2019 as growth and market stress mounted. The key similarity is that terminal-rate projections can shift quickly. The key difference is that inflation was below target in 2019, giving the Fed cover to cut. Today, inflation is above target, and that cover is gone.

Both episodes remind us that central banks react to conditions, not to their own projections. But they also remind us that the conditions required to justify a pivot are different this time.

### Implications for Decision-Makers

What this affects is the quality of risk-taking across asset classes.

Long-duration assets must now justify themselves against a higher discount rate. Cash-flow certainty and near-term profitability become more important relative to growth narratives. Leverage becomes more expensive, which pressures the weakest borrowers first. Fiscal policy becomes more consequential because monetary policy has less room to offset it.

What it does not affect is the underlying productive capacity of the economy, the pace of technological change, or the basic rule that capital eventually flows to where it is treated well. It also does not provide a reason to panic. Higher rates are a constraint, not necessarily a catastrophe.

| Asset class | Channel | Implication |

| Derivatives / volatility | Policy uncertainty | Volatility term structure may steepen around central bank meetings |

We do not offer buy or sell recommendations. The implication is analytical: the era in which the central bank reliably backstopped every dip is fading, and portfolios should be built with that in mind.

### Open Question

If the next shock arrives while inflation is still above target and debt service is already consuming a larger share of government budgets, what will central banks prioritize? Growth, credibility, or currency stability? And is the ECB’s June 2026 hike a one-off inflation response or the opening move of a new tightening cycle? If markets conclude that central banks no longer have the room to be the buyer of last resort, what premium will they demand for taking risk?

That is the endgame of the rate-cut cycle. Not a crash, not a rescue, but a world in which the central bank’s options are narrower than they have been in a long time. The question is whether investors have adjusted their mental models accordingly.
