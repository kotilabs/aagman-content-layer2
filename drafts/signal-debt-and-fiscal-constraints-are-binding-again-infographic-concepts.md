## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The Global Debt Clock — Four Numbers That Sum the Regime
- **Central insight:** Global public debt, total global debt, annual interest payments, and EM refinancing needs together show that leverage has moved from a balance-sheet footnote to a binding budget constraint.
- **Data to include:**
  - Global gross government debt: ~94% of GDP (2025), 100% by 2029 — IMF
  - Global total debt: $353 trillion (end-March 2026) — IIF
  - Global government interest payments: ~3% of GDP, up from ~2% four years ago — IMF
  - EM refinancing needs: >$9 trillion in 2026 — IIF
- **Visualization type:** Large-number hero layout with four proportional blocks.
- **Composition notes:** Lead with the $353 trillion total-debt figure as the anchor. Arrange the other three numbers around it with directional arrows showing the flow from stock → interest → rollover. Use muted public-finance palette (navy, slate, small red accents for stress). Payoff: the four numbers are individually knowable, but together they describe a regime shift.
- **Sources:** IMF Fiscal Monitor (Apr 2026); IIF Global Debt Monitor (Feb 2026), IIF Debt Monitors (May 2026).

### Concept 2: How Fast Interest Became a Major Budget Line
- **Central insight:** Government interest payments have risen from ~2% to ~3% of global GDP in four years, crowding out the fiscal space that once absorbed shocks.
- **Data to include:**
  - Global government interest payments: ~2% of global GDP → ~3% of global GDP — IMF
  - Global fiscal gap: >1% of GDP cushion a decade ago → near zero today — IMF
  - U.S. net interest: $857 billion FY2026 YTD (~$23.8 billion/week) — CBO
  - Nigeria interest/revenue: 53.7% of federal revenue in 2026 — IMF
- **Visualization type:** Split bar or area chart showing the four-year rise, paired with small callouts for the U.S. and Nigeria examples.
- **Composition notes:** Left side: a timeline/area chart of global interest payments. Right side: two vertical callouts stacked — one for the U.S. weekly interest bill, one for Nigeria's revenue share. The implied comparison is between advanced-economy scale and emerging-market intensity. Payoff: interest is no longer a rounding error.
- **Sources:** IMF Fiscal Monitor (Apr 2026); CBO, via Fortune (Jul 2026); IMF, via TheCable (Jun 2026).

### Concept 3: The 2026 Rollover Wall
- **Central insight:** Emerging markets face >$9 trillion in refinancing needs in 2026, advanced economies are lengthening T-bill issuance, and the entire stock was largely issued when rates were lower.
- **Data to include:**
  - EM refinancing needs: >$9 trillion in 2026 — IIF
  - OECD sovereign bond debt outstanding: $61 trillion (83% of GDP) — OECD
  - U.S. debt held by the public: 101% of GDP (FY2026), 120% by FY2036 — CBO
  - Japan general government debt: 200.8% of GDP — CEIC Data
- **Visualization type:** Horizontal stacked bar or waterfall showing the composition of refinancing by borrower category, with a yield-rate overlay.
- **Composition notes:** Start with a wide bar segmented by U.S., other advanced, emerging markets. Layer a small timeline strip above it showing that much of this stock was issued during 2020–21 low-rate windows. Add a thin red band for the portion maturing within 12 months. Payoff: the rollover problem is not future-tense; it is this year's calendar problem.
- **Sources:** IIF Global Debt Monitor (Feb 2026); OECD Global Debt Report 2026 (Mar 2026); CBO February 2026 baseline; CEIC Data (Mar 2026).

### Concept 4: Fiscal Space Is Now a Line Item, Not a Cushion
- **Central insight:** The IMF's global fiscal gap has collapsed from a >1% of GDP cushion a decade ago to roughly zero, leaving no buffer against shocks.
- **Data to include:**
  - Global fiscal gap: >1% of GDP cushion → near zero — IMF
  - U.S. FY2026 deficit: $1.9 trillion (5.8% of GDP) — CBO
  - China fiscal deficit: ~8% of GDP — IMF
  - France 2025 deficit: 5.1% of GDP — INSEE
  - Sub-Saharan Africa median fiscal deficit: 3.2% of GDP in 2026 — IMF
- **Visualization type:** Divergence chart or slope graph showing the gap disappearing, with country-level deficit callouts below.
- **Composition notes:** Top half: a simple slope from +1% to ~0% labeled "fiscal gap." Bottom half: a row of small bars for the U.S., China, France, and Sub-Saharan Africa median, each showing deficit as a share of GDP. Payoff: deficits are structural, not cyclical, and the safety margin is gone.
- **Sources:** IMF Fiscal Monitor (Apr 2026); CBO February 2026 baseline; INSEE, via Xinhua (Jun 2026); IMF Regional Economic Outlook, via Leadership News (Apr 2026).

### Concept 5: One Economy, One Stat — The Pressure Map
- **Central insight:** The debt constraint shows up differently across economies: as an interest-revenue ratio in Nigeria, a yield spike in Japan, a deficit trajectory in the U.S., and a debt-ratio projection in China.
- **Data to include:**
  - U.S.: net interest $857 billion FY2026 YTD; debt held by public 101% of GDP, 120% by FY2036 — CBO
  - China: deficit ~8% of GDP; debt toward 127% by 2031 — IMF
  - Japan: debt 200.8% of GDP; 10-year yield 2.79% — CEIC; Trading Economics
  - France: debt 117.5% of GDP; deficit 5.1% of GDP — INSEE
  - Nigeria: interest/revenue 53.7% — IMF
- **Visualization type:** World map or regional grid with one highlighted stat per major economy.
- **Composition notes:** Use a sparse world map with five pinned callouts. Each pin shows one country, one number, and one source. Keep the design institutional and low-saturation. Payoff: the constraint is global, but its expression is local.
- **Sources:** CBO February 2026 baseline; IMF Fiscal Monitor (Apr 2026); CEIC Data; Trading Economics (Jul 2026); INSEE, via Xinhua (Jun 2026); IMF, via TheCable (Jun 2026).

## What to avoid

- **Avoid a simple "debt is bad" headline.** The research supports a regime-shift framing, not a moral claim. The interesting point is that the arithmetic of debt sustainability has changed, not that debt itself is novel.
- **Avoid conflating total global debt with government debt.** The $353 trillion IIF figure includes household and corporate debt; it should not be presented as a sovereign-debt number.
- **Avoid implying an imminent crisis.** The research presents competing interpretations, including the view that nominal growth is still outrunning borrowing costs in many places. Do not collapse that nuance into alarmism.
- **Avoid ranking countries by "riskiness" without context.** Japan's 200.8% debt ratio is sustainable in part because it is yen-denominated and domestically held; Nigeria's 53.7% interest/revenue ratio reflects a different kind of stress.
- **Avoid visualizing unverifiable forward paths as certainties.** CBO and IMF projections should be labeled as projections, not outcomes.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with Concepts 1, 2, and 5 as the strongest trio. Concept 1 gives the bird's-eye arithmetic, Concept 2 shows the mechanism (interest burden), and Concept 5 shows the geographic heterogeneity. Concepts 3 and 4 are viable if a longer series is needed, but they overlap with Concept 1's stock/rollover logic and Concept 2's fiscal-space logic.
