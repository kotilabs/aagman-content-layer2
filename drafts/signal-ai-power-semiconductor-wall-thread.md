1/
The scarce input for artificial intelligence is no longer the GPU; it is the path between the grid and the rack.

2/
Hyperscalers are projected to spend more than $600 billion on infrastructure this year, about 75% of it on AI. Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft alone could top $700 billion, up from roughly $410 billion in 2025.

Data center electricity use rose 17% in 2025, and the IEA expects it to roughly double by 2030. Goldman Sachs forecasts 165% growth from 2023 levels, with global data center power demand rising from ~55 GW today to 84 GW by 2027.

3/
For two years the shortage story was GPUs and high-bandwidth memory. CoWoS packaging is ramping, but HBM and AI memory demand remain tight.

TSMC's monthly CoWoS capacity could reach 120,000–140,000 wafers this year, up from roughly 35,000 in late 2024. HBM sales are forecast at $54.6 billion in 2026, up 58% year over year, and data centers are projected to consume about 70% of memory chips produced this year.

4/
The shortage has migrated to the less glamorous layer.

Power management IC lead times are now 35–40 weeks. Baseboard management controllers are at 21–26 weeks. TrendForce cut its 2026 general server shipment forecast from ~20% growth to ~13% because suppliers are prioritizing higher-margin AI server components.

5/
These parts are made on mature nodes—often 8-inch wafers using BCD processes—that have not attracted the investment showered on leading-edge logic.

Global 8-inch wafer foundry capacity is expected to contract by about 2.4% in 2026. Demand is rising into a shrinking supply base.

6/
Memory is part of the same reallocation. Data centers are projected to consume 70% of memory chips produced this year. HBM alone takes 23% of DRAM wafer starts.

Every wafer steered toward AI is one removed from automotive, industrial, and consumer electronics.

7/
Chips are fast. Grids are slow.

AI data center projects entering service in PJM territory in 2025 took more than seven years from start to operation. Generator step-up transformer lead times exceeded 160 weeks in early 2026. Wood Mackenzie projects the US data center electrical equipment market will grow from roughly $20 billion this year to $65 billion by 2030.

8/
Suppliers are responding, but with long-cycle capex.

Infineon raised fiscal 2026 capex by €500 million to €2.7 billion, targeting power semiconductors for AI data centers. It expects €1.5 billion in AI data-center revenue this year and €2.5 billion next. Gartner now calls Infineon the company to beat in the segment.

9/
The physical content is old; the demand scale is new.

BloombergNEF projects AI-powered data centers will consume about 400,000 tonnes of copper a year over the next decade, peaking at 572,000 tonnes in 2028. Microsoft's 2009 Chicago facility used roughly 27 tonnes of copper per megawatt—a benchmark that still applies.

10/
This is not a conventional chip shortage. It is a capital-cycle mismatch.

Hyperscalers order silicon on quarterly cadences. Transformers, substations, transmission lines, and 8-inch fabs need half a decade or more. The result is a shortage that cannot be solved by expanding a single link.

11/
The allocation is zero-sum.

AI servers, general-purpose servers, automotive, and industrial power electronics are drawing from the same mature-node pool. The spread of the shortage into non-AI sectors is the variable to watch.

12/
What remains unresolved is whether the physical layer can keep believing in the software layer.

Hyperscalers can raise capex. Grids, foundries, and copper supply cannot accelerate on the same schedule.
