## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: Two Metals, Two Worlds
- **Central insight:** Copper and gold have diverged in 2026: copper is up double digits while gold has corrected from its January record. One is answering questions about physical supply; the other is answering questions about money, debt, and geopolitical hedging.
- **Data to include:**
  - Gold: ~$4,010/oz as of 20 Jul 2026, down from $5,501.70/oz intraday high on 28 Jan 2026; roughly -8.6% YTD; central bank buying 244t in Q1 2026, full-year forecast 700–900t (World Gold Council, LBMA, Fortune).
  - Copper: LME copper at $13,895/tonne on 22 Jul 2026, up 10.5% YTD; copper concentrate TC index at -$102.70/tonne and 2026 benchmark at $0 (Fastmarkets, Étude); Grasberg -35% vs. pre-incident guidance, Kamoa-Kakula Q1 copper-in-concentrate output -54% YoY, Codelco March output -9.98% YoY (Fastmarkets, MINING.COM / Cochilco).
- **Visualization type:** Split-screen comparison / diverging flow chart with two price paths.
- **Composition notes:** Top: two indexed price paths from early 2026 to July 2026 — copper rising +10.5% and gold falling roughly -8.6% from its January peak. Left lane in gold tones labeled "What gold prices" with arrows flowing from central banks, fiscal deficits, geopolitical hedging, and safe-haven demand into a gold bar. Right lane in copper tones labeled "What copper prices" with arrows flowing from mine disruptions, negative smelter charges, concentrate shortages, and electrification demand into a copper coil. A bold headline across the top: "Same year, opposite directions. They are not the same trade." The payoff is the center gutter where the two lanes meet — the reader sees that the only thing these metals share is a price axis.
- **Sources:** World Gold Council (29 Apr 2026); LBMA (1 Jul 2026); Fortune (20 Jul 2026); Westmetall (22 Jul 2026); Fastmarkets (20 Apr 2026); Étude (20 Jun 2026); MINING.COM (18 Nov 2025, 12 May 2026); Cochilco via MINING.COM.

### Concept 2: Gold's Sovereign Bid
- **Central insight:** Central banks are buying gold at roughly twice the pace of the 2010–2021 average, creating a price-insensitive structural floor.
- **Data to include:**
  - 2010–2021 annual average: 473 tonnes/year.
  - 2025: 863.3 tonnes.
  - Q1 2026: 244 tonnes (up 3% YoY, 17% QoQ).
  - 2026 forecast: 700–900 tonnes.
- **Visualization type:** Bar chart / timeline hybrid.
- **Composition notes:** A horizontal timeline from 2010 to 2026 with bars for annual central bank purchases. The 2010–2021 bars cluster low; 2022–2025 jump to a higher plateau; Q1 2026 is highlighted as an inset bar at the right. A dotted line marks the 473t historical average. The reader sees the regime shift first, then reads the specific numbers. Annotation flags Poland (31t), Uzbekistan (25t), and PBOC (7t) as Q1's largest reported buyers.
- **Sources:** World Gold Council, "Gold Demand Trends: Full Year 2025 — Central Banks" (29 Jan 2026); World Gold Council, "Gold Demand Trends Q1 2026 — Central Banks" (29 Apr 2026); World Gold Council, "Gold Demand Trends Q1 2026 — Outlook" (29 Apr 2026).

### Concept 3: Smelters Now Pay to Process Ore
- **Central insight:** Copper concentrate shortages are so severe that smelters are paying miners for the right to turn ore into metal — the 2026 benchmark fee hit zero for the first time on record.
- **Data to include:**
  - 2026 annual TC/RC benchmark: $0/tonne (lowest on record).
  - Fastmarkets weekly copper concentrates TC index: -$102.70/tonne on 10 Apr 2026.
- **Visualization type:** Diverging bar / "below zero" chart.
- **Composition notes:** A single vertical axis showing treatment charges over recent years, with positive bars in the past and the 2026 bar dropping below the zero line into negative territory. The negative bar is colored alarm red. A callout explains: "Smelters used to charge miners to process ore. Now they pay for the privilege." The payoff is the conceptual flip — the price of turning copper into metal has gone negative, which is the market's way of screaming that concentrate is scarce.
- **Sources:** Fastmarkets, "Kamoa-Kakula Q1 copper concentrate output falls 54% YOY" (20 Apr 2026); MINING.COM, "Antofagasta agrees spot-indexed copper ore sales with some Chinese smelters" (1 Jul 2026); Étude, "Copper in 2026: Record Highs, a Zero-Dollar Smelting Fee, and the Metal Powering Electrification" (20 Jun 2026).

### Concept 4: The Forecast That Swung 246,000 Tonnes
- **Central insight:** In six months, the International Copper Study Group flipped its 2026 copper balance forecast from a 150,000-tonne deficit to a 96,000-tonne surplus — but not all forecasters agree; J.P. Morgan and Morgan Stanley still project deficits.
- **Data to include:**
  - Oct 2025 ICSG forecast: 150,000-tonne deficit for 2026; demand growth 2.1%; mine output growth 2.3%.
  - Apr 2026 ICSG forecast: 96,000-tonne surplus for 2026; demand growth 1.6%; mine output growth 1.6%.
  - Net swing: 246,000 tonnes.
  - 2027 forecast: 377,000-tonne surplus.
  - Counterpoint: J.P. Morgan forecasts a 330,000-tonne deficit; Morgan Stanley forecasts a 600,000-tonne deficit.
- **Visualization type:** Before/after balance scale or diverging arrow chart with a dissenting-callout panel.
- **Composition notes:** A simple balance scale with "Oct 2025" on the left tipping toward deficit (-150k) and "Apr 2026" on the right tipping toward surplus (+96k). The 246k swing is labeled as a bold arrow between them. Below, a smaller bar shows the 2027 surplus (+377k). Add a separate callout box: "Not consensus: J.P. Morgan and Morgan Stanley still forecast 2026 deficits." The reader sees that copper's narrative flipped within a single forecast cycle, but the forecast disagreement is the real signal.
- **Sources:** International Copper Study Group, "Copper Market Forecast 2025/2026" press release (8 Oct 2025); Mining Weekly, "Global refined copper market to swing to surplus in 2026, group says" (23 Apr 2026); Crux Investor / Mining Visuals (J.P. Morgan and Morgan Stanley forecasts).

### Concept 5: The $690 Tariff Gap
- **Central insight:** U.S. Section 232 copper tariffs created a temporary but massive premium that disconnected COMEX copper from LME copper.
- **Data to include:**
  - U.S. Section 232 copper tariff: 50% ad valorem, effective 8 Jun 2026.
  - COMEX–LME copper spread: widened above $400/tonne after April 2026; peaked near $690/tonne in early June 2026; narrowed to ~$200/tonne by late June as arbitrage cargoes arrived.
- **Visualization type:** Timeline / area chart.
- **Composition notes:** A line chart from April to July 2026 showing two copper prices — LME cash and COMEX — diverging, then converging. The gap between them is shaded and peaks at $690/tonne in early June; label that explicitly as the peak rather than the current gap. A vertical marker at 8 June 2026 labels the 50% tariff effective date. A second annotation at late June explains the narrowing to ~$200/tonne: "Arbitrage cargoes arrived." The payoff is showing that copper pricing has been partly a tariff-expectation trade, not just a fundamentals trade.
- **Sources:** The White House, "Further Adjusting the Tariff Regimes for Imports of Aluminum, Steel, and Copper into the United States" (1 Jun 2026); Shanghai Metals Market, "[SMM Analysis] Section 232 Copper Investigation: What June 30 Could Mean for Global Copper Markets" (2 Jul 2026).

## What to avoid

- **Avoid a simple "copper vs. gold price performance" chart that only shows both went up.** That confirms the consensus the signal is trying to challenge. The insight is divergence in *drivers*, not convergence in price.
- **Avoid forecasting the 2026 copper balance as a settled fact.** The research shows ICSG/Goldman see surplus while J.P. Morgan and Morgan Stanley see deficit. Present the disagreement, not a single answer.
- **Avoid implying the COMEX–LME gap is still $690/tonne.** It peaked there in early June and had narrowed to ~$200/tonne by late June. Timeframe matters.
- **Avoid conflating "central banks bought 244 tonnes in Q1" with a guaranteed full-year 700–900 tonnes.** The Q1 number is reported; the full-year number is a WGC forecast. Label each accordingly.
- **Avoid treating Grasberg and Kamoa-Kakula disruptions as current ongoing events without dates.** Grasberg large-scale restart was planned for Q2 2026; Kamoa-Kakula data is Q1 2026. The reader needs to know the data is not all live July production.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with Concepts 1, 2, 3, and 5. Concept 1 is the priority — it directly answers the operator note to surface the divergence visually. Concepts 2 and 3 give the infographic strong, source-backed "did you know" moments. Concept 5 adds the policy-distortion angle that is timely and distinct. Concept 4 is solid but more abstract; use it if a fifth panel is needed or swap it in if the final design favors forecast uncertainty over tariff arbitrage.
