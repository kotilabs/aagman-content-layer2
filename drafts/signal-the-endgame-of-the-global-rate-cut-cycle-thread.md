1/ The cutting stopped at 3.50–3.75%.

After the post-inflation easing cycle of 2024–25, the Fed is parked at 3.50–3.75%. The June FOMC left the range unchanged. The question is no longer how low rates will go, but what a "terminal" rate at this level does to a world carrying far more debt than it did a decade ago.

2/ Central banks are no longer easing into recovery. They are holding above the 2010s floor.

The ECB raised its deposit facility rate in June 2026 — its first hike since September 2023. The Bank of Japan lifted its policy rate to 1.00% on June 16. The Fed's median dot plot for end-2026 is 3.8%. The direction is broadly tighter, not lower.

3/ Inflation is the reason they cannot declare victory.

US CPI is 4.2% year-over-year; core CPI is 2.9%. The Fed's own 2026 PCE inflation projection sits at 3.6%. The IMF sees global headline inflation at 4.7% this year and 3.9% next. The Fed is unlikely to ease while projecting an inflation overshoot.

4/ The dot plot has moved up, not down.

Between March and June 2026, the Fed raised its 2026 projection by 40 basis points, from 3.4% to 3.8%. The longer-run estimate is now 3.1%, materially above the 2010s assumption that neutral was close to zero.

5/ This is the base-case interpretation, not a certainty.

Projections can shift. A recession or financial accident could reprime rate-cut pricing. But for now, the revealed stance is hold-and-project-higher. Markets are being asked to price a world where the terminal rate is no longer a theoretical floor.

6/ This isn't a pause before the next cut. It is a structurally constrained policy space.

The "terminal" rate is high enough to enforce valuation discipline, but not high enough to deflate the debt accumulated over the last decade.

7/ The policy rate anchors a higher hurdle for assets, though the Treasury curve does the actual discounting.

A sustained 3.5–4.0% rate changes the arithmetic for equities, real estate, and long-duration credit. Cash flows that looked adequate under zero rates now have to clear a higher bar.

8/ The debt stock, not just the flow, now constrains policy choices.

Gross global debt is projected to reach 100% of GDP by 2029. Global government interest payments are near 3% of global GDP. Rates are too low to force rapid deleveraging, but too high to allow the aggressive stimulus that markets grew used to in the 2010s.

9/ There is a real-rate counterargument worth naming.

With headline CPI at 4.2%, the ex-post real Fed funds rate is roughly -0.5% to -0.7%. Even against core CPI at 2.9%, it is only about +0.6% to +0.85%. Nominal rates look high only compared with the post-GFC zero-bound regime, not against history.

10/ The strongest case against the thesis is also its source of risk.

If inflation proves temporary and growth cracks, the Fed can still cut. That is why the dot plot's longer-run estimate is 3.1%, not 4.5%. The neutral rate is unobservable, inferred only with lag, and it shifts.

11/ Growth, inflation, or debt service will determine the next move — but the timing is unknown.

All three are rising as shares of GDP, policy attention, and market narrative. The cutting cycle is over for now. What replaces it depends on which of those three breaks first.
