1/ The global monetary anchor fractured in mid-June. The Fed, ECB and RBA no longer agree on what comes next.

2/ The Fed held at 3.50%–3.75% but its median 2026 dot jumped to 3.8% from 3.4% in March. Nine of eighteen members now project at least one hike.

3/ The ECB hiked 25bp into a growth downgrade — its first increase since September 2023. Lagarde refused to call it an "insurance hike."

4/ The RBA paused at 4.35% after three rapid hikes, keeping a conditional hiking bias while admitting the economy is slowing.

5/ India moved first. The RBI met on 3–5 Jun and left the repo rate at 5.25% — before the ECB (11 Jun), RBA (16 Jun) or Fed (17 Jun) decided. No hike, no cut, no hawkish tilt.

6/ Instead, the RBI opened the capital-account toolbox: FAR expansion, higher NRI/OCI/PROI equity limits, concessional forex swaps for PSU ECBs, full hedging on fresh FCNR(B) deposits, and restored export-proceeds realisation.

7/ The government joined in — exempting foreign investors from income tax on G-Sec interest and capital gains, retrospectively from April.

8/ This isn't a central bank waiting for the Fed. It's an attempt to separate domestic rate-setting from external-sector management.

9/ The rupee rallied from near 97 to 94.39. FPIs put ₹35,000 crore into Indian bonds within weeks. For now, the toolbox is working.

10/ But the underlying bet is supply-side: that the inflation spike — fuel pass-through, monsoon risk, energy prices — will fade without choking growth.

11/ The RBI projects Q3 CPI at 5.9%, just ten basis points below the 6% tolerance ceiling. There is little room for error.

12/ Meanwhile the Fed's dots and the dollar's path are external variables India cannot control. Capital-account measures are conditional on global risk appetite — and they are not costless: the RBI absorbs the hedging cost and takes currency risk onto its balance sheet.

13/ If oil stays near $73 and the FCNR(B) window attracts analyst estimates of $40–50 billion, the RBI may have threaded the needle — though actual mobilisation depends on spreads and risk appetite.

14/ If the Fed hikes and oil re-spikes, the RBI will face the old choice — defend the rupee with rates, or let it adjust.

15/ The divergence is real. Whether capital-account engineering can replace rate defence under pressure is the experiment to watch.
