## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: The Ownership Crossover
- **Central insight:** For the first time in at least 14 years, domestic institutional investors own a larger share of NSE-listed companies than foreign portfolio investors.
- **Data:** FPI ownership **15.88%** (Q4 FY26, 14-year low); DII ownership **18.92%** (record high); the gap flipped from **DII +0.45 pp** over FPI a year earlier to **DII +3.04 pp** now.
- **Visualization type:** Divergence / slope chart or paired horizontal bars.
- **Composition notes:** Open with the two ownership shares side by side as large numbers. Show a thin horizontal line or paired bars for the prior year (DII 17.6% / FPI 17.1%) and the current quarter (DII 18.92% / FPI 15.88%) so the crossover is immediately visible. Payoff: the arrow from +0.45 pp to +3.04 pp in one year.
- **Sources:** Prime Database / Capitaline, May 2026; corroborated by NSE Market Pulse (15.8% FPI, FY26) and JM Financial (14.7% FPI, April 2026).

### Concept 2: Foreign Exodus vs. Domestic Sponge
- **Central insight:** In FY26, domestic institutions bought roughly six times the value of equities that foreigners sold, absorbing the outflow and more.
- **Data:** Net FPI outflow **–US$16.5 billion** in FY26 (RBI); DII buying **~US$95.8 billion** in FY26 (NSE Market Pulse).
- **Visualization type:** Opposing flow / arrow diagram.
- **Composition notes:** Two large arrows pointing at a central "Indian equities" node. The red outward arrow is labeled FPI selling (–$16.5 bn); the green inward arrow is much larger, labeled DII buying (+$95.8 bn). A small callout notes net domestic addition of ~$79 bn. The eye follows the size asymmetry first; the payoff is that locals did not merely offset foreigners — they overwhelmed them.
- **Sources:** RBI Annual Report 2025-26 via PSU Watch, 29 May 2026; NSE Market Pulse via Angel One, 28 May 2026.

### Concept 3: The Calm in the Storm
- **Central insight:** Despite record foreign selling, fear in the options market fell to a five-month low by early July 2026.
- **Data:** India VIX closed at **11.65** on 7 July 2026, near the bottom of its 52-week range (**8.72–28.91**); FPIs had already pulled **>₹2.87 lakh crore** in H1 2026; VIX spiked to **~14.68–15.16** the next day on renewed US-Iran tensions.
- **Visualization type:** Dual-layer timeline — VIX line chart with FPI outflow bars overlaid.
- **Composition notes:** Top half shows India VIX from January to July 2026, with the March/April spike (~28.9) and the subsequent collapse to 11.65. Bottom half shows monthly FPI outflow bars in red. The reader first sees the volatility collapse, then notices it happened while foreigners were still selling. A small inset warns that the calm broke on 8 July (+26% spike).
- **Sources:** Yahoo Finance historical data; HDFC SKY, 8 July 2026; NSDL via Upstox, 14 June 2026.

### Concept 4: SIPs Are Broadening? Or Narrowing?
- **Central insight:** SIP rupee contributions stayed near record highs even as the account-level stoppage ratio crossed 100% — the money pipe is thick, but the user base may be narrowing.
- **Data:** SIP contributions **₹32,087 cr** (Mar 2026 record), **₹31,115 cr** (Apr), **₹30,954 cr** (May); SIP stoppage ratio **>100%** in March and April 2026.
- **Visualization type:** Dual-axis line chart or split-panel comparison.
- **Composition notes:** Top panel: monthly SIP rupee inflows as a green area chart, anchored by the ₹30,000-crore line. Bottom panel: the stoppage ratio line crossing the 100% threshold in March–April. A shaded "warning zone" highlights the divergence. Payoff: the same retail machine looks strong in rupees but fragile in account count.
- **Sources:** AMFI via Zee Business / Upstox, 10–11 June 2026; AMFI via Finnovate, 1 June 2026.

### Concept 5: India’s Disappearing MSCI EM Weight
- **Central insight:** Foreign funds have cut India so aggressively that it is now the biggest underweight in emerging-market portfolios.
- **Data:** India MSCI EM neutral weight fell to **15.25%** (two-year low); **~71%** of large EM funds underweight India (as cited, end-July 2026); India returned **+5%** in 2025 vs MSCI EM **+25%** and MSCI China **+35%**.
- **Visualization type:** Bar race or small-multiples comparison.
- **Composition notes:** Three vertical bars for 2025 returns (China tallest, EM middle, India shortest) sit above a timeline of India’s MSCI EM weight dropping to 15.25%. A callout notes 71% underweight positioning (as cited, end-July 2026). Payoff: the underweight is extreme, which is either a warning or a contrarian setup.
- **Sources:** HSBC via Multibagg.ai, 13 June 2026.

## What to avoid

- **Monthly FPI flow chart without context.** The February 2026 inflow (+₹22,615 crore, highest in 17 months) is a critical exception; a simple "every month was red" bar chart would be misleading.
- **"DII > FPI" ownership claim without caveats.** Different sources use different universes (NSE-listed, Nifty 50, JM Financial coverage). Always cite the specific measure and date.
- **VIX calm without the 8 July 2026 spike.** The 11.65 close was immediately followed by a 26% one-day jump; omitting this makes the calm look more durable than it is.
- **"SIP collapse" framing.** The stoppage ratio >100% is account-level, not rupee-level. Do not imply SIP money dried up — it stayed above ₹30,000 crore.
- **2013 taper tantrum analogy as a standalone infographic.** Useful for perspective, but the artifact lacks a clean comparable dataset (exact ownership shares, VIX, flows) across both episodes.
- **Rupee or forex-reserves chart in isolation.** The ₹95/USD and US$691 bn reserve numbers need the current-account-deficit / BoP context to avoid misreading them as a crisis.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with **Concepts 1, 2, 3, and 4**. Concept 1 is the strongest single visual story because it captures the structural ownership crossover in one glance. Concepts 2 and 3 add the flow and volatility dimensions that explain *how* the crossover happened. Concept 4 surfaces the key tension in the domestic-flow narrative. Concept 5 is viable if the designer wants an external / positioning angle, but it is slightly less central to the domestic-flow thesis and should be deprioritized unless space allows.
