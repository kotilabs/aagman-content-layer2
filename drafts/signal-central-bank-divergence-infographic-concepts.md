## Recommended concepts

### Concept 1: Three Banks, One Week, No Consensus
- **Central insight:** In six days, the world’s three large convertible-currency central banks delivered three incompatible postures — the ECB tightening into weakness, the Fed holding but repricing hawkish, and the RBA pausing after a rapid re-tightening.
- **Data to include:**
  - ECB: +25bp on 11 June; deposit rate 2.25%; 2026 GDP forecast cut to 0.8%; inflation forecast raised to 3.0%.
  - Fed: hold at 3.50%–3.75% on 17 June; median 2026 dot 3.8% (up from 3.4%); 9 of 18 participants project ≥1 hike.
  - RBA: hold at 4.35% on 16 June after Feb/Mar/May hikes; statement retains "further if required" hiking bias.
- **Visualization type:** Three-column comparison chart with a small calendar strip across the top (11 → 16 → 17 June).
- **Composition notes:** Reader sees the calendar first, then three equal-width panels colour-coded by bank. Each panel shows the actual decision, the forward signal, and one contradictory macro fact (ECB: hike + GDP cut; Fed: hold + hawkish dot; RBA: pause + retained hiking bias). The payoff is the absence of a single global anchor.
- **Sources:** Federal Reserve FOMC statement and SEP, 17 June 2026; ECB monetary policy decisions, 11 June 2026; RBA monetary policy decision, 16 June 2026.

### Concept 2: The 40-Basis-Point Dot
- **Central insight:** The Fed held rates but its own projections moved more in one meeting than many actual hikes do — the median 2026 dot jumped 40bp, and half the committee now sees a hike.
- **Data to include:**
  - March 2026 median 2026 fed funds rate: 3.4%.
  - June 2026 median 2026 fed funds rate: 3.8%.
  - March distribution: 0 participants projected hikes; the bulk sat at 3.38%–3.62%.
  - June distribution: 9 of 18 at ≥3.875%; 6 at ≥4.125%; 1 participant projected a cut.
- **Visualization type:** Before/after dot-plot histogram or slope chart showing the distribution shift from March to June.
- **Composition notes:** A split screen: left side labelled "March" with a tight cluster around 3.4%, right side labelled "June" with the same participants redistributed toward higher rates. A bold connecting line highlights the 40bp median move. The payoff is that the Fed did not hike, yet the committee moved sharply toward hiking.
- **Sources:** Federal Reserve, Summary of Economic Projections, 17 June 2026.

### Concept 3: Hiking Into a Downgrade
- **Central insight:** The ECB raised rates for the first time since 2023 while simultaneously cutting its growth forecast — a tension that is easy to state and hard to sustain.
- **Data to include:**
  - ECB deposit facility rate: 2.25% (+25bp), effective 17 June 2026.
  - ECB 2026 GDP forecast: 0.8% (down from 1.0% in March).
  - ECB 2026 headline inflation forecast: 3.0% (up from 2.6% in March).
  - Euro area HICP: 3.2% y/y in May; services inflation 3.5% y/y (up from 3.0% in April).
  - Lagarde quote option: decision "robust across all scenarios" and not an "insurance hike."
- **Visualization type:** Diverging arrows or a simple two-axis chart — policy rate rising, GDP forecast falling, inflation forecast rising.
- **Composition notes:** A central label reads "11 June 2026 ECB decision." Three arrows radiate: one up for the deposit rate, one down for the GDP forecast, one up for inflation. A small inset shows services inflation accelerating. The payoff is the stagflationary trade-off the ECB chose.
- **Sources:** ECB monetary policy decisions and press conference transcript, 11 June 2026.

### Concept 4: Oil Falls, Inflation Forecasts Rise
- **Central insight:** While Brent crude collapsed from its April peak toward $73 on the Iran deal, the Fed raised its 2026 inflation forecast by 90bp — the market was pricing two different time horizons at once.
- **Data to include:**
  - Brent crude: ~$140/bbl in April 2026 → $72.95/bbl on 26 June 2026.
  - Fed 2026 PCE inflation forecast: 2.7% (March) → 3.6% (June).
  - Fed 2026 core PCE inflation forecast: 2.7% (March) → 3.3% (June).
  - U.S.–Iran 14-point MoU signed 18 June 2026; Hormuz reopening within 30 days.
- **Visualization type:** Dual-axis line chart or two opposing trend arrows.
- **Composition notes:** Left side shows oil price plunging; right side shows the Fed inflation forecast stepping up. A thin vertical line marks the 18 June Iran MoU. The headline sits in the middle: "Two timelines, one market." The payoff is the tension between spot disinflation from oil and the Fed’s lagged forecast revision.
- **Sources:** Trading Economics, Brent crude, 27 June 2026; Federal Reserve SEP, 17 June 2026; Anadolu Agency, 16 June 2026.

### Concept 5: The Curve’s Twist
- **Central insight:** The Treasury curve flattened for the right reason — front-end yields rose on Fed hawkishness while long-end yields fell on oil relief, producing a policy shock rather than a growth shock.
- **Data to include:**
  - 2-year Treasury yield: rose ~10bp.
  - 30-year Treasury yield: fell ~7bp.
  - 2s30s spread: compressed ~17bp (week ending 19 June 2026).
- **Visualization type:** Simplified Treasury yield curve with two snapshots (pre- and post-FOMC) showing the twist.
- **Composition notes:** Two curves on one set of axes — a steeper pre-FOMC curve and a flatter post-FOMC curve with the front end higher and the long end lower. A small annotation explains the two drivers. The payoff is that flattening does not always mean recession.
- **Sources:** Mariemont Capital, "June 2026 FOMC Meeting," 22 June 2026. *Note: this concept depends on a single market-source estimate; verify against Treasury/FRED data before final design.*

## What to avoid

- **A generic "central banks are diverging" world map.** The signal is not geographic; it is about the specific postures of three banks in one week. A map adds decoration, not insight.
- **The EUR/USD "fell below 1.15" claim as a standalone number.** Market reports differ on whether the pair breached 1.15 or merely stayed near it after the ECB hike. Without a single authoritative price source, this is better used as a qualitative mechanism (currencies price the expected path, not the current change) than as a headline figure.
- **A 1970s stagflation visual.** The research artifact treats this as a competing interpretation that Lagarde explicitly rejects, not as a verified fact. Historical analogy should remain a brief footnote, not the central visual.
- **Any claim that the Fed "will" hike in 2026.** The dot plot is a distribution of individual assessments, not a commitment. The correct framing is that the median now implies a possible hike, not a forecast.

## Final recommendation

Proceed with Concepts 1, 2, 3 and 4. They rest on verified central-bank data and each captures a distinct dimension of the divergence: the three-way split, the Fed’s projection shift, the ECB’s growth-inflation tension, and the oil-rates time mismatch.

Concept 5 is structurally interesting but should be held until the Treasury yield data can be cross-checked against Federal Reserve H.15 or Treasury par-yield series; the 17bp compression figure currently rests on a single sell-side note.
