# China's Deflation Trap: Stimulus Without Demand

## Beijing is spending aggressively, yet households are still repairing balance sheets. The gap between supply-side support and domestic demand is the signal to watch.

The signal is not the size of the stimulus. It is that the stimulus is flowing to factories, local governments, and balance sheets while household demand remains flat.

> **Editor's note — July 2026:** This article is based on 2025 and early-2026 data and treats the period as a retrospective on China's deflation trap. Since then, the picture has stabilized: in June 2026 headline CPI was +1.0% year-on-year, core CPI was +1.0% YoY, PPI was +4.1% YoY, and first-tier new-home prices rose month-on-month for the fourth consecutive month, with 20 of 70 large and medium-sized cities reporting MoM gains. The rebound partly reflects low base effects and commodity-price swings, and demand-sensitive components remain moderate. The question is whether the recovery is durable or driven by base effects and policy front-loading.

Imagine a restaurant owner who notices the dining room emptying out. Instead of asking why his regulars stopped coming, he buys a bigger oven, renovates the kitchen, and asks the bank for a cheaper loan on the equipment. The kitchen is now world-class. The suppliers are happy. But the customers — many of whom just saw the value of their homes fall — are eating at home. The owner has improved his capacity to serve meals he is not yet selling. That is roughly where China found itself through 2025 and into early 2026. The policy machinery was running hot; the household demand engine was not.

A deflation trap, in this context, means stimulus large enough to hit growth targets but not large enough to lift prices because households are still repairing balance sheets.

## Context

China closed 2025 with a GDP growth rate of 5.0%, exactly the official target, and a national output of RMB 140.2 trillion. On the surface, the economy performed as planned. Retail sales rose 3.7%, industrial production grew 5.9%, and the urban surveyed unemployment rate averaged a stable 5.2%. The government targeted a deficit-to-GDP ratio of around 4%, issued RMB 4.4 trillion of local-government special-purpose bonds, and allocated RMB 1.3 trillion of ultra-long special treasury bonds. Monetary policy shifted to "moderately loose," with rate cuts and reserve-requirement reductions.

Yet consumer prices tell a different story. Full-year 2025 CPI came in at 0.0%, well below the official target of around 2%. Producer prices fell 2.6% for the year, marking the third consecutive year of decline. New-home prices in 70 large and medium-sized cities fell 0.4% month-on-month and 2.7% year-on-year in December, the steepest annual drop in five months. Only 6 of those 70 cities recorded price gains. The economy met its growth target, but it did so while domestic demand remained soft and the property sector continued to deflate.

What changed is the composition of growth and the composition of stimulus. Beijing is clearly trying. It doubled central funding for consumer-goods trade-ins to RMB 300 billion, which the State Council said was fully disbursed by the end of September 2025 and generated more than RMB 2 trillion in related sales between January and August. The central bank acknowledged in its fourth-quarter report that "challenges such as strong supply and weak demand persisted." The signal is not that China is doing nothing. It is that what China is doing is not yet reaching the part of the economy that sets prices: household demand.

## Why isn't China's stimulus creating inflation?

The short answer is that most of it is flowing through supply-side and balance-sheet channels rather than into household income. The mechanism is where the gap becomes visible.

## Mechanism

The mechanism is a mismatch between supply-side stimulus and demand-side balance-sheet repair. Understanding it requires looking at where the money goes, not just how much of it there is.

Most of the 2025 stimulus flowed through channels that support production, local-government solvency, and bank balance sheets: equipment upgrades, manufacturing credit, technology and green-energy lending, local-debt swaps, and bank recapitalisation. These measures keep factories open, local governments solvent, and financial-system refinancing risk contained. They do not, however, put durable purchasing power into households' hands.

Household demand is being held back by wealth loss and precautionary behaviour. Urban Chinese households hold close to 70% of their wealth in housing, according to research from Stanford's Center on China's Economy and Institutions. That same research estimates that a 10% fall in home prices cuts household consumption by roughly 1.5–2.3%. Applied to observed price declines of 20–40%, the implied consumption loss is 2–4% of GDP. With new-home prices still falling and existing-home prices falling faster across city tiers, households feel poorer. They spend less, borrow less, and save more. Real-estate investment fell 17.2% in 2025. New commercial-building sales by area fell 8.7% and by value fell 12.6%. Land-sales revenue, a key funding source for local governments, remains under pressure.

The result is a feedback loop. Falling property prices reduce household wealth and confidence. Cautious households spend less. Weaker consumption reduces corporate pricing power. Firms cut investment and hiring. Lower investment deepens the property drag, which further weakens confidence. The PBOC's own data shows that interest rates on new personal housing loans were around 3.1% in December 2025, a historically low level. Credit supply is ample and cheap; credit demand is not.

The inflation data confirms the demand gap. The December 2025 headline CPI ticked up to 0.8% year-on-year, but the move was driven by temporary food-supply factors: fresh vegetable prices rose 18.2% year-on-year, partly on weather-related supply disruptions. Core CPI, which excludes food and energy, was only 1.2% in December and 0.7% for the full year. Non-food prices, rents, and transport prices remained soft. Producer prices fell 1.9% in December, extending three years of factory-gate deflation. The economy is producing; it is not pricing.

## Signal / Deviation

The deviation is straightforward: China is running stimulus at a scale that would normally generate inflationary pressure, yet prices are flat or falling. M2 grew 8.5% year-on-year at the end of 2025. Aggregate financing to the real economy grew 8.3%. Both expanded faster than the official nominal-GDP growth implied by the 5.0% real-GDP target and near-zero GDP deflator.

The point is not that China is out of money. It is that the money is not going where prices are set. Liquidity is not the binding constraint; household demand is.

What is unusual is where the stimulus lands and where it does not. The RMB 300 billion trade-in program is the largest demand-side measure, but it is still a goods subsidy rather than income support. It encourages households to replace old appliances and cars, which is useful, but it does not repair balance sheets or durably raise household income. Meanwhile, the property sector — the single largest source of household wealth and a historically dominant share of economic activity — continues to contract. At its peak in 2019, real estate accounted for nearly a third of China's economic activity.

The external account adds another dimension. China recorded a record trade surplus in 2025, with exports rising roughly 5.5% to about USD 3.77 trillion and imports roughly flat at about USD 2.58 trillion. Auto exports surged 21%, and full-electric vehicle exports jumped 67% to 1.65 million units. The surplus is partly a sign of competitiveness; it is also a symptom of weak domestic absorption. Chinese manufacturers are selling abroad because domestic demand is not absorbing what domestic supply can produce.

## Interpretation Spectrum

There are at least four plausible readings of this signal, and they are not mutually exclusive.

**Interpretation A: A managed transition, not a trap.** This is the view closest to official Chinese statements. GDP hit 5%, retail sales rose, industrial production was solid, high-tech manufacturing and EV exports are strong, and PPI declines narrowed late in the year. On this reading, China is engineering a deliberate rotation away from property and toward advanced manufacturing and technology. The deflationary pressure is transitional noise.

The weakness in this view is that it confuses output with price stability. Hitting a GDP target through investment and exports does not mean the household balance-sheet problem has been solved. A country can grow at 5% with 0% CPI for a long time if the growth is concentrated in supply-side sectors while households remain cautious. Growth and deflation can coexist.

**Interpretation B: A balance-sheet recession.** This reading, associated with economist Richard Koo and researchers at Stanford, treats the property slump as the dominant force. Households are deleveraging after a historic wealth shock. Private investment fell 6.4% in 2025. Core CPI and PPI are weak. Fiscal policy is not primarily aimed at household income. The analogy to Japan after 1991 is structural, not anecdotal.

The counterargument is that China is not Japan. Its banking system is state-controlled, allowing forbearance and controlled debt restructuring rather than a sudden systemic crisis. Capital controls limit capital flight. The government retains more policy control than Japan's authorities did in the 1990s. Exports and manufacturing are providing an offset that Japan lacked at the time.

**Interpretation C: Structural overcapacity and export of deflation.** This view focuses on the supply side. PPI has fallen for three years. Steel and EV exports are surging despite tariffs. Manufacturing investment is strong while domestic prices are weak. The risk, from this angle, is that China transmits deflationary pressure to global manufacturing margins, provoking further trade barriers.

The weakness here is that it treats a demand shortfall as a supply problem. Closing the demand gap would absorb much of the perceived overcapacity without needing to export it. Not all export strength is dumping; some of it reflects genuine competitiveness in EVs and clean technology.

**Interpretation D: A deliberate policy choice to favour supply over consumption.** Some political-economy observers argue that Beijing prefers industrial and technology targets over household consumption for strategic reasons. The 2025 policy mix — equipment upgrades, local debt swaps, bank recapitalisation, tech and green lending — supports this reading. Direct household income support remains small relative to GDP. The gap between the rhetoric and the spending is where the signal lives.

The counterargument is that official rhetoric repeatedly stresses expanding domestic demand, and the trade-in program was doubled. The PBOC also created RMB 500 billion of central-bank lending for service consumption and elderly care. Even if the strategic preference is real, the scale of the property wealth shock may eventually force a larger consumption-led response for social-stability reasons.

## Historical Memory

Two historical episodes are useful for perspective, not prediction.

Japan after 1991 offers the most frequently cited parallel: a post-bubble property downturn, negative wealth effects, investment overhang, and a prolonged period of weak domestic demand. The lesson is that even without a banking crisis, the real-economy drag from property wealth loss and pessimistic sentiment can last many years. China has tools Japan did not — state-controlled banks, capital controls, and a still-competitive export sector — but the underlying feedback loop is similar.

China's own 1998–2002 deflation is the domestic precedent. Beijing responded with infrastructure spending, monetary easing, and export growth following WTO accession. The difference today is the starting condition: China is far more indebted, demographically older, and operating in a less friendly external trade environment. The playbook exists, but the field has changed.

## Implications for Decision-Makers

What this affects is how one reads Chinese macro data and, by extension, the second-order effects on global markets. Readers tracking this alongside [Japan's carry-trade tipping point](/japan-yen-carry-trade-tipping-point-2026) and the [AI power-semiconductor wall](/ai-data-center-power-semiconductor-wall-2026) will notice a common thread: the location of the binding constraint is often not where the headline story sits. A 5% GDP print in China no longer implies healthy domestic demand. Flat CPI and falling PPI suggest that monetary and fiscal easing is being absorbed by balance-sheet repair rather than translating into price pressure. For investors and analysts, the composition of Chinese growth now matters more than the headline.

The signal also matters for global manufacturing and trade. A China that cannot absorb its own industrial output will continue to export it. Record trade surpluses and surging steel and EV exports put margin pressure on manufacturers in ASEAN, India, Latin America, and Europe, increasing the risk of additional tariffs or anti-dumping measures.

Aagman's structural bias treats export-of-deflation and overcapacity as recurring themes in late-cycle capital structures, but the evidence in this piece is mixed: the demand shortfall is real, while the export surge also reflects genuine competitiveness in EVs and clean technology.

What this does not affect, at least not yet, is the immediate case for a systemic crisis. The state-dominated financial system, capital controls, and large foreign-exchange reserves give Beijing tools to prevent a sudden collapse. Stability and recovery are different things.

## Open Question

If Beijing continues to stimulate supply while households repair their balance sheets, can China avoid a Japan-style lost decade without ever experiencing a Japan-style financial crisis? Or will the political threshold eventually shift, and a much larger, durable transfer of purchasing power to households become the only way to close the demand gap?

The answer will not be found in the next GDP print. It will be found in whether the next round of fiscal policy sends money to households or to factories — and whether Chinese families begin to believe, finally, that the value of their homes has stopped falling.
