## Independent gaps

- **Gap:** UAE's exit from OPEC+ on 1 May 2026 is missing from all surfaces.
  - Why it matters: The 188 kb/d August increase was decided by only seven core producers (Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iraq, Kuwait, Kazakhstan, Algeria, Oman) because the UAE had left the alliance. Omitting this makes OPEC+ look more unified than it is and understates why the group's response appears cautious.
  - Suggested addition or correction: Add one line in the OPEC+ slide(s): "The decision came from the seven remaining core producers after the UAE left OPEC+ on 1 May."
  - Severity: should-fix

- **Gap:** Gold's 17 June price action is simplified to a peace-deal effect.
  - Why it matters: External sources report gold fell ~3% on 17 June because the Warsh Fed surprised hawkish at the same time the peace MOU was signed. Presenting the deal as purely supportive for gold obscures the real-rate/dollar cross-current that the research itself emphasizes as gold's dominant channel.
  - Suggested addition or correction: In Instagram Slide 6 (and any blog rewrite), note that gold's 17 June move was a tug-of-war: lower oil reduced hike urgency, but the hawkish Fed and stronger dollar pushed back.
  - Severity: should-fix

- **Gap:** Dollar dynamics are under-used.
  - Why it matters: The research's cross-asset section notes that a weaker dollar briefly supported gold while a sustained dollar recovery would weigh on bullion. This is a key transmission channel for the gold thesis and is absent from the carousels.
  - Suggested addition or correction: Add one line on the dollar-gold link, e.g., "Gold's relief was capped by a stronger dollar after the Fed turned hawkish."
  - Severity: optional

- **Gap:** Historical analogues (1973 embargo, 1990–91 Gulf War) are absent.
  - Why it matters: The research includes two analogues explicitly to warn that war premiums can deflate fast while second-round inflation effects linger. This is valuable context for a carousel audience and would strengthen any eventual blog.
  - Suggested addition or correction: Add a one-sentence analogue in the open-question slide or a future blog: "The 1990–91 Gulf War showed war premiums can collapse once the worst case is priced out, even when physical supply takes months to normalize."
  - Severity: optional

- **Gap:** Permanent demand destruction vs. deferred consumption is not sharply posed.
  - Why it matters: The research lists this as a key open question determining whether the IEA's 2027 surplus materializes. Instagram Slide 11 touches on it but could be more explicit.
  - Suggested addition or correction: Sharpen the final slide wording: "The open question is whether the 2026 demand drop is permanent destruction or deferred consumption that returns at lower prices."
  - Severity: optional
