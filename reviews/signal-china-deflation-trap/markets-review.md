## Independent gaps

- **Gap:** The outputs ignore H1 2026 data showing a material turnaround in price dynamics and property-market stabilization.
  - Why it matters: As of July 2026, headline CPI is +1.0% YoY, core CPI +1.0% YoY, PPI +4.1% YoY, and first-tier new-home prices have risen MoM for four consecutive months (20 of 70 cities reported MoM gains in June). A mid-2026 "deflation trap" piece that omits this looks stale or one-sided. The 2025 data remain valid as a retrospective, but the live macro call is now more contested.
  - Suggested addition or correction: Add an editor's note and a short section/visual annotation summarizing the H1 2026 inflection: CPI/PPI have rebounded, tier-1 home prices are stabilizing, and the key question is whether the recovery is durable or driven by base effects and policy front-loading.
  - Severity: blocker
  - Sources: NBS Chinese-language CPI release (2026-07-09) — [link](https://www.stats.gov.cn/sj/zxfb/202607/t20260709_1964084.html); NBS PPI release (2026-07-10) — [link](https://www.stats.gov.cn/english/PressRelease/202607/t20260710_1964093.html); IndexBox summary of NBS 70-city price data (2026-07-15) — [link](https://www.indexbox.io/blog/china-first-tier-home-prices-rise-for-fourth-consecutive-month-in-june-2026/)

- **Gap:** The blog and infographic underplay base-effect and commodity-price drivers of the 2026 reflation.
  - Why it matters: PPI turning positive (+4.1% YoY in June 2026) may partly reflect low base effects and commodity-price swings rather than a clean demand recovery. Without acknowledging this, the piece is vulnerable to a "missed the turn" critique.
  - Suggested addition or correction: If updating for 2026, note that the rebound partly reflects base effects and that demand-sensitive components remain moderate.
  - Severity: should-fix

- **Gap:** The trade-surplus / export-deflation angle omits the tariff front-running and competitiveness nuance.
  - Why it matters: The research artifact itself cautions that some export strength reflects genuine EV/clean-tech competitiveness and potential tariff front-running, not solely domestic-demand shortfall. Infographic Concept 5's title loses this nuance.
  - Suggested addition or correction: Qualify the export-deflation framing with "partly" and mention competitiveness/front-running as alternative drivers.
  - Severity: should-fix

- **Gap:** The analysis does not engage with the possibility that Beijing's 2026 pivot toward consumption may already be underway.
  - Why it matters: The IMF, IISS, and official Chinese media have flagged that the 15th Five-Year Plan and 2026 policy agenda prioritize household consumption. Ignoring this weakens the "deliberate supply-side choice" interpretation if the piece is presented as current analysis.
  - Suggested addition or correction: Add a line noting the 2026 consumption rhetoric and the risk that the supply-demand mismatch may narrow if household support scales up.
  - Severity: optional
  - Sources: IMF, "How China's Economy Can Pivot to Consumption-led Growth" (2026-02-18) — [link](https://www.imf.org/en/news/articles/2026/02/18/cf-how-chinas-economy-can-pivot-to-consumption-led-growth); IISS, "China's consumption problem" (2026-02-19) — [link](https://www.iiss.org/online-analysis/charting-china/2026/chinas-consumption-problem/)

- **Gap:** The infographic concepts omit the FX/rates and property-credit cross-asset angles that are central to the research artifact's markets framing.
  - Why it matters: The research explicitly covers Chinese equities, property credit, commodities, FX/rates, and EM spillovers. The recommended concepts focus on CPI/PPI, stimulus composition, housing wealth, and exports. A markets-facing audience would benefit from a concept on RMB/rates or property-credit spreads.
  - Suggested addition or correction: Consider adding a concept on "Stimulus without reflation: what it means for RMB and rates" or on property-credit vulnerability.
  - Severity: optional
