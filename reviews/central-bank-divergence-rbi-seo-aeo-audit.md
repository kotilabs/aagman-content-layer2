## Executive summary

The draft is structurally sound, intellectually honest, and factually well-supported. Its biggest search weakness is discoverability: the H1 and H2s do not explicitly target the query tension (“central-bank divergence” + “RBI”), and the mandatory analogy opening pushes the direct answer below the fold. The most important optimization is to add a brief “In short” structural bridge after the analogy and to sharpen the H1, URL slug, and at least two H2s/H3s for query capture.

## Recommended changes

1. **Location:** H1 (title)
   - **Issue:** The H1 omits the central search tension (“central-bank divergence”), so the piece may rank weakly for the signal keyword.
   - **Fix:** Change H1 to *“The RBI’s Capital-Account Optionality: Why India Used External-Sector Tools as Global Central Banks Diverged”*.
   - **Priority:** High

2. **Location:** Opening analogy → Context transition (paragraphs 1–8)
   - **Issue:** The first 100 words do not give a direct structural answer, which hurts GEO and AEO capture.
   - **Fix:** After the analogy/lead, add one bridge paragraph: *“In short: in mid-June 2026 the Fed, ECB and RBA diverged. The RBI held the repo rate at 5.25% and opened its capital-account toolbox—FAR, NRI/OCI limits, PSU ECB swaps, FCNR(B) hedging, export-proceeds relief—so it could manage external pressure without hiking.”*
   - **Priority:** Critical

3. **Location:** Opening section, after the bridge
   - **Issue:** No early definition pattern for AEO snippets.
   - **Fix:** Add a 40–60 word definition block: *“Central-bank divergence happens when major central banks move in conflicting directions because they face different inflation-growth mixes. India’s capital-account optionality is the choice to manage external pressure through regulatory and inflow tools while keeping the domestic policy rate unchanged.”*
   - **Priority:** High

4. **Location:** Mechanism section, five RBI measures
   - **Issue:** The five levers are embedded in prose; not easily extracted as a list/table snippet.
   - **Fix:** Convert the description into a numbered list or a two-column table (measure → purpose). Keep the same wording to preserve voice.
   - **Priority:** Medium

5. **Location:** Throughout the article
   - **Issue:** No internal links to other Aagman Layer 1 or Layer 2 pieces.
   - **Fix:** Add 3–5 descriptive internal links: “2013 FCNR(B) swap,” “Fully Accessible Route,” “neutral stance,” “Fed’s median dot,” and “Brent crude.” Use placeholder or actual Aagman slugs.
   - **Priority:** High

6. **Location:** Interpretation spectrum
   - **Issue:** Aagman’s structural bias toward India’s long-cycle story is implicit but not declared.
   - **Fix:** Insert one sentence flagging the bias as an undercurrent, not evidence, e.g. *“This reading is consistent with Aagman’s structural bias that India is in a long-term domestic-demand-driven bull market, but the bias does not prove the case.”*
   - **Priority:** Medium

7. **Location:** Historical / structural memory section
   - **Issue:** The 2013 comparison is strong in prose but could be snippet-ready.
   - **Fix:** Add a small 2013 vs. 2026 FCNR(B) comparison table: U.S. rates, spread over dollar alternatives, RBI swap terms, rupee level, reserves, current-account deficit. Trim a weaker paragraph if word count is tight.
   - **Priority:** Quick Win

## What to leave alone

- The everyday-analogy opening and calm, conversational Layer 2 voice.
- All verified numbers, dates, and source links (do not round or change them for SEO).
- The four-reading interpretation spectrum and the explicit separation of knowns/unknowns.
- The “what this affects / what this does not affect” implications framing.
- The open, non-advisory ending.
- The full source table at the bottom.
